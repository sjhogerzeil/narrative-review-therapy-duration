# Design

Structured narrative review using the methodology of **critical interpretive synthesis** (CIS; Dixon-Woods et al., 2006). Sources are drawn from six layers representing different epistemological perspectives on the same question. Each source is annotated using a structured template that extracts data relevant to the primary and secondary research questions, cross-cutting methodological observations, and verbatim quotes with full citations.

## Methodological framework

CIS was developed for questions that:
- Span heterogeneous evidence (empirical, theoretical, experiential)
- Cannot be answered through statistical synthesis
- Require interpretive argument rather than aggregation
- Benefit from iterative, question-driven search rather than exhaustive screening

This review follows CIS in using an iterative snowball search strategy, structured annotation, and argumentative synthesis. It extends the CIS approach by:
- Organizing evidence into explicit layers with distinct epistemological roles
- Using structured YAML frontmatter for efficient cross-source scanning
- Maintaining an append-only audit trail for replicability of the final analysis
- Steel-manning the strongest counterarguments in a dedicated layer

## Layers

| # | Layer | What it provides | Evidence type |
|---|-------|-----------------|---------------|
| 1 | Academic and clinical literature | Theory, clinical frameworks, treatment guidelines | Peer-reviewed articles, books, chapters |
| 2 | Naturalistic outcome studies | Empirical duration and outcome data | Longitudinal studies, meta-analyses |
| 3 | Counterpoint — accelerated models | Strongest challenges to the working thesis | Model-specific evidence, RCTs, clinical claims |
| 4 | Practitioner essays | Clinical wisdom on duration and trajectory | Blogs, essays, conference papers |
| 5 | Client narratives | Lived temporal experience of healing | Forum posts, first-person accounts |
| 6 | Memoirs and autobiographies | Complete healing arcs from retrospective vantage | Published memoirs |

## Rationale for multi-layer approach

No single evidence type can answer the question. RCTs impose artificial time limits. Naturalistic studies track outcomes but rarely ask about subjective trajectory. Practitioner wisdom captures duration but lacks systematic data. Client narratives capture the lived experience but suffer from survivorship bias. Memoirs offer the most complete arcs but are rare and unrepresentative. By triangulating across layers, patterns that no single layer could establish become visible.
