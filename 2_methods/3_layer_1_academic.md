# Layer 1: Academic and clinical literature

Peer-reviewed articles, clinical books, and theoretical works addressing duration and trajectory of healing from complex developmental trauma.

## 1a. Trauma-specific clinical literature

Focus: Long-term outcomes, case studies, treatment guidelines, and practitioner observations.

Search terms:
- complex PTSD long-term treatment duration
- developmental trauma therapy duration years
- long-term psychotherapy complex trauma outcomes
- dissociative disorder treatment duration
- structural dissociation long-term treatment
- ISSTD treatment guidelines complex trauma
- phase-based trauma treatment duration
- personality disorder long-term therapy outcomes
- complex trauma treatment length predictors

Sources: PubMed, PsycINFO, Journal of Trauma & Dissociation, European Journal of Psychotraumatology

Key authors: Judith Herman, Onno van der Hart, Kathy Steele, Bessel van der Kolk, Ellert Nijenhuis, Janina Fisher, Pat Ogden

## 1b. Psychoanalytic and psychodynamic tradition

Role: Theoretical background — the most developed account of *why* deep change takes years. Not primary evidence for duration, but essential for understanding the mechanisms that make long-term work necessary.

Search terms:
- self psychology treatment duration Kohut
- object relations long-term therapy developmental trauma
- Winnicott holding environment regression treatment duration
- relational psychoanalysis developmental trauma
- Bromberg multiple self states treatment
- intersubjectivity long-term therapy
- mentalization-based treatment personality disorder duration

Key authors: Heinz Kohut, D.W. Winnicott, Philip Bromberg, Robert Stolorow, Jessica Benjamin, Peter Fonagy (mentalization), Anthony Bateman

## 1c. Person-centred, humanistic, and existential traditions

Focus: A different theoretical lens on why healing takes time — not through structural models but through the conditions for growth (Rogers), the encounter with existential givens (Yalom, van Deurzen), and the slow restoration of organismic trust. Often underrepresented in trauma literature but directly relevant.

Search terms:
- person-centred therapy complex trauma long-term
- Rogers conditions of worth developmental trauma
- existential psychotherapy trauma duration
- emotion-focused therapy complex trauma outcomes
- humanistic therapy long-term outcomes
- experiential therapy developmental trauma

Key authors: Carl Rogers, Irvin Yalom, Emmy van Deurzen, Leslie Greenberg, Robert Elliott, Margaret Warner (person-centred work with "difficult" clients)

## 1d. Developmental neuroscience

Role: Theoretical models that explain or predict the duration of therapy — why deep change in this population is slow, or under what conditions it could be faster. This layer provides mechanistic reasoning, not direct evidence about treatment duration. It should be read as: "what does neuroscience say about the *expected* timescale of structural change, and what variables might shorten or lengthen it?"

Focus: Neurobiological accounts of structural change in early-traumatized brains — neural plasticity constraints, polyvagal reorganization, implicit memory systems, and conditions that may accelerate or impede reorganization.

Search terms:
- developmental neuroscience trauma recovery timeline
- neuroplasticity complex trauma treatment
- polyvagal theory trauma therapy duration
- implicit memory trauma restructuring
- Allan Schore regulation development trauma

Key authors: Allan Schore, Stephen Porges, Bruce Perry, Ruth Lanius
