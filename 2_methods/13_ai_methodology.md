# AI methodology

This review was conducted with substantial assistance from a large language model (LLM). This section describes the AI system used, the division of labor between the human author and the AI, and the quality control mechanisms employed. It follows the disclosure requirements of the ICMJE Recommendations (2026, Section V), the COPE position on authorship and AI tools, and the AMEE Guide No. 192 on AI disclosure in academic publishing.

## System

- **Model:** Claude Opus 4.6 (Anthropic), accessed via Claude Code (CLI interface)
- **Context window:** 1 million tokens
- **Access period:** March 2026
- **Orchestration:** Single human author directing AI subagents through an iterative, checkpoint-gated workflow. The AI operated as a research assistant executing tasks designed and supervised by the author, not as an autonomous agent making unsupervised interpretive decisions.

## What the AI cannot do

In accordance with ICMJE (2026) and COPE guidelines, the AI is not listed as an author because it cannot:

- Take responsibility for the accuracy, integrity, or originality of the work
- Approve the final version of the manuscript
- Be accountable for all aspects of the work
- Respond to peer review or post-publication queries
- Declare conflicts of interest or positionality

The human author bears sole responsibility for all claims, interpretations, and conclusions in this review.

## Quality control mechanisms

Five mechanisms were employed to control the quality of AI-generated output:

1. **Checkpoint-gated workflow.** The review proceeded through nine phases. At three critical junctures — after Phase 1 (search and annotation), after Phase 3 (synthesis), and after Phase 4 (discussion) — the human author reviewed the AI's output before authorising the next phase. The synthesis underwent the most intensive review: the 4-factor framework was approved only after the human author verified that the construct was warranted by cross-layer evidence.

2. **Structural counterpoint (Layer 3).** A dedicated evidence layer was designed to contain the strongest challenges to the working thesis. This structural safeguard ensures that the review's confirmation bias is checked by evidence the AI was required to seek, annotate, and engage with — not merely acknowledge.

3. **Execution log.** AI subagents appended meta-commentary to an execution log (`execution_log.md`) after each task, documenting issues, questions, emerging patterns, and suggestions. The human author reviewed this log to identify structural gaps and plan drift.

4. **Back-reference verification.** Every claim in the discussion sections includes source back-references (e.g., `[source-id]`). These are traceable to specific source annotations in `3_results/sources/`, which in turn link to the original sources. A reader can verify any claim by following the reference chain.

5. **Sync script.** An automated consistency checker (`sync.sh`) verifies that all cited sources exist, all source annotations are indexed, and the bibliography is complete. This provides mechanical verification of referential integrity.

## The audit trail

The complete audit trail for this review is preserved in the git repository:

- **Git history** captures every change, from initial project structure through final tables. Each commit message describes what was done and by whom.
- **Git tags** mark key milestones: `v0.1-setup` through `v0.8-tables-figures`.
- **Source annotations** (`3_results/sources/`) contain the structured evidence base — one file per source with YAML metadata and extracted data.
- **Extraction tables** (`3_results/primary/` and `3_results/secondary/`) show how source-level data was aggregated into synthesis dimensions.
- **Process notes** (`.claude/PROCESS-NOTES.md`) document session-level reasoning, non-obvious decisions, and open questions — written for a future collaborator who needs to understand why things were done a particular way.
- **Execution log** (`execution_log.md`) captures AI subagent meta-commentary.

This level of transparency is unusual for a narrative review. It is made possible by the AI-assisted workflow: every step that would normally occur in a researcher's private notes and reading process is captured in structured, version-controlled files.

## Epistemological implications

The use of AI in an interpretive review raises questions that should be stated openly.

**What is gained.** The AI enabled a scope of coverage (89 sources across six epistemological layers, 16 extraction dimensions, 9 discussion sections) that would be difficult for a solo human reviewer to achieve in the same timeframe. The structured annotation template and extraction tables impose a consistency of treatment across sources that reduces the variability inherent in human annotation. The counterpoint layer and checkpoint-gated workflow provide structural safeguards against confirmation bias that are at least as rigorous as those in a conventional single-author review.

**What is at risk.** The AI's interpretive choices are shaped by its training data, which over-represents published academic literature and under-represents clinical experience, oral tradition, and marginalised perspectives. The AI may reproduce the same institutional biases that the CIS methodology is designed to interrogate. The human author's role as the sole interpretive authority — reviewing, approving, and directing the AI's output — is the primary safeguard against this risk, but it is imperfect: the author cannot fully evaluate interpretive choices they did not generate.

**What is different.** In a conventional review, the author reads every source, thinks about every connection, and writes every sentence. The interpretive process is internal and largely invisible. In this review, the interpretive process is external and documented: the AI generates interpretations, the human author evaluates them, and the git history records the evolution. This is not straightforwardly better or worse than conventional review — it is different, and readers should assess the review's claims with this difference in mind.
