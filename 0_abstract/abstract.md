# Abstract

The question "how long does healing from complex developmental trauma take?" appears to divide the clinical field. Brief, trauma-focused protocols report symptom remission in weeks; psychodynamic and phase-oriented traditions describe structural change unfolding over years; client narratives and memoirs recount trajectories spanning decades. This structured narrative review uses critical interpretive synthesis (CIS) to examine 89 sources across six epistemological layers — academic literature, naturalistic outcome studies, counterpoint evidence for brief treatment, practitioner essays, client narratives from online forums, and published memoirs — and finds that the apparent disagreement is an artefact of unstated endpoint definitions. The review proposes a four-factor framework: a safety prerequisite (the therapeutic clock starts at felt safety, not at the first session), an endpoint level (symptom reduction in weeks, functional improvement in months, structural personality change in years), the structural depth of the original injury (primary through tertiary dissociation), and the independent contribution of elapsed time through the sleeper effect (therapy installs capacity; life develops it). Nine independent theoretical traditions — from self psychology through polyvagal theory — converge on the claim that structural change requires repeated relational cycles over developmental time and cannot be compressed by technique. The strongest counterpoint evidence (EMDR intensive formats, pharmacologically enhanced protocols) measures symptom-level endpoints and does not contradict the long-duration thesis at the structural level. The review is limited by survivorship bias (all layers select for people who stayed in therapy), the dose-versus-elapsed-time confound (no study has directly compared the same dose at different frequencies), and the absence of long-term follow-up beyond ten years in defined complex trauma populations. The clinical implication is that honest expectation-setting requires naming the endpoint: symptom relief is achievable in months; structural healing, for those who need it, takes years — and the recommendation to pursue it carries an obligation to make it accessible.
