# Dose versus elapsed time

## The confound

"Five years of therapy" could mean 250 sessions (weekly), 130 sessions (biweekly), or 500 sessions (twice-weekly). Most sources in this review report calendar duration without specifying session frequency, total session count, or both. This conflation makes it impossible, on the face of it, to determine whether the thesis is really about therapeutic dose (the total number of sessions needed), elapsed life-time (the calendar years needed for maturation, life experience, and neural reorganisation), or both.

The confound is not academic. If the argument is primarily about dose, then intensive formats become a much stronger counterargument: deliver the same number of sessions in two years instead of ten. If the argument is about elapsed time, then session frequency may matter less than continuity of the therapeutic relationship over years. If both are independently necessary, that is the strongest version of the long-duration thesis — but also the hardest to demonstrate.

The extraction data confirm the severity of the confound. Of the approximately 40 annotated sources, only 11 report both session count and calendar duration [lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr; zimmermann-2014-munich-dose; murphy-2009-pct-abuse; bateman-fonagy-2008-mbt-8yr; koeser-2023-tads-cost; griffiths-2025-phase-oriented-review; sciarrino-2020-intensive-review; dejongh-2024-emdr-science; dejongh-2023-emdr-case; feder-2025-ketamine-exposure; mitchell-2021-mdma-phase3]. The dose-effect literature (Howard et al., Lambert via Shedler) measures exclusively in sessions. The clinical and memoir literature reports exclusively in years. These are different evidence bases answering different questions, and most of the available data cannot bridge the gap.

## Resolution: elapsed time as an independent factor

Despite the confound, the review's cross-layer triangulation produces a resolution. The synthesis identifies elapsed time as Factor 3 — a contributor to healing that operates independently of therapeutic dose. The evidence for this claim rests on four converging lines.

**The Helsinki convergence.** The Helsinki Psychotherapy Study provides the single strongest piece of evidence. Psychoanalysis (PA: 646 sessions at 4 times per week over 56 months) and long-term psychodynamic psychotherapy (LPP: 232 sessions at 2–3 times per week over 31 months) produced indistinguishable outcomes at 10-year follow-up, despite a nearly 3:1 difference in total dose [lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr]. If dose were the primary determinant, psychoanalysis should have produced proportionally better outcomes. It did not. Both treatments may have exceeded what the synthesis terms the "transmuting internalisation threshold" — the point at which enough psychic structure has been built to be self-sustaining — after which additional sessions produce diminishing returns. What mattered was not how many sessions were delivered but how many years elapsed for the installed capacity to develop through subsequent life experience.

The Munich Psychotherapy Study reinforces this finding from a different angle. Zimmermann et al. (2014) found that post-treatment interpersonal gains were mediated by dose (more sessions produced more interpersonal change), but follow-up depression reduction was mediated by psychoanalytic technique, not dose [zimmermann-2014-munich-dose]. This dissociation is precisely what the elapsed-time hypothesis predicts: dose determines the immediate output of therapy, but what grows after therapy depends on the type of structure installed, not the quantity of installation.

**The sleeper effect.** The most direct evidence for elapsed time as an independent factor comes from therapeutic gains that emerge or grow after treatment has ended. Shedler's (2010) meta-analytic review found effect sizes for psychodynamic therapy increasing from 0.97 at end of treatment to 1.51 at follow-up beyond 9 months — a pattern unique to psychodynamic approaches [shedler-2010-efficacy]. The TADS trial found LTPP non-significant versus treatment as usual at end of treatment but strongly significant (p = 0.001) at 42-month follow-up [fonagy-2015-tads]. MBT gains continued growing for five years after all treatment ended, while the TAU group deteriorated despite receiving more services [bateman-fonagy-2008-mbt-8yr]. The EDT meta-analysis found experiential dynamic therapies non-significant versus active controls at post-treatment but significant at follow-up [lilliengren-2025-edt-meta]. Psychoanalytic therapy (median 242 sessions) outperformed CBT (median 59 sessions) specifically at 5-year follow-up for chronic depression with childhood trauma history — the advantage emerged after treatment, not during it [mia-psychoanalytic-beats-cbt].

If therapeutic gains grow after treatment ends, then elapsed time is doing work that sessions cannot. The therapy installs capacity; life develops it.

**The borrowed-versus-owned mechanism.** Kohut's (1984) distinction between borrowed and owned psychic structure provides the theoretical mechanism [kohut-1984-how-analysis-cures]. Borrowed structure is the patient functioning better because the therapist's regulatory capacity is available as an external selfobject resource. Owned structure is the product of thousands of cycles of attunement, optimal frustration, and repair — genuinely internal regulatory capacities that continue to develop autonomously after treatment ends. The distinction predicts both the sleeper effect in long-term treatment (owned structure continues developing) and the reverse sleeper effect in short-term treatment (borrowed structure erodes when the scaffold is removed). Abbass's (2014) Cochrane review found precisely this reverse pattern for short-term psychodynamic therapy: gains at post-treatment that lost significance at long-term follow-up [abbass-2014-stpp-cochrane]. The prediction is confirmed in both directions.

**Between-session integration.** Spring's memoir provides the lived-experience confirmation at the micro-level. Her claim — "the gaps between therapy sessions can be as developmentally important as the therapy sessions themselves" [spring-2020-recovering-developmental-trauma] — describes the mechanism by which elapsed time operates within treatment, not only after it. Each session installs a micro-increment of neural capacity, which consolidates between sessions through the patient's real-world relational and emotional experience. The therapy-vs-life distinction, from this perspective, is false: therapy provides the scaffolding; life provides the developmental experience within which the scaffolding becomes structure.

Heacock's case provides a real-time observation of this process. During Erin's extended postpartum leave, Sherlock demonstrated self-regulation capacity he had previously relied on her to provide — "I'm going to be okay. You have given me the tools I need" [heacock-2022-psychotherapy-twice-told]. The absence of the therapist revealed that structure had been installed that could function independently. Spring's post-therapy stable period (2017–2019) shows the same phenomenon on a larger scale: gains from therapy ending in 2016 continued developing through three subsequent years of generative living [spring-2021-falling-down-getting-back-up]. The Redditor who described the first five years of an 11-year healing arc as "behavioural/external changes" followed by emotional healing captures the same temporal structure: the earlier period set conditions for later transformation that occurred largely outside therapy sessions [reddit-2024-what-feels-like-after].

## Do intensive formats compress the timeline or just the dose?

This question is the critical test of the elapsed-time hypothesis. If intensive delivery produces the same structural outcomes in compressed calendar time, then elapsed time is not independently necessary — dose is all that matters. If intensive delivery produces symptom reduction but not structural change, the elapsed-time hypothesis is supported.

The available evidence suggests the latter, though with important caveats. The intensive format literature — EMDR intensive producing 88% CPTSD remission in 8 days [dejongh-2024-emdr-science], Sciarrino's review showing d = 1.57 in 1–3 weeks [sciarrino-2020-intensive-review], ketamine-enhanced exposure therapy achieving d = 1.9 in three weeks [feder-2025-ketamine-exposure], MDMA-assisted psychotherapy eliminating PTSD diagnosis in 67% after 18 weeks [mitchell-2021-mdma-phase3] — consistently measures symptom-level endpoints (diagnostic remission, questionnaire scores below clinical threshold). These are Level 1 outcomes in the synthesis framework. No intensive format study in the review measures personality organisation, mentalizing capacity, attachment security, or other structural endpoints.

This absence is not proof of absence. It may be that intensive formats do produce structural change that no one has measured. But the evidence pattern is suggestive: the studies that measure structural change (Helsinki, Munich, MBT, TADS) are all long-duration studies that track patients over years; the studies that find rapid effects are all measuring symptoms over weeks. The two literatures are answering different questions.

One finding complicates the picture in an instructive way. The Reddit user who processed 36 traumatic memories in a one-week EMDR intensive described the intervention as decisive — but only after years of prior self-directed work [reddit-2025-full-cure-how-long]. The intensive did not replace elapsed time; it punctuated it. Similarly, Razvi's personal trajectory included 4–5+ years of conventional therapy (2.5 years somatic, 1.5–2 years attachment-focused) before MDMA sessions catalysed a qualitative shift [heacock-2019-healing-psychedelics]. The psychedelic session compressed a specific processing step; it did not compress the developmental arc within which that step was embedded. The intensive format may function not as a replacement for elapsed time but as an accelerant within it — a high-dose punctuation that requires both pre-existing structure (built over time) and subsequent integration (also requiring time) to produce durable structural change.

Shedler's dose-response data further contextualise the issue. Drawing on Lambert's data, he reports that 50% of patients show measurable improvement by 21 sessions and 75% by 40+ sessions [shedler-2020-tyranny-of-time]. Perry's more specific data for personality disorders, cited in Leichsenring (2013), require 92 sessions (1.3 years) for 50% recovery and 216 sessions (2.2 years) for 75% recovery [leichsenring-2013-emerging-evidence]. These figures concern symptom improvement and personality disorder recovery, respectively — different endpoints requiring different doses. But even the personality disorder dose curve measures from first session and therefore conflates pre-safety and post-safety sessions, as the synthesis argues. If the first 50 sessions of a 200-session treatment establish safety rather than producing structural change, then the "true dose" for structural change is closer to 150 sessions — a figure that might, in principle, be deliverable in compressed calendar time. The question is whether the between-session integration that Spring describes [spring-2020-recovering-developmental-trauma] is a rate-limiting step that cannot be compressed, or whether it can be achieved through intensive immersion.

No study in the literature directly answers this question. No trial has compared the same total dose delivered at different frequencies over different calendar durations for a complex developmental trauma population. The confound cannot be fully resolved with available evidence.

## The silence on frequency

The extraction data reveal a telling pattern of silence. Of the 11 newly annotated sources, not one provides explicit session frequency data. Most practitioner, memoir, and counterpoint sources treat session frequency as unimportant or assumed (weekly as the default), while the most granular frequency data continue to come from naturalistic studies (Layer 2) and, unexpectedly, from Reddit threads (Layer 5). The Reddit poster who reported 900+ appointments over 10 years of twice-weekly therapy [reddit-2024-10-years-900-appointments] provides more precise dose-duration data than most academic sources. The myptsd-2020-session-frequency thread reveals that frequency decisions in practice are shaped by attachment dynamics (reducing frequency triggers attachment anxiety) and access constraints (pro bono therapy means monthly sessions, described as "pure hell"), not by clinical protocol [myptsd-2020-session-frequency].

This silence is analytically consequential. If the field does not routinely record session frequency, it cannot distinguish between dose and duration, and claims about "how long therapy takes" remain systematically ambiguous.

A deeper finding emerges from the client data: session frequency is entangled with attachment dynamics in ways that clinical protocols do not acknowledge. The myptsd-2020-session-frequency thread reveals that reducing frequency triggers attachment anxiety in patients whose primary injury is relational — the very population for whom frequency decisions are most clinically consequential [myptsd-2020-session-frequency]. A patient who experiences the therapist as a primary attachment figure may interpret a shift from twice-weekly to weekly not as a clinical decision but as relational withdrawal. Conversely, the therapist who increases frequency to manage a crisis may inadvertently deepen the dependency that Steele (2001) identifies as the mechanism of cure [steele-2001-dependency] — which is therapeutically appropriate but makes later frequency reduction more difficult. The implication is that frequency is not a neutral dosing parameter to be optimised for efficiency. It is a relational variable that carries attachment meaning and must be managed as such. Studies that manipulate frequency as an independent variable are confounding dose with relational experience.

## Implications for the thesis

The evidence supports a resolution of the dose-versus-elapsed-time confound that strengthens the long-duration thesis in an unexpected way. The thesis "healing takes years" is not a dose claim that happens to be stated in calendar time. It is a claim about the nature of structural change: that the process of converting borrowed structure into owned structure requires real-world developmental experience that unfolds over time and cannot be compressed by increasing the session count. The Helsinki convergence [lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr] demonstrates this: tripling the dose did not halve the time, because time is not merely the container for sessions — it is itself part of the medicine.

This framing generates a specific, testable prediction. If elapsed time contributes independently of dose, then two conditions should hold: first, beyond a sufficient dose threshold, additional sessions should produce diminishing returns (confirmed by the Helsinki convergence); second, the same total dose delivered over a longer calendar period should produce superior structural outcomes compared to the same dose delivered in compressed time (not yet tested). The absence of this critical comparison is the most important empirical gap identified in the review.

The practical implication is that the argument for long-term therapy is not primarily an argument about access — about accumulating enough sessions — but about development. Therapy at the structural level works like a developmental intervention: it installs capacity for mentalising, self-regulation, and relational engagement that continues to develop through the patient's subsequent life experience. Shedler (2010) calls this a "catalytic" effect [shedler-2010-efficacy]. The therapy does not do the healing; it creates the conditions under which healing can proceed autonomously. This requires time — not because more sessions are needed, but because the developmental process that therapy initiates cannot be rushed.
