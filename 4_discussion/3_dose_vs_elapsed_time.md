# Dose vs. elapsed time

## The problem

"Five years of therapy" could mean 250 sessions (weekly), 130 sessions (biweekly), or 500 sessions (twice-weekly). Most sources report calendar duration without specifying session frequency, total session count, or both. This conflation makes it impossible to determine whether the thesis is really about:

- **Therapeutic dose** — the total amount of therapy needed (number of sessions, total hours)
- **Elapsed life-time** — the calendar years needed for maturation, life experience, and neural reorganization, with therapy as a scaffold
- **Both** — a minimum dose delivered over a minimum elapsed time, because both are independently necessary

This is not an academic distinction. If the argument is primarily about dose, then intensive formats (Layer 3) become a much stronger counterargument: deliver the same number of sessions in 2 years instead of 10. If the argument is about elapsed time, then session frequency may matter less than continuity of the therapeutic relationship over years. If both are independently necessary, that is the strongest version of the long-duration thesis — but also the hardest to demonstrate.

The dose-effect literature (Howard et al., Lambert) measures sessions, not years. The clinical literature on this population typically reports years. These are different evidence bases answering different questions, and conflating them weakens both.

## How we mitigated it

1. **Cross-cutting observation in every source note.** Each annotation asks: "Does this source distinguish between number of sessions, session frequency, and calendar years? Does it measure dose, duration, or both?"
2. **Secondary question 4 (session frequency)** extracts frequency data explicitly, creating a dedicated extraction file (`3_results/secondary/4_frequency.md`) that captures session counts, frequencies, and calendar durations separately where available.
3. **Layer 2 (naturalistic studies)** includes dose-effect research (Howard, Lambert) that measures in sessions, providing the empirical basis for disentangling dose from time.
4. **Layer 3 (counterpoint)** includes intensive formats that test the dose hypothesis directly — same or higher total sessions in compressed calendar time.

## What remains unmitigated

- Most sources in Layers 4, 5, and 6 (practitioner essays, client narratives, memoirs) report calendar years without session counts or frequencies. This data is probably unrecoverable.
- The dose-effect literature was largely developed for non-complex populations. Its applicability to the population in this review is an extrapolation, not a demonstrated finding.
- No study in the literature directly compares the same total dose delivered at different frequencies over different calendar durations for this specific population. The confound cannot be fully resolved with available evidence.
- Our own extraction may reproduce the conflation: if most sources report "years" and we aggregate across sources, we will report "years" — without being able to say whether a 10-year journey with weekly sessions and a 3-year journey with three-times-weekly sessions represent the same underlying process.

## Feed-back into the plan

*To be completed during/after data extraction. Questions to answer:*

- How many sources report both session count and calendar duration? Is the overlap enough to construct a meaningful dose-duration comparison?
- Does the dose-effect literature (Layer 2) provide dose estimates for complex populations? What is the estimated number of sessions needed?
- Do intensive format outcomes (Layer 3) suggest that compressed delivery achieves comparable structural change?
- Should the synthesis explicitly distinguish between claims about dose and claims about elapsed time, presenting them as related but separable arguments?
- Consider adding a secondary question: "Is there a minimum calendar duration below which healing cannot occur regardless of dose?" — this would sharpen the thesis.

## Implications for the thesis

The thesis "healing takes years" may need to be decomposed into two sub-claims:
1. *Healing requires a high total dose of therapy* (many sessions) — which is about access and resources
2. *Healing requires years of elapsed time* (maturation, life experience, the slow work of neural reorganization that cannot be compressed) — which is about the nature of the process itself

If only (1) is true, the argument is about access. If (2) is true, the argument is about something deeper. If both are true, they interact in ways that the review should try to articulate.
