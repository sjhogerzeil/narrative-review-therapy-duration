# Synthesis

## Thesis statement

The question "how long does healing from complex developmental trauma take?" cannot be answered as asked. The answer depends on what is meant by "healing" — and the field's apparent disagreement about duration dissolves once this is made explicit. Symptom reduction (fewer flashbacks, lower distress scores) takes weeks to months. Functional improvement (holding relationships, maintaining employment, reduced crises) takes months to years. Structural change (personality reorganisation, mentalizing capacity, integration of dissociated parts, earned attachment security) takes years, sometimes decades. Growth and reconnection (post-traumatic growth, identity transformation, the capacity to love and be loved) may have no definable endpoint.

These are not competing claims. They are nested levels of the same process. The review's working thesis — that healing takes years — is precisely true at the structural level and precisely false at the symptom level. Both "it takes 8 sessions" and "it takes 8 years" can be correct simultaneously, because they measure different things.

But even this formulation requires a critical qualification: **the therapeutic clock does not start at the first session**. It starts when safety — environmental, relational, and autonomic — is sufficiently established for the developmental process to begin. Pre-safety therapy time, which may span months or years, inflates all duration estimates in the literature. The ElishaAlison case is paradigmatic: the same therapist, the same modality, produced nothing during active abuse and produced full healing in 3.5 years once safety was achieved [reddit-2023-fully-healed-how-long]. The variable was not the therapy. It was the safety. Fisher's clinical principle — "slower is faster" [fisher-transforming-legacy] — captures this from the clinician's side: rushing past safety establishment into trauma processing overwhelms the nervous system and produces the very treatment failures that are then cited as evidence that therapy does not work.

This synthesis proposes a framework — endpoint level, structural depth, elapsed time, and the safety prerequisite — that resolves the apparent contradiction and generates specific, testable predictions about when brief treatment is sufficient and when it is not.

## The endpoint-duration nexus

The most striking finding across 89 sources in six epistemological layers is that **reported duration is almost entirely determined by the definition of the endpoint**. This is not a minor methodological caveat — it is the central finding of the review.

Five endpoint levels emerge from the data, each with a characteristic timeline and evidence base:

**Level 1: Symptom reduction.** Loss of diagnosis, score below clinical threshold on standardised instruments (CAPS-5, PCL-5, BDI, ITQ, CORE-OM). Evidence: EMDR intensive produces 88% CPTSD remission in 8 days [dejongh-2024-emdr-science]; ketamine-enhanced exposure therapy achieves d=1.9 in three weeks [feder-2025-ketamine-exposure]; MDMA-assisted psychotherapy eliminates PTSD diagnosis in 67% after 18 weeks [mitchell-2021-mdma-phase3]; intensive delivery formats achieve d=1.57 in 1–3 weeks with only 5.51% dropout [sciarrino-2020-intensive-review]. Timeline: **weeks to months**.

**Level 2: Functional improvement.** Holding employment, maintaining relationships, reduced crisis episodes, improved social functioning. Evidence: GPM milestone framework describes measurable functional improvements at 6 weeks (reduced distress), 6 months (reduced self-harm), 12 months (improved relationships), and 18 months (social functioning) [links-2015-gpm-bpd]. Even in treatment-resistant populations, functional differences between LTPP and TAU emerged at 42-month follow-up [fonagy-2015-tads]. At 8-year follow-up, MBT patients showed progressive vocational improvement, while treatment-as-usual patients deteriorated [bateman-fonagy-2008-mbt-8yr]. Timeline: **months to 1–2 years**.

**Level 3: Structural change.** Personality reorganisation, mentalizing capacity, integration of dissociated parts, earned attachment security, capacity for mutual recognition, cohesive self-structure. Evidence: Perry's dose-response data shows 50% personality disorder recovery at 92 sessions (1.3 years), 75% at 216 sessions (2.2 years) [leichsenring-2013-emerging-evidence]. The Helsinki Psychotherapy Study found personality organisation, defence style, and self-concept continuing to change at 10-year follow-up [lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr]. Phase-oriented treatment for DID is projected at 3–5 years, with most participants still symptomatic at study endpoints [griffiths-2025-phase-oriented-review]. Single-case evidence: 160 sessions over 5 years for severe childhood abuse, with growth achieved but symptoms not eliminated [murphy-2009-pct-abuse]. Bisson frames this level diagnostically: C-PTSD adds disturbances in self-organisation (emotional regulation, negative self-concept, disturbed relationships) beyond core PTSD symptoms, and "the recovery rates from complex post-traumatic stress disorder are not likely to be as high as they are from post-traumatic stress disorder" [bisson-2024-recognising-cptsd]. A patient can meet recovery criteria for PTSD while remaining profoundly impaired in the C-PTSD domains — the structural endpoint is categorically different from the symptom endpoint. Timeline: **years**.

**Level 4: Growth and reconnection.** Post-traumatic growth, identity transformation, the capacity for intimacy and agency, "tolerable selfhood." Evidence: memoir accounts describe multi-decade trajectories — 10+ years [foo-2022-what-my-bones-know], 40+ years [walker-2013-complex-ptsd; levine-2024-autobiography-of-trauma], healing still ongoing at 90+ [eger-2017-the-choice]. Practitioner accounts describe 7+ years for post-traumatic growth [psychnetworker-transcending-trauma]. Client narratives describe identity-level transformation after 5–11+ years [reddit-2024-recovered-cptsd; reddit-2024-what-feels-like-after]. Joseph's (2012) model describes post-traumatic growth as continuing "indefinitely" until the assumptive world fully accommodates the trauma. Timeline: **years to decades, potentially lifelong**.

**Level 5: Recovery as direction, not destination.** Spring's "heading north" metaphor represents the most radical endpoint redefinition in the dataset [spring-2020-what-does-recovery-look-like]. Recovery is not a location (Scotland) but an orientation (north). At any given moment, a person may be crawling, stopped, or even temporarily heading south — but as long as the overall direction is northward, they are recovering. If the endpoint is directional rather than locational, then the question "how long does it take?" becomes structurally unanswerable in its usual form. You cannot ask "how long until you arrive?" if there is no arrival point. Spring's 15-year arc — from "regularly trying to kill myself, having full-blown dissociative identity disorder" to "living a life that is undeniably good" — is not the time to reach an endpoint but the elapsed time of a northward journey that continues. She defines functional markers (living mostly in the "green zone," remembering trauma without reliving it, experiencing joy alongside grief, choosing from the "front brain") but explicitly frames these as descriptions of a direction, not a destination. Timeline: **ongoing, no completion point**.

These levels are nested, not parallel. Symptom reduction is necessary but insufficient for functional improvement, which is necessary but insufficient for structural change, which is necessary but insufficient for growth. Each level subsumes the previous ones. A patient whose CAPS-5 score drops below the clinical threshold has achieved symptom reduction but may still lack the relational capacity, self-coherence, or mentalizing ability that structural change would confer. Conversely, a patient who has achieved structural change will necessarily also show symptom reduction and functional improvement — these are not separate achievements but earlier stations on the same arc.

A further complication concerns the grain size of the endpoint. Fosha's AEDP introduces the distinction between micro-endpoints and macro-endpoints [fosha-2019-aedp-intimacy]. Within a single AEDP session, a genuine transformation can be witnessed, meta-processed, and consolidated — the client experiences a qualitative shift. But is this a state change that may or may not accumulate into structural change, or is it a permanent transformation? Fosha's documented 6-session complete treatment suggests that for some clients, a small number of micro-endpoints can constitute a sufficient treatment. But AEDP does not claim this generalises to complex developmental trauma with deep structural disruption. The complication for the endpoint-duration nexus is that the scale at which the endpoint is defined matters: session-level transformation (AEDP) is rapid; personality-level reorganisation (Kohut, Lindfors) takes years. Both observations may be simultaneously true about the same process viewed at different magnifications.

The implication is stark: **the counterpoint literature (Layer 3) and the supporting literature (Layers 1, 4, 6) are not disagreeing about the same population receiving the same treatment. They are measuring different levels of change.** The EMDR literature measures diagnostic remission; the psychoanalytic literature measures personality organisation; the memoirs describe identity transformation. All are correct about their own endpoint. The field's "disagreement" about duration is an artefact of unstated endpoint definitions.

## The factors that determine duration

The endpoint level is the strongest single predictor of reported duration, but additional factors modulate the timeline within each level.

### Factor 0: The safety prerequisite

The evidence for safety as clock-starter is among the strongest emergent findings of this review, supported by 35+ entries across all six layers. The construct is straightforward: effective therapy duration begins at the establishment of safety — environmental, relational, and autonomic — not at the first appointment. All pre-safety therapy time is inflated duration data.

The direct evidence is compelling. ElishaAlison saw the same therapist during years of active abuse and "got nothing out of it"; the same therapist, post-safety, produced full healing in 3.5 years [reddit-2023-fully-healed-how-long]. Eger (2017) describes 35 years before processing could begin. Davitt spent decades in repression before breakdown, then found that environmental prerequisites (financial resources, supportive employer, time off work) were necessary before sustained therapy was possible [bisson-2024-recognising-cptsd]. Sherlock spent 3 years in therapy before he stopped hiding behind anger and began disclosing authentically — Erin's approach was "slow and deliberate... like you would with a wounded animal" [heacock-2022-psychotherapy-twice-told]. Shedler's combat veteran Ryan illustrates the same pattern from the practitioner side: "the real story only comes out after about a year of weekly therapy sessions, because it takes that long to establish the sort of trust traumatized vets need to start opening up" [shedler-2020-tyranny-of-time].

The theoretical support is equally dense. Herman's Phase 1 (safety) takes "months to years" for chronic trauma [herman-1992-trauma-recovery]. Porges' neuroception of safety must activate the social engagement system before the therapeutic relationship can function [porges-2022-polyvagal]. Steele identifies the circular structure: the phobia of attachment means that treatment cannot begin until the patient tolerates the relationship, but tolerating the relationship IS the treatment [steele-2005-phase-oriented]. Grossmark describes "companioning" — quiet relational presence with deeply dissociated patients — as a stance that may need to be sustained for months or years before the patient becomes available for processing [grossmark-psychoanalytic-companioning]. Fisher's TIST model explicitly avoids EMDR with highly functioning complex trauma clients due to overwhelm risk: "traumatic activation shuts down the prefrontal cortex" [fisher-cutting-edge-tist].

The silence pattern is analytically productive. The psychoanalytic theorists (Kohut, Winnicott, Benjamin, Bromberg, Fonagy) describe mechanisms that presuppose safety without naming it. Winnicott's "holding environment" IS safety; Kohut's "selfobject transference" IS felt safety; Benjamin's "surviving destruction" IS demonstrating that the relationship is safe enough to contain aggression. The psychoanalytic tradition's silence on safety is terminological, not substantive — it describes what happens once safety exists, using its own vocabulary, but does not identify safety establishment as a distinct clock-starting event.

Equally telling: ALL dose-effect studies measure from first session, not from safety establishment. This means every reported effect size in the literature conflates pre-safety and post-safety sessions into a single dose metric. If the dose-effect curve's early flat period represents not "therapy not working yet" but "safety not established yet," then the curve's inflection point is the moment safety is achieved — and this inflection would occur at different session numbers for different patients, explaining the enormous individual variation in dose-response data.

The counter-evidence should be acknowledged. De Jongh's (2024) van Vliet study found no difference between EMDR alone and EMDR plus STAIR stabilisation [dejongh-2024-emdr-science]. But this argues about stabilisation exercises, not about felt safety. The intensive format (8 days residential, multiple daily sessions, structured environment) may itself provide safety through containment — removing the patient from stressors, immersing them in a therapeutic environment, and holding them in a treatment team. Sciarrino's (2020) finding that intensive formats produce dramatically lower dropout (5.51% versus 18–39%) is consistent with this interpretation: the format achieves safety through structure rather than through extended time [sciarrino-2020-intensive-review]. Farina's observation that rapid responders may have "different underlying structures, with more capacities for adaptive information processing" [farina-2025-rapid-rare] maps directly onto the safety construct: patients who arrive with environmental stability, some secure relational history, and adequate adaptive capacity have already partially cleared the safety prerequisite. Their clock started earlier — in life, not in therapy.

Four implications follow. First, duration estimates from naturalistic data are systematically inflated, because they include pre-safety treatment time. Second, the "right therapist" confound is partly a safety confound: finding the right therapist may matter because the right therapist creates felt safety. Third, safety is not only relational — it is environmental: therapy cannot produce structural change while the environment remains actively traumatising [reddit-2021-cant-heal-while-surviving]. Fourth, the phase model is confirmed but the first phase is underestimated: Herman's Phase 1 is universally endorsed, but its actual duration — potentially years for severe developmental trauma — is the single most understudied variable in the treatment literature.

### Factor 1: Endpoint level

As described above. The higher the endpoint level, the longer the timeline. This relationship is not linear — it is roughly exponential. Symptom reduction takes weeks; structural change takes years; the ratio is not 4:1 but closer to 50:1 or 100:1 in session count.

### Factor 2: Structural depth of the injury

Not all complex developmental trauma produces the same degree of structural disruption. This is Farina's (2025) key insight: patients with "different underlying structures" may carry the same CPTSD diagnosis but differ fundamentally in the depth of developmental damage. The structural dissociation model [vanderhart-2006-haunted-self] provides the most precise vocabulary: primary structural dissociation (one emotional part) is less entrenched than secondary (multiple emotional parts) or tertiary (multiple apparently normal parts — DID). Each level of dissociative complexity multiplies the required therapeutic work because each dissociative part must develop its own relationship to the therapist, resolve its own phobias, and integrate into the personality system.

Razvi's three-tier autonomic framework adds a complementary gradient [heacock-2019-healing-psychedelics]. The tiers — mild stress (anxiety, hypervigilance), high stress/panic (fight-flight), and dissociative shutdown (parasympathetic collapse with endogenous opioid release) — map onto increasing treatment resistance. Crucially, dissociative shutdown produces outwardly calm, cognitively functional people who are internally numb: "our cognitive capacity is online and actually more unencumbered when people are in this final state of trauma... they can talk fine, they can do math fine, they just can't feel fine, they can't relate to anybody." Any therapy that relies on self-report cannot reach this level. Conventional somatic therapy takes 6–12 months per core dissociative issue; MDMA may compress this, but even Razvi's personal pre-psychedelic conventional trajectory was 4–5+ years [heacock-2019-healing-psychedelics].

The empirical evidence supports this gradient. Brand et al. (2019) found that higher-baseline dissociation predicted both greater AND faster improvement — but this apparent paradox reflects the fact that more severely dissociative patients have more room to improve on symptom measures, not that they achieve structural integration faster [brand-2019-topdd]. Griffiths (2025) found that affect and impulse management improved faster (d=1.61) than dissociation itself (d=0.63) — symptoms respond before structure [griffiths-2025-phase-oriented-review]. The distinction matters: a patient with primary structural dissociation and adequate pre-existing adaptive information processing capacity may genuinely achieve structural change in months. A patient with tertiary structural dissociation and pervasive developmental deficits may require years for the same endpoint level.

This factor explains a puzzle in the data: why some CPTSD patients respond rapidly to concentrated treatment (supporting the counterpoint) while others require years (supporting the thesis). They are not the same patients. The diagnosis is the same; the structural depth is not.

### Factor 3: Elapsed time (independent of dose)

The third factor is the most counterintuitive. Evidence from multiple sources suggests that elapsed calendar time contributes to healing independently of therapeutic dose — that is, independently of the number or frequency of sessions.

The strongest evidence comes from the sleeper effect: therapeutic gains that emerge or grow after treatment has ended. Shedler's (2010) meta-analytic review found that effect sizes for psychodynamic therapy increase from 0.97 at end of treatment to 1.51 at follow-up beyond 9 months — a pattern unique to psychodynamic approaches and absent in other modalities [shedler-2010-efficacy]. The TADS trial found LTPP non-significant versus TAU at end of treatment but strongly significant (p=0.001) at 42-month follow-up [fonagy-2015-tads]. MBT gains continued growing for 5 years after all treatment ended, while the TAU group deteriorated despite receiving more services [bateman-fonagy-2008-mbt-8yr]. The Helsinki study found psychoanalysis patients improving most in years 5–7, after treatment had concluded [lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr]. The EDT meta-analysis found experiential dynamic therapies non-significant versus active controls at post-treatment but significant at follow-up [lilliengren-2025-edt-meta]. Psychoanalytic therapy (median 242 sessions) outperformed CBT (median 59 sessions) specifically at 5-year follow-up for chronic depression with childhood trauma history — the advantage emerged after treatment, not during it [mia-psychoanalytic-beats-cbt].

Kohut's (1984) distinction between "borrowed" and "owned" psychic structure provides the theoretical mechanism. Borrowed structure is the patient functioning better because the therapist's regulatory capacity is available as an external selfobject resource. When this external resource is removed at termination, gains may erode — this is what happens in short-term treatments where the therapist provides skills and scaffolding. Owned structure, by contrast, is the product of thousands of cycles of attunement, optimal frustration, and repair (transmuting internalisation): genuinely internal regulatory capacities that continue to develop autonomously after treatment ends. The borrowed-vs-owned distinction predicts both the sleeper effect in long-term treatment (owned structure continues developing) and the reverse sleeper effect in short-term treatment (borrowed structure erodes). Abbass (2014) found precisely this reverse pattern for short-term psychodynamic therapy: gains at post-treatment that lost significance at long-term follow-up [abbass-2014-stpp-cochrane].

Spring's memoir data provides the lived-experience confirmation. Her between-session integration claim — "the gaps between therapy sessions can be as developmentally important as the therapy sessions themselves" [spring-2020-recovering-developmental-trauma] — describes the micro-level mechanism: each session installs a micro-increment of neural capacity, which consolidates between sessions. Heacock provides a real-time observation of the borrowed-to-owned transition: during Erin's extended postpartum leave, Sherlock demonstrated self-regulation capacity he had previously relied on her to provide — "I'm going to be okay. You have given me the tools I need" [heacock-2022-psychotherapy-twice-told]. Spring's post-therapy stable period (2017–2019) shows the sleeper effect in vivo: gains from therapy ending in 2016 continued developing through the subsequent years of joyful, generative living [spring-2021-falling-down-getting-back-up].

The implication is that therapy, at least when it targets structural change, does not work like a medication with a dose-response curve. It works like a developmental intervention: it installs capacity — for mentalizing, for self-regulation, for relational engagement — that continues to develop through the patient's subsequent life experience. Shedler (2010) calls this a "catalytic" effect. The therapy does not do the healing; it creates the conditions under which healing can proceed autonomously.

This explains the Helsinki convergence [lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr]: psychoanalysis (646 sessions, 4x/week) and long-term psychodynamic therapy (232 sessions, 2-3x/week) produced indistinguishable outcomes at 10-year follow-up despite a nearly 3:1 difference in dose. If elapsed time contributes independently, then beyond a sufficient therapeutic dose, additional sessions produce diminishing returns — the patient needs not more therapy but more life. Both treatments may have exceeded the "transmuting internalisation threshold" — the point at which enough structure has been built to be self-sustaining — after which the difference between 232 and 646 sessions is noise.

It also explains the therapy-vs-life confound identified across layers. Paris (2020) notes that BPD remits gradually over 24 years regardless of treatment — a natural course effect. Bateman and Fonagy (2008) observe that their MBT patients' post-treatment improvement coincided with re-entering education and employment, suggesting a "virtuous cycle" between therapeutic gains and life engagement. Client narratives repeatedly describe life events — finding a safe partner [reddit-2023-fully-healed-how-long], leaving an abusive environment [reddit-2021-cant-heal-while-surviving], reconnecting with estranged family [murphy-2009-pct-abuse] — as turning points more decisive than any therapeutic technique.

The three factors (plus the safety prerequisite) interact. A patient with shallow structural injury seeking symptom reduction (Factor 1: low endpoint, Factor 2: shallow depth) who arrives with adequate pre-existing safety (Factor 0: cleared) may achieve their goal in weeks through intensive treatment, and Factor 3 (elapsed time) is largely irrelevant. A patient with deep structural disruption seeking personality integration (Factor 1: high endpoint, Factor 2: deep injury) who must first establish environmental and relational safety (Factor 0: not yet cleared) requires not only more sessions but also more elapsed life-time for the installed capacity to develop through real-world experience. For this patient, all factors are fully operative, and the expected timeline is years.

## Why the field appears to disagree

The framework above resolves what initially appears to be a fundamental disagreement in the literature.

**The counterpoint position** (de Jongh, Sciarrino, Feder, Mitchell, Paris, Lilliengren) can be stated precisely: concentrated, evidence-based treatment produces large, rapid, and durable symptom reduction in patients diagnosed with PTSD and CPTSD, including those with developmental trauma histories. Stabilisation phases are often unnecessary. Session limits need not prevent effective treatment. The field's attachment to long-term therapy is unsupported by the evidence and may reflect ideology rather than science.

**The long-term position** (Herman, Steele, Van der Hart, Kohut, Winnicott, Fonagy, Schore, Porges, Perry) can be stated equally precisely: complex developmental trauma produces structural disruptions — in personality organisation, mentalizing capacity, attachment security, autonomic regulation, and self-coherence — that cannot be resolved through symptom-targeted interventions, however effective those interventions are at the symptom level. Structural change requires sustained relational experience over developmental time. There are no shortcuts because the process IS the elapsed time.

These positions are not contradictory. They are claims about different endpoint levels. The counterpoint evidence is strongest precisely where the long-term evidence is weakest (symptom reduction), and weakest where the long-term evidence is strongest (structural change). De Jongh's (2024) 88% CPTSD remission in 8 days is measured by diagnostic instruments (ITQ); Lindfors's (2019) 10-year convergence is measured by personality organisation scales (LPO). They are measuring different things.

The field's confusion arises from four sources:

1. **Implicit endpoint assumptions.** Most RCTs measure symptom reduction because it is tractable and fundable. Most clinical theorists describe structural change because it is what they observe in long-term work. Neither tradition routinely specifies which endpoint level it claims authority over. The result is that each tradition's findings are overgeneralised to endpoint levels where they do not apply.

2. **Diagnostic conflation.** The ICD-11 CPTSD diagnosis captures a heterogeneous population spanning primary to tertiary structural dissociation, minimal to pervasive developmental deficits, and high to low adaptive information processing capacity. Patients at one end of this spectrum may genuinely achieve durable change in 8 days; patients at the other end may require 8 years. Treating them as a single population produces contradictory findings — not because the treatments are inconsistent, but because the patients are structurally different despite sharing a diagnosis. Wylie documents that nearly 80% of surveyed children exposed to interpersonal trauma had experienced multiple and/or prolonged traumatic events, yet all are subsumed under diagnostic categories (ADHD, ODD, bipolar, PTSD) that fail to capture the common aetiology [wylie-long-shadow-trauma].

3. **Institutional incentives.** The evidence base is shaped by what gets funded and published. RCTs require defined endpoints, manualised protocols, and time-limited interventions — producing evidence for brief treatment that is rigorous by design. Long-term treatment outcome evidence is rare because it is expensive, logistically difficult, and incompatible with standard research funding cycles. The result is a systematic asymmetry: the evidence for brief treatment is plentiful and precise; the evidence for long-term treatment is sparse and observational. This is not because brief treatment is more effective — it is because it is more researchable. As Shedler and Gnaulati put it, the "evidence-based therapy myth" is a managed-care-driven narrative that misrepresents research findings to justify session limits [shedler-2020-tyranny-of-time].

4. **The Fisher-Fosha tension: stabilisation-first versus transformation-from-the-get-go.** A fourth dimension of disagreement operates within the long-term camp itself, between two experienced trauma clinicians who both reject brief symptom-focused treatment but disagree fundamentally about how deep therapy should proceed. Fisher, trained under Herman and Van der Kolk, argues for stabilisation-first: the nervous system must be regulated, the prefrontal cortex must be online, and psychoeducation must precede any processing. She explicitly avoids EMDR with highly functioning complex trauma clients due to overwhelm risk [fisher-cutting-edge-tist]. Her principle — "slower is faster" — means that the early abreactive approaches failed precisely because they skipped stabilisation [fisher-transforming-legacy]. Fosha, trained in Davanloo's short-term dynamic tradition, argues the opposite: healing is "already there to access from the first contact with the patient, including the most traumatised person that we encounter" [fosha-2019-aedp-intimacy]. Rather than preparing the patient for transformation, the therapist should entrain it immediately through experiential, body-based, relationally safe engagement. Meta-processing consolidates change within the session rather than deferring integration.

    Both clinicians are experienced, both work with complex trauma, and both ground their claims in clinical observation. What explains the divergence? Two interpretations emerge from the data. First, they may be working with different structural depths within the CPTSD population: Fisher explicitly describes patients with tertiary structural dissociation and chronic destabilisation; Fosha describes patients whose defences, while rigid, still permit experiential engagement when met with sufficient relational safety. The Fisher-Fosha tension may therefore be another instance of the diagnostic conflation problem — the same diagnosis masking different structural injuries. Second, the disagreement may concern the onset, not the duration, of transformation. Fosha claims that transformation begins immediately; she does not claim it completes rapidly. A client might experience genuine within-session transformation from the first session and still require years for full structural repair. The Fisher-Fosha tension sharpens the temporal question: does the clock start at the establishment of safety (Fisher) or at the first moment of experiential contact (Fosha)? The answer may depend on the structural depth of the patient.

## The developmental mechanism: why structural change takes years

The framework describes WHAT determines duration. A separate question is WHY structural change requires years. Here, the review finds a remarkable convergence across nine independent theoretical traditions.

Kohut (1984) describes transmuting internalisation: the patient gradually builds psychic structure through thousands of cycles of empathic attunement, optimal frustration, and repair. Winnicott (1965) describes regression to dependence and resumed maturational processes — developmental time that cannot be compressed. Bromberg (1998) describes gradual expansion of the capacity to "stand in the spaces" between dissociated self-states. Benjamin (1988) describes repeated cycles of mutual recognition, destruction, and survival that incrementally build the capacity for intersubjectivity. Fonagy (2002) describes the development of mentalizing capacity through attachment-based experiential learning.

From neuroscience: Schore (2000, 2021) describes experience-dependent maturation of right-hemisphere regulatory structures. Porges (2022) describes autonomic reorganisation from chronic defensive states toward ventral vagal flexibility. Perry and Hambrick (2019) describe sequential, bottom-up brain reorganisation requiring "patterned, repetitive" experiences at each developmental level. The BCPSG (2010) describes the "accumulation of many small shifts" in implicit relational knowing.

These nine descriptions — from self psychology, object relations, relational psychoanalysis, intersubjectivity theory, mentalization theory, affect regulation neuroscience, polyvagal theory, the neurosequential model, and implicit process research — were developed independently, use different concepts and methods, and emerge from different clinical and research traditions. Yet they converge on three features:

1. **Change happens through many repeated relational cycles** — not through insight, technique, or a single breakthrough.
2. **What changes is internal structure** — not symptoms, but the organisation of self, relationship, and regulation.
3. **The process is inherently temporal** — it cannot be compressed by technique, frequency, or pharmacological enhancement, because the relevant "dose" is not sessions but completed developmental cycles.

The new sources deepen this convergence. Ogden (2006) provides the hierarchical processing model that explains the mechanism's pace: sensorimotor processing is characterised by "greater fixity and concreteness" — closer to "read-only memory" than cognitive "random-access memory" [ogden-2006-trauma-and-the-body]. Emotional responses to very strong stimuli "do not appear to extinguish" (Frijda, 1986, via Ogden); new responses must be built alongside old ones rather than replacing them. This is inherently slower than cognitive restructuring. Fisher crystallises the clinical implication: "survivors have symptoms instead of memories" (Van der Kolk, via Fisher), and these implicit (somatic, emotional, perceptual) memories must be processed through "bite-sizing" because the nervous system can integrate only small pieces at a time [fisher-transforming-legacy]. The developmental mechanism operates at the level of implicit memory — a level more resistant to change than explicit, narrative memory.

Spring provides the lived-experience confirmation. Her developmental-rate metaphor describes survivors rebuilding missed developmental stages from scratch — "being soothed, calming down, tolerating distress, labelling emotions" — with each stage requiring the prior stage [spring-2020-recovering-developmental-trauma]. Her 9.5-year timeline maps precisely onto the logic of sequential stage-building: "you can't do the next stage until you've conquered this stage." Progress is invisible week-to-week, just as a baby's daily bath or mealtime does not look like learning but is enormous developmental work. Bisson's clinical case converges from a completely independent source: Davitt describes himself as "an eight-year-old boy, even though I was a 50-year-old man" — arrested emotional development that must be rebuilt in therapy, a process that is developmental by definition [bisson-2024-recognising-cptsd].

Razvi's personal healing trajectory illustrates the layered nature of the mechanism [heacock-2019-healing-psychedelics]. Talk therapy provided intellectual understanding only ("didn't fundamentally change anything in terms of the patterns"). Two and a half years of somatic work released traumatic charge. A further 1.5–2 years of attachment work built new relational patterns. Then a single MDMA session accessed the dissociative core. Each layer addressed a different level of the hierarchy; each required the prior layer as "scaffolding." The three-tier framework (stress → panic → dissociation) maps these layers onto the nervous system: dissociative shutdown is the deepest and most treatment-resistant level, inaccessible to modalities that rely on self-report. Even with psychedelic compression of the final layer, the total pre-psychedelic conventional trajectory was 4–5+ years.

The probability that the convergence across these independent traditions is coincidental is low. It constitutes the review's strongest theoretical argument for why structural change requires years: the biology and psychology of developmental repair operate on a timescale set by the pace of experience-dependent neural maturation and relational learning, not by the efficiency of the therapeutic technique.

**However, the neurobiological rationale for this convergence is more contested than the convergence itself.** Tilson (2025) mounts a serious critique of two frameworks frequently cited to explain WHY the developmental mechanism is slow: the triune brain model, which she argues was "disproved in the 1970s," and polyvagal theory, which she describes as "untenable or highly implausible" per Grossman's critique [tilson-2025-body-not-keeping-score]. If these frameworks are scientifically unfounded, the specific neurobiological explanation for the mechanism's pace weakens. But the clinical convergence holds independently: Spring, Heacock, Fisher, Bisson, and Razvi all observe slow developmental change without depending on triune brain theory or polyvagal theory for their observations. The mechanism's existence is more robustly supported than its neurobiological explanation. The review should be honest about this gap: the claim "structural change takes years" rests on convergent clinical observation and outcome data; the claim "this is because of hierarchical brain architecture and vagal regulation" rests on contested neuroscience.

This mechanism also explains why the sleeper effect is specific to approaches that target structural change. Short-term symptom-focused treatments (CBT, brief STPP) show the opposite pattern: gains that diminish at follow-up [abbass-2014-stpp-cochrane]. If the treatment installs symptom management skills rather than structural capacity, there is nothing to continue developing after treatment ends. The sleeper effect requires that something structural has been initiated — a developmental process that, once set in motion, continues under its own power through the patient's subsequent relational life.

## Six trajectory shapes

Cross-layer analysis reveals six distinct trajectory shapes, each associated with different endpoint levels and populations:

1. **Dose-response** [howard-1986-dose-effect; paris-2020-access-pd; shedler-2020-tyranny-of-time]. Negatively accelerated: steep early gains, diminishing returns. Shedler's synthesis of the Lambert OQ-45 data (N=10,000+) gives the clearest quantification: 50% clinically significant change at 21 sessions, 75% at 40+ sessions, with continued improvement at 2+ years and no ceiling [shedler-2020-tyranny-of-time]. Best fits mild-to-moderate presentations at symptom-reduction endpoints. The default assumption of the RCT literature.

2. **Sleeper/catalytic** [shedler-2010-efficacy; fonagy-2015-tads; bateman-fonagy-2008-mbt-8yr; lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr; mia-psychoanalytic-beats-cbt]. Gains emerge or grow after treatment ends. Best fits structural change endpoints in psychodynamic/psychoanalytic treatment. Therapy installs owned capacity (Kohut); life exercises it. The borrowed-vs-owned bridge provides the theoretical explanation: below the transmuting internalisation threshold, gains are borrowed and will erode; above it, gains are owned and will grow.

3. **Spiral/recursive** [herman-1992-trauma-recovery; steele-2005-phase-oriented; vanderhart-2006-haunted-self; bromberg-1998-standing-spaces; blueknot-trauma-practice]. Patients cycle through phases, returning to earlier work as deeper material emerges. Phase-based in structure, organic in execution. The Blue Knot Foundation endorses this explicitly: "these phases are NOT linear — safety must be re-established repeatedly" [blueknot-trauma-practice]. The dominant trajectory in the complex trauma treatment literature.

4. **Episodic/cyclical** [blueknot-trauma-practice; myptsd-2021-how-long-therapy-2]. Alternating periods of intensive engagement and self-managed maintenance, extending over years or decades. Normalised by practitioner and client narratives as a valid pattern, not a treatment failure. The Blue Knot Foundation frames this as a legitimate model: some individuals benefit from therapy "on and off over their lifetime" [blueknot-trauma-practice].

5. **Rapid/targeted** [dejongh-2024-emdr-science; sciarrino-2020-intensive-review; feder-2025-ketamine-exposure]. Concentrated symptom reduction, often pharmacologically enhanced. Linear and goal-directed. Best fits patients with high pre-existing adaptive capacity (and, the safety construct suggests, high pre-existing safety), or those seeking symptom reduction rather than structural change.

6. **Episodic/developmental** [spring-2021-falling-down-getting-back-up]. This trajectory is distinct from the cyclical pattern (trajectory 4) because it includes documented retention of prior gains across episodes. Spring's case is paradigmatic: ~9 years of therapy ending in 2016, followed by ~3 years of stable, generative functioning (2017–2019), then a new trauma (rape) requiring a new episode of therapy. Crucially, the second episode was qualitatively different from the first. Spring entered with psychoeducation, self-compassion, and active engagement strategies entirely absent during her 2005 breakdown: "I knew what was happening. My trauma reactions made sense to me" [spring-2021-falling-down-getting-back-up]. Prior gains were retained — the sleeper effect operating through the post-therapy years had consolidated owned structure that survived the new trauma. The trajectory implies that the total healing arc may include multiple therapy episodes separated by functional periods, where each episode operates at a higher developmental level than the last. This is not relapse; it is developmental progression disrupted by new events, with the critical feature that prior developmental achievements are preserved.

These trajectories are not modality-specific — they are endpoint-specific. A patient pursuing symptom reduction through EMDR will follow trajectory 5. The same patient, later pursuing structural change through psychodynamic therapy, may follow trajectory 2 or 3. The trajectory reflects the goal, not the method.

## What the framework cannot explain

The framework has genuine explanatory power, but several findings resist easy integration.

**The Brand paradox.** Brand et al. (2019) found that patients with HIGHER baseline dissociation improved MORE and FASTER than less dissociative patients [brand-2019-topdd]. If structural depth predicts longer duration, more dissociative patients should improve more slowly. One explanation is that the measures captured symptom reduction, not structural integration — higher-dissociation patients had more room to improve on symptom scales. But this remains an open question.

**The Helsinki convergence.** Psychoanalysis (646 sessions) and long-term psychodynamic therapy (232 sessions) produced equivalent outcomes at 10 years [lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr]. The elapsed-time factor (Factor 3) explains why tripling the dose did not triple the outcome. But it does not explain whether 232 sessions was sufficient for structural change or whether both groups reached a natural developmental ceiling. The study's convergence at 10 years could reflect adequate dose in both conditions, or it could reflect a ceiling effect on the structural measures used.

**The Fisher-Fosha puzzle.** Two experienced trauma clinicians, both working with complex developmental trauma, reach opposite conclusions about whether stabilisation must precede transformation. Fisher says yes: "slower is faster"; skip stabilisation and the nervous system overwhelms [fisher-transforming-legacy; fisher-cutting-edge-tist]. Fosha says no: "healing from the get-go"; transformation is accessible from the first session if the therapist provides sufficient relational safety [fosha-2019-aedp-intimacy]. This is not a disagreement resolvable by the framework's factors alone. It may reflect different patient populations (structural depth), different definitions of "transformation" (micro-endpoint vs. macro-endpoint), or a genuine theoretical divergence about the role of stabilisation. The puzzle matters because both positions have clinical consequences: Fisher's approach may delay genuine processing for patients who are ready; Fosha's approach may overwhelm patients who are not. The resolution likely lies in accurate assessment of the individual patient's structural depth and pre-existing safety — but such assessment tools do not yet exist.

**The "right therapist" confound.** Client narratives repeatedly describe years of ineffective therapy followed by rapid progress once a well-matched therapist was found [myptsd-2019-how-long-therapy; reddit-2023-7-therapists-average]. Heacock provides the most granular data: when Sherlock was transferred to another trainee during Erin's graduation, the new therapist said something that ruptured the relationship in a single session — "she said something that made me angry... she looked at me and said, and that's why Erin's afraid of you. And I never went back after that" [heacock-2022-psychotherapy-twice-told]. Three years of trust-building evaporated in one sentence. Davitt similarly required two failed therapeutic relationships before finding his match [bisson-2024-recognising-cptsd]. This suggests that much of the reported duration reflects the escalation gap — the time spent finding the right match — rather than the actual healing process. Duration data from naturalistic accounts may systematically overestimate the time needed when the match is right from the start.

**The safety prerequisite.** Multiple client accounts describe therapy as ineffective until environmental safety was achieved — the same therapist, same modality, dramatically different outcomes [reddit-2023-fully-healed-how-long; reddit-2021-cant-heal-while-surviving]. This implies that the "clock" does not start at the first session but at the establishment of safety. All duration data that includes pre-safety treatment time is inflated relative to the actual therapeutic process. The review cannot estimate the magnitude of this inflation, but it is potentially large: Eger (2017) describes 35 years before processing could begin; ElishaAlison describes years of therapy during ongoing abuse that produced nothing. The construct has been elevated to Factor 0 in the framework, but the fundamental measurement problem remains: we do not know how to separate pre-safety time from post-safety time in existing datasets.

**The evidence critique.** Tilson (2025) raises a challenge that the framework must confront honestly: Somatic Experiencing, one of the therapeutic approaches most closely associated with the long-term position, has produced only "one small RCT in 50+ years" [tilson-2025-body-not-keeping-score]. Polyvagal theory, cited by multiple sources in this review as the neurobiological basis for autonomic reorganisation, has been criticised as "untenable or highly implausible." The triune brain model, used by Ogden as a hierarchical processing metaphor, was debunked decades ago. This does not invalidate the clinical convergence — nine independent traditions converge on slow developmental change regardless of which neuroscience is invoked. But it does mean that the field's strongest theoretical frameworks for explaining WHY structural change takes years rest on contested science. The clinical observation is robust; the explanatory mechanism is not.

**The cost-effectiveness challenge.** Koeser et al. (2023) found LTPP costs approximately £33,000 per QALY — above the NICE threshold of £20,000/QALY [koeser-2023-tads-cost]. Even if long-term therapy works, it may not be cost-effective by standard health-economic criteria. The authors argue the time horizon was too short to capture the full benefit (the sleeper effect would increase QALYs over a longer horizon), but this remains unproven. The institutional reality is that treatments exceeding cost-effectiveness thresholds face funding barriers regardless of clinical efficacy.

**AI-assisted self-therapy.** An emerging phenomenon reported in Reddit threads [reddit-2025-full-cure-how-long] but without empirical validation. If effective, it would challenge the assumption that a human therapeutic relationship is necessary for structural change. The developmental mechanism convergence described above — grounded in relational cycles, right-brain attunement, and implicit relational knowing — suggests that AI-mediated processing may operate at a different endpoint level (symptom management, cognitive reframing) than human-relational therapy (structural change). But this is speculation; the phenomenon warrants monitoring.

## Critique of the literature as a body

Beyond appraising individual sources, the synthesis reveals structural features of how the field has constructed the question of therapy duration.

**The time-horizon problem.** The RCT literature overwhelmingly measures outcomes at end-of-treatment or at brief follow-up (3–12 months). The sleeper effect data demonstrate that this time horizon systematically underestimates the benefit of structural interventions and overestimates the durability of symptom-focused interventions. The Abbass (2014) Cochrane review of short-term psychodynamic therapy found gains at post-treatment that lost significance at long-term follow-up — the reverse of the sleeper effect [abbass-2014-stpp-cochrane]. If the standard evaluation window had been 5 years rather than 5 months, the evidence base for brief treatment would look different.

**The silence on dropout.** The field does not systematically track who leaves therapy or what happens to them. Only 10 of 89 sources in this review report dropout data in any form. The treatment-engagement paradox is devastating for duration claims: the interpersonal difficulties that define the population (hostility, alliance problems, rigid interaction, poor reflective ability) are the same factors that predict dropout [freixo-ferreira-2023-bpd-dropout; knekt-2021-dropout]. All duration claims in the literature are survivorship-biased — they describe how long healing takes for the people who stayed. The people who most need long-term treatment are systematically missing from the evidence.

**The silence on access.** The majority of sources across all layers do not address who can access the treatment they describe. Kohut's (1984) model assumes 4–5 sessions per week for years. Bateman and Fonagy's (2008) MBT requires 5-day-per-week partial hospitalisation. Even standard weekly therapy for 3–5 years represents a financial commitment inaccessible to most people with complex developmental trauma, who are disproportionately concentrated in lower socioeconomic groups [paris-2020-access-pd]. Spring names this structural failure directly: survivors who have been in therapy "10, 20, 30 years" without recovery are not failing — society has failed to "adequately provide public transport to enable people to head north" [spring-2020-what-does-recovery-look-like]. The recommendation "healing takes years" is clinically honest but structurally unjust unless accompanied by a commitment to making years of treatment accessible. The review cannot resolve this tension, but it is obligated to name it.

**The psychoanalytic vocabulary problem.** A striking finding from the safety-as-clock-starter analysis is that the psychoanalytic tradition describes safety comprehensively — but in its own vocabulary, rendering the concept invisible to the broader literature. Winnicott's "holding environment" IS therapeutic safety. Kohut's "selfobject transference" IS felt safety. Benjamin's "surviving destruction" IS demonstrating that the relationship can contain aggression. Bromberg's "standing in the spaces" requires a safe relational context. The psychoanalytic tradition's apparent silence on safety is terminological, not substantive. This vocabulary gap has real consequences: when the phase-oriented literature (Herman, Van der Hart, Steele) identifies "safety" as a prerequisite, the psychoanalytic literature appears not to address it — when in fact it addresses little else. The field's discourse about safety and stabilisation proceeds as if these are concepts invented by the phase model, when they are in fact descriptions of what Winnicott and Kohut described decades earlier using different words. The convergence across traditions is even stronger than it appears, once the vocabulary is translated.

**The absence of long-term follow-up.** No study in this review follows a complex trauma population beyond 10 years with repeated measurement. The Helsinki study [lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr] comes closest but used a mixed anxiety/mood disorder population, not a defined complex trauma sample. The memoir evidence (Layer 6) provides the only window into multi-decade trajectories, but memoirs are self-selected, narratively shaped, and subject to survivorship bias. The field literally does not know what happens to complex trauma patients over 20 or 30 years of living — whether structural gains hold, whether new challenges emerge, whether the developmental process initiated by therapy continues or stalls. This is the most consequential gap in the evidence base.

**The Layer 4 gap.** Eight practitioner-layer sources (Layer 4) were insufficiently integrated in the initial synthesis, despite containing directly relevant data. These sources — drawn from clinical essays, practitioner guides, and therapy-adjacent journalism — collectively paint a picture that the academic literature alone does not: Shedler's dose-response data showing 50% improvement at 21 sessions and 75% at 40+ [shedler-2020-tyranny-of-time]; Alpern's direct statement from within the EMDR/SE tradition that complex trauma "cannot be resolved in six sessions of anything" [alpern-2025-truth-complex-trauma]; the Blue Knot Foundation's 84% expert consensus on the phase model with treatment potentially lasting "decades" [blueknot-trauma-practice]; the traumalescence concept offering the only explicit developmental timeline in the dataset (3–6 months awareness, 6–18 months neural strengthening, 18–36 months integration) [substack-traumalescence]; Grossmark's companioning as inherently non-brief therapeutic stance [grossmark-psychoanalytic-companioning]; Wylie's documentation of diagnostic conflation (80% multiple trauma) [wylie-long-shadow-trauma]; and the recognition prerequisite — the insight that "because living in a triggered state of alarm is so familiar, many don't realise trauma is the source" [mia-injured-not-broken], meaning that the clock cannot start until the person even recognises they are injured. The victim-to-author turning point described in Woodward Thomas [psychnetworker-evolving-beyond] identifies the specific developmental moment at which the trajectory shifts from backward-looking processing to forward-looking transformation. These sources are individually modest in methodological rigour, but collectively they represent the clinical consensus that the RCT literature systematically fails to capture.

**Institutional incentives shape the evidence.** The evidence base for brief, manualised treatments is large and methodologically rigorous because these treatments are compatible with RCT design, insurance reimbursement, and academic publishing incentives. The evidence base for long-term, relational treatments is sparse and observational because these treatments are expensive to study, difficult to manualise, and incompatible with standard funding cycles. The result is a systematic bias in the evidence landscape: the treatments with the most evidence are not necessarily the most effective for the defined population; they are the most researchable. CIS methodology (Dixon-Woods et al., 2006) was developed precisely for situations where the available evidence is shaped by systematic factors that a conventional hierarchy-of-evidence approach would obscure.

## The synthesising argument

The review's central argument can now be stated precisely:

**The duration of healing from complex developmental trauma is determined by the interaction of a safety prerequisite with three factors: the level of change sought (symptom reduction, functional improvement, structural change, growth, or ongoing directional recovery), the depth of structural disruption (from primary to tertiary dissociation, from preserved to pervasive developmental deficits), and the contribution of elapsed life-time to post-therapeutic development.** The therapeutic clock does not start at the first session but at the establishment of environmental, relational, and autonomic safety — a prerequisite whose duration is itself poorly studied but potentially large. When the endpoint is symptom reduction, the structural depth is shallow, and safety is already established, weeks of concentrated treatment may suffice. When the endpoint is structural change, the injury is deep, and safety must first be built, years of sustained relational experience — within and beyond formal therapy — are required, because the developmental mechanism of change operates on a biological timescale that cannot be compressed.

The field's disagreement about duration is not a genuine disagreement. It is a failure to specify endpoint levels, a conflation of structurally different patients under shared diagnostic labels, an unrecognised safety prerequisite that inflates all duration estimates, and a systematic asymmetry in the evidence base that privileges researchable treatments over effective ones.

The working thesis — that healing takes years — is refined rather than refuted by the counterpoint evidence. It should be stated with four qualifications:

1. **Symptom reduction is achievable in weeks to months** for many patients, including some with CPTSD diagnoses, through concentrated evidence-based treatment. This is a genuine clinical achievement, not a false positive. But it is not the same as structural healing.

2. **Structural change requires years** — not because therapists are inefficient, but because the developmental mechanism of change is inherently temporal. Nine independent theoretical traditions converge on this conclusion. The sleeper effect provides empirical confirmation: therapy installs owned capacity (Kohut) that continues to develop after treatment ends, sometimes for years. The borrowed-vs-owned distinction explains both the sleeper effect in long-term treatment and the reverse sleeper effect in short-term treatment — and identifies the transmuting internalisation threshold as the critical clinical variable.

3. **The clock starts at safety, not at the first session.** Duration estimates from the literature are systematically inflated by the inclusion of pre-safety treatment time. When safety is already established (through environmental stability, pre-existing secure attachment, or the containing structure of intensive formats), the therapeutic process may be substantially shorter than naturalistic accounts suggest. This qualification simultaneously supports the thesis (the process still takes years for patients who must build safety from scratch) and the counterpoint (rapid responders may have pre-existing safety that accelerates the process).

4. **The recommendation "healing takes years" carries an access obligation.** If years of specialised therapy are necessary for structural healing, then the field bears responsibility for making this accessible — through stepped care models [links-2015-gpm-bpd], intensive formats that reduce dropout [sciarrino-2020-intensive-review], team-based approaches [bateman-fonagy-2008-mbt-8yr; farina-2025-rapid-rare], and policy advocacy for funding structures that match the timescale of the condition. Spring is right that many survivors who have spent decades without recovery are not failing; the system is failing them [spring-2020-what-does-recovery-look-like].

The synthesis does not claim that all patients with complex developmental trauma require years of therapy. It claims that all patients with deep structural disruption who seek structural change — personality integration, earned attachment security, mentalizing capacity, the ability to love without terror — will find that this takes years. And it claims that the field's inability to say this clearly, because of institutional incentives, unstated endpoint assumptions, contested neuroscience, and the conflation of structurally different patients, constitutes a failure of honesty that harms the patients who most need accurate expectations.
