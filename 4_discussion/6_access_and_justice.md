# Access, privilege, and justice

## The problem

If the conclusion of this review is "healing from complex developmental trauma takes 5–15 years of therapy," this is implicitly an argument for something most people on earth cannot access. The population most affected by developmental trauma — people from disadvantaged backgrounds, intergenerational poverty, marginalized communities — are the least likely to have access to long-term, relationally-oriented therapy.

This is not an unfortunate side-effect of the thesis. It is a core tension within it. A review that argues for long-term therapy without addressing who can actually have it is ethically incomplete.

## The tension

- **If the thesis is right,** then the gap between what people need and what they can access is enormous — and the review must say so, plainly.
- **If access is impossible for most,** then the review should consider whether advocating for long-term individual therapy is the *right* response, or whether the evidence should also inform alternative delivery models (group-based, community-based, lower-cost, technology-assisted).
- **The Western, individualistic therapy model** may not be the only path to healing — and the review's silence on non-Western and community-based healing traditions is itself a limitation with justice implications.

## How we mitigated it

1. **This dedicated discussion section** — not a footnote in the limitations, but a standalone discussion of the ethical dimension.
2. **The implications section** (`4_discussion/8_implications.md`) addresses policy, insurance, and funding structures as domains of impact.
3. **The therapy-vs-life discussion** (§4) opens the door to non-therapy pathways that may be more accessible.
4. **The counterpoint layer** includes intensive formats that may compress calendar duration and reduce total cost.

## What remains unmitigated

- The review studies Western individual psychotherapy almost exclusively. Non-Western healing traditions, community-based approaches, and peer-support models are absent from the evidence base — not because they are irrelevant but because the search strategy was not designed to capture them.
- The review does not attempt to estimate costs, insurance coverage patterns, or access statistics. It is a clinical argument, not a health economics analysis.
- The reflexivity section should address the reviewer's own positionality regarding access — as someone likely practising within the system being critiqued.

## Feed-back into the plan

*Questions to address during synthesis:*

- Do any sources in any layer address access, cost, or the structural barriers to long-term therapy? Log these.
- Do client narratives (Layer 5) mention financial barriers, insurance limitations, or forced termination due to access issues? This would be direct evidence.
- Does the therapy-vs-life data suggest alternative pathways that are more accessible? If community, relationships, and creative practice are significant factors, what does that imply for service delivery?
- Should a future iteration add a layer or sublayer on community-based and non-Western approaches to healing from developmental trauma? (This would be a targeted re-run.)
- Consider: does the review's honest conclusion create an obligation to address what should be done for people who cannot access what the review says they need?

## Implications for the thesis

The thesis cannot stand alone as a clinical claim. It carries an implicit policy argument: if healing requires years of therapy, then either (a) long-term therapy must be made accessible, or (b) alternative pathways to the same outcome must be found, or (c) we must accept that structural healing is a privilege. Option (c) is the current reality. The review should name it.
