# Access, privilege, and justice

## The structural injustice

If the conclusion of this review is that healing from complex developmental trauma requires years of relationally-oriented therapy, then the review is advocating for something most people on earth cannot access. This is not an unfortunate implication of the thesis. It is a core tension within it, and a review that argues for long-term therapy without confronting who can actually receive it is ethically incomplete.

The injustice is structural, not incidental. Developmental trauma is concentrated in populations with the fewest resources: intergenerational poverty, marginalised communities, families fractured by adversity. Paris states the problem with characteristic directness: DBT costs $220 per week, over $10,000 per year; "most PD patients have limited education and low-level jobs" [paris-2020-access-pd]. The population most damaged by developmental trauma is the population least likely to access the treatment this review says they need. Every duration estimate in this review — 3 to 5 years for phase-oriented treatment [herman-1992-trauma-recovery; vanderhart-2006-haunted-self], 5 to 7 years for personality organisation change [lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr], decades for full post-traumatic growth [walker-2013-complex-ptsd; eger-2017-the-choice] — carries an implicit price tag that excludes most of the relevant population.

Spring names this failure most plainly. Survivors who have been in therapy for 10, 20, or 30 years without recovery are not failing — society has failed to "adequately provide public transport to enable people to head north" [spring-2020-what-does-recovery-look-like]. The metaphor is precise: recovery is a direction (northward), and therapy is the transport system. Those who can afford private cars — frequent sessions with specialist clinicians over years — may travel steadily. Those who depend on public transport find the routes infrequent, the coverage patchy, and the journeys interrupted by forced transfers. Spring counts herself among "the lucky unlucky ones," distinguishing her situation from "the unlucky unlucky ones" who lack even the resources she has [spring-2021-falling-down-getting-back-up].

## The evidence: silence and ground truth

The access extraction reveals a striking pattern: the majority of sources across all six layers do not address who can access the treatment they describe. The silence is itself a finding.

The psychoanalytic layer is the most resource-intensive and the most silent. Kohut's model assumes four to five sessions per week for years [kohut-1984-how-analysis-cures]. Winnicott's developmental regression requires sustained high-frequency contact [winnicott-1965-maturational]. Bateman and Fonagy's MBT programme requires five-day-per-week partial hospitalisation — a level of provision rarely available even within the NHS [bateman-fonagy-2008-mbt-8yr]. None of these sources addresses who can afford this. The neuroscience layer is entirely silent on access: seven sources, zero mentions [porges-2022-polyvagal; hambrick-perry-2019-nmt; schore-2000-right-brain; lanius-2011-neurobiology-cptsd; samson-teicher-2023-maltreatment; ogden-2006-trauma-and-the-body; bcpsg-2010-change-psychotherapy]. Mechanism-focused research abstracts away the patient's material reality.

The ground truth comes from clients. Reddit posters describe the lived experience of access failure with an immediacy absent from the clinical literature. One poster describes $4,000–6,000 deductibles and "500 work-arounds for being poor" [myptsd-2015-cant-afford-therapy]. Another describes being on Medicaid with access to only two therapists — one female, one male — and no further choice [reddit-2023-fully-healed-how-long]. A third tolerates racism, bigotry, and homophobia from available therapists because there are no alternatives [reddit-2023-7-therapists-average]. A fourth receives monthly sessions — "pure hell" — because affordability, not clinical need, determines frequency [myptsd-2020-session-frequency].

These are not anecdotes decorating a structural argument. They are the structural argument expressed in lived experience. Access does not merely determine whether someone receives therapy. It determines the dose. When affordability permits only monthly sessions rather than weekly ones, the patient receives a subtherapeutic frequency that may extend duration, undermine the relational continuity on which structural change depends, and ultimately produce the treatment failures that are then attributed to the intractability of the condition.

Two cases illustrate the inverse: what happens when access barriers are removed. Foo's therapeutic turning point — the most transformative relationship in the memoir layer — was enabled by $6,400 of pro bono therapy from Dr. Ham [foo-2022-what-my-bones-know]. Mac accessed 160 sessions through icap, a UK charity — a level of provision unavailable through standard pathways [murphy-2009-pct-abuse]. In both cases, healing occurred because the access barrier was bypassed through charity, not because the system provided what was needed. Pro bono provision is not a solution. It is an indictment.

## The cost-effectiveness trap

Even when long-term therapy demonstrably works, it may not satisfy standard health-economic criteria. Koeser et al. found that long-term psychodynamic psychotherapy costs approximately £5,500 in direct treatment costs and produces a cost per quality-adjusted life year of approximately £33,000 — above the NICE threshold of £20,000 per QALY [koeser-2023-tads-cost]. The authors argue that the time horizon was too short to capture the full benefit: the sleeper effect, documented extensively in this review, means that gains continue to accrue for years after treatment ends. If the economic evaluation had extended to ten or fifteen years rather than four, the cost-effectiveness ratio would shift. But this remains unproven, and the institutional reality is that treatments exceeding cost-effectiveness thresholds face funding barriers regardless of clinical efficacy.

The cost-effectiveness framework itself may be structurally unsuited to evaluating treatments for conditions produced by developmental adversity. A QALY measures health-related quality of life in standardised units. But the gains from structural change — earned attachment security, mentalising capacity, the ability to parent without transmitting trauma intergenerationally — extend beyond the individual patient and beyond the timeframe of any economic evaluation. The intergenerational transmission argument is rarely quantified, but it is clinically evident: Wylie documents that nearly 80% of surveyed children exposed to interpersonal trauma had experienced multiple or prolonged events [wylie-long-shadow-trauma], suggesting that untreated parental trauma is a significant risk factor for the next generation's developmental injury. A cost-effectiveness analysis that captures only the treated patient's QALYs systematically undervalues treatments that interrupt intergenerational cycles.

## Partial solutions: what the evidence suggests

The review cannot resolve the access problem, but it can identify directions from the evidence that reduce, rather than merely describe, the gap.

**Stepped care models.** Links's Good Psychiatric Management provides the most explicit access-oriented design in the evidence base [links-2015-gpm-bpd]. GPM was developed as a generalist-accessible, first-line treatment to reduce the specialist bottleneck: primary care providers deliver structured psychoeducation and case management, reserving specialist referral for patients who do not meet milestones at defined intervals (6 weeks, 6 months, 12 months, 18 months). The model explicitly addresses Paris's concern about ethical responsibility to keep waiting lists short. It does not resolve the duration problem — patients who need structural change still need years — but it ensures that the first tier of care does not require the scarce specialist resources that bottleneck the system.

**Intensive formats.** Sciarrino's systematic review of intensive treatment delivery provides the strongest access-adjacent finding: concentrated formats (daily sessions over one to three weeks) achieve large effect sizes (d = 1.57) with dramatically lower dropout — 5.51% versus 18–39% in standard weekly delivery [sciarrino-2020-intensive-review]. Lower dropout is itself an access intervention. If the population most affected by developmental trauma is the population most likely to drop out of standard treatment, then a format that retains them in treatment addresses an access barrier that is relational and psychological, not only financial. The intensive format's containing structure — removing the patient from their stressful environment, immersing them in a therapeutic milieu — may itself provide the safety prerequisite (Factor 0 from the synthesis) through structure rather than through extended time. The cost per episode may be comparable to or lower than years of weekly sessions, though residential programmes introduce their own barriers (travel, childcare, employment disruption).

**Team-based approaches.** MBT's team model [bateman-fonagy-2008-mbt-8yr] and Farina's multi-clinician recommendation [farina-2025-rapid-rare] distribute the therapeutic burden across providers rather than concentrating it in a single specialist relationship. This has workforce implications: training many generalists to provide adequate relational care may be more sustainable than training a few specialists to provide optimal care. Fisher's workbook model [fisher-transforming-legacy] pushes in this direction by providing structured psychoeducation as a shared resource within the therapeutic relationship — though Fisher explicitly warns against using it outside that relationship, maintaining the relational frame.

**The therapy-plus ecology.** The therapy-versus-life analysis (Section 4) carries access implications. If healing is co-produced by therapy and life, then interventions that improve the conditions of life — stable housing, safe relationships, vocational opportunity, community belonging — are not supplementary to therapy but integral to healing. This reframes the access question. The challenge is not only to make years of specialist therapy available but to create environments in which the capacity installed by therapy can be exercised. Spring's distinction between "heading north" and arriving at a destination applies here: a system that provides therapy without addressing the material conditions that prevent patients from using their developing capacities outside the consulting room is providing a compass without clearing the road.

**Pharmacologically enhanced therapy.** MDMA-assisted psychotherapy [mitchell-2021-mdma-phase3] and ketamine-enhanced exposure therapy [feder-2025-ketamine-exposure] achieved large effects in condensed timeframes. If pharmacological agents can compress the processing that otherwise requires years, they represent a potential access intervention — though regulatory barriers, cost, and the early state of the evidence limit near-term applicability. Heacock's account of Razvi's trajectory is instructive: conventional somatic therapy took 4–5 years to address dissociative trauma; MDMA accessed the dissociative core in a single session [heacock-2019-healing-psychedelics]. If this generalises, pharmacologically enhanced formats could dramatically reduce the total treatment time for the most structurally damaged patients — the patients for whom access barriers are most devastating.

## What remains unresolved

The review cannot resolve the access problem because the problem is political and economic, not clinical. The evidence tells us what people need. It does not tell us how to fund it, how to distribute it, or how to reconcile the timescale of structural healing with the timescale of insurance coverage and health-system budgets.

Three tensions remain genuinely unresolved. First, the review studies Western individual psychotherapy almost exclusively. Non-Western healing traditions, community-based approaches, and peer-support models are absent from the evidence base — not because they are irrelevant but because the search strategy was not designed to capture them. This is a limitation with justice implications: the review may inadvertently reinforce the assumption that individual therapy is the only valid pathway, when community-based practices may offer more accessible routes to the relational and regulatory capacities that constitute structural change.

Second, the review does not quantify costs, coverage, or access statistics across health systems. It identifies the structural injustice but cannot estimate its magnitude.

Third, the reflexivity that Spring and Davitt model — acknowledging one's own privilege in having accessed treatment [spring-2021-falling-down-getting-back-up; bisson-2024-recognising-cptsd] — applies to the review itself. The evidence base is shaped by whose voices reach publication. Memoirs are written by people who have recovered enough to write. Reddit posts are written by people with internet access and sufficient English literacy. Clinical case studies are conducted with patients who remained in treatment long enough to be studied. The voices most absent from this review — those who needed long-term therapy, could not access it, and did not recover — are precisely the voices that the access argument most urgently requires. Their absence is the most consequential form of survivorship bias in the evidence base.

## The obligation

The thesis — that healing from complex developmental trauma takes years — carries an ethical obligation. If the field knows that years of treatment are needed, and if the field knows that most affected individuals cannot access years of treatment, then silence on this gap is complicity with a structural injustice. The review does not claim to know how to close the gap. It claims that the gap must be named, that the naming is not optional, and that any clinical recommendation about therapy duration that omits the access question is incomplete.

Spring is right: the problem is not that survivors fail to heal. The problem is that society fails to provide the transport [spring-2020-what-does-recovery-look-like].
