# Response to accelerated and brief models

If the working thesis is "healing takes years," the strongest challenge comes from models that demonstrate deep change happening faster. Thirteen sources across five counterpoint domains — EMDR intensive protocols, pharmacologically assisted therapies, intensive delivery formats, experiential dynamic therapies, and critiques of the long-duration position itself — present evidence that cannot be dismissed. This section engages each model at its strongest, acknowledges where the evidence genuinely challenges the thesis, and identifies where the evidence runs out. The goal is not rebuttal but honest engagement: a practitioner trained in EMDR, AEDP, or ISTDP should recognise their model as fairly represented, even if they disagree with the conclusion.

The key move is endpoint divergence. As established in the synthesis, the field's apparent disagreement about duration largely dissolves once the endpoint level is specified. The counterpoint models overwhelmingly measure symptom reduction (loss of diagnosis, score changes on standardised instruments); the thesis concerns structural change (personality reorganisation, earned attachment security, mentalizing capacity). Both positions can be simultaneously correct about different things. But this framing must not become a device for defining the counterpoint out of existence. Where the evidence genuinely challenges the thesis — most notably in de Jongh's CPTSD data and the Helsinki convergence — it is acknowledged.

## EMDR intensive: the strongest counterpoint

The EMDR evidence for complex presentations represents the most direct challenge to the long-duration thesis, because it addresses a population that overlaps substantially with the one defined in this review.

**The strongest version of the argument.** De Jongh, Roos, and El-Leithy (2024) present two landmark findings. The Voorendonk et al. (2020) study offered 8 days of residential intensive treatment — combining EMDR, exposure, psychoeducation, and physical activity — to 308 PTSD patients, 66% of whom met ICD-11 CPTSD criteria. Eighty-eight percent of CPTSD patients no longer met diagnostic criteria after 8 days [dejongh-2024-emdr-science]. The van Vliet et al. (2021) RCT compared 16 sessions of standalone EMDR with EMDR preceded by 8 sessions of stabilisation (STAIR) in 121 adults with PTSD from early childhood trauma. No difference between conditions: stabilisation added nothing, and only 3% of those initially diagnosed with CPTSD retained the diagnosis [dejongh-2024-emdr-science]. A single-case study further demonstrated resolution of both CPTSD and BPD in 10 sessions over 5 weeks [dejongh-2023-emdr-case].

**Where this evidence genuinely challenges the thesis.** These data directly address the review's defined population — adults with PTSD from early childhood trauma presenting with CPTSD symptoms — and the results are striking. The stabilisation finding is equally important: it challenges the phase-based model that has dominated complex trauma treatment for three decades (Herman, 1992; Cloitre et al., 2012). If teaching emotion regulation skills before trauma processing adds nothing to outcomes, then some portion of what passes for long-term therapy may be spent on preparations that do not help. The long-duration position must account for this finding rather than explain it away.

**Where the evidence runs out.** Three limitations are genuine, not rhetorical. First, the 8-day study lacked a control condition and did not examine long-term effects; we do not know about relapse [dejongh-2024-emdr-science]. Second, "no longer meeting diagnostic criteria" is not equivalent to structural healing. The CPTSD diagnosis includes disturbances in self-organisation — affect dysregulation, negative self-concept, relationship difficulties — and losing the diagnosis implies improvement in these domains, but it is measured categorically (meets/does not meet criteria), not dimensionally. Whether personality integration, earned secure attachment, or the capacity for mutual recognition has occurred is not assessed. Third, and most importantly, Farina, Dimaggio, and Mosquera (2025) — experienced clinicians working with severe PTSD and personality disorders — respond to de Jongh's case: "such quick good outcomes cases are rare, and usually therapies are complex, difficult and time-consuming" [farina-2025-rapid-rare]. They raise the possibility that rapid responders may have "a different underlying structure, with more capacities for adaptive information processing and less avoidance" — suggesting that identical CPTSD diagnoses may mask fundamentally different structural depths [farina-2025-rapid-rare]. This maps directly onto the framework's Factor 2: patients with shallow structural injury respond rapidly; patients with deep structural disruption require more time, regardless of the modality employed.

**What the long-duration position can learn.** The EMDR literature forces precision about which components of long treatment are doing the work. If stabilisation exercises do not improve outcomes, then the case for extended treatment must rest on the therapeutic relationship, not on the stabilisation curriculum. The specific EMDR techniques for managing CPTSD complications — cognitive interweave for processing blocks, non-verbal processing for shame, normalisation of dissociation [dejongh-2023-emdr-case] — are clinically valuable regardless of treatment length.

## Pharmacologically assisted therapies: MDMA and ketamine

**The strongest version of the argument.** The Mitchell et al. (2021) Phase 3 RCT tested MDMA-assisted therapy for severe PTSD (n=90): three 8-hour MDMA sessions plus manualized therapy over 18 weeks. Sixty-seven percent of the MDMA group no longer met PTSD criteria, versus 32% placebo (d=0.91). Crucially, 87% had developmental trauma histories, and participants with the dissociative subtype responded equally well [mitchell-2021-mdma-phase3]. The theoretical claim is that MDMA pharmacologically creates the conditions — reduced fear, enhanced interpersonal trust, widened window of tolerance — that relational therapy builds over months or years. MDMA may "reopen an oxytocin-dependent critical period of neuroplasticity that typically closes after adolescence" [mitchell-2021-mdma-phase3], facilitating the processing of developmental fear memories in weeks rather than years.

Feder et al. (2025) tested a complementary approach: six ketamine infusions combined with five sessions of written exposure therapy for chronic PTSD (mean duration 18.1 years). Despite the tiny sample (n=13), the effect size was very large (d=1.9), with 54% losing their PTSD diagnosis at 12 weeks and 61.5% maintaining response at 6 months [feder-2025-ketamine-exposure]. The rationale — that ketamine "primes the brain" via enhanced neuroplasticity before psychotherapy — reframes the duration question: if the rate-limiter is neurobiological capacity for change, pharmacological enhancement may compress what the brain would otherwise require years to achieve.

Gorman et al. (2020) added a further dimension: posttraumatic growth (PTG), measured alongside symptom reduction in pooled Phase 2 MDMA data (n=60). The MDMA group showed large PTG effects (g=1.14) — positive changes in self-perception, interpersonal relationships, and philosophy of life [gorman-2020-mdma-ptg]. If MDMA facilitates genuine growth beyond symptom reduction, the endpoint divergence argument weakens, because PTG captures domains that overlap with what the long-duration position calls structural change.

**Where the evidence genuinely challenges the thesis.** The Mitchell trial's finding that dissociative patients responded equally well is particularly important, because dissociation is often cited as the feature that makes conventional treatment slow and difficult. If MDMA can bypass dissociative barriers pharmacologically, this challenges the assumption that dissociative defences require years of relational work to resolve.

**Where the evidence runs out.** The FDA rejected MDMA for PTSD in August 2024, citing functional unblinding, therapist misconduct in earlier trials, and insufficient evidence of sustained benefit [mitchell-2021-mdma-phase3]. The Gorman PTG data required a 2024 correction removing participants treated by therapist pairs involved in ethical violations [gorman-2020-mdma-ptg]. The Feder ketamine trial was open-label with no control group (n=13), and over half the participants were simultaneously receiving other psychotherapy [feder-2025-ketamine-exposure]. More fundamentally, the CAPS-5 "dissociative subtype" captures depersonalisation and derealisation — not the part-based structural dissociation (secondary and tertiary) described by Van der Hart, Nijenhuis, and Steele (2006) that characterises the most complex presentations. Both MDMA trials excluded DID and likely excluded current BPD, removing the most structurally disrupted patients before randomisation [mitchell-2021-mdma-phase3]. The PTG finding is intriguing but the PTGI is a self-report measure of perceived growth that has been criticised for capturing changed self-narrative rather than observable structural change, and PTG and PTSD reduction were not significantly correlated in the data [gorman-2020-mdma-ptg].

**What the long-duration position can learn.** The neuroplasticity hypothesis is important regardless of whether pharmacological enhancement becomes clinically available. If chronic PTSD involves a neurobiological rate-limit on change that ketamine or MDMA can temporarily lift, this suggests that part of what long-term therapy achieves may be the gradual accumulation of small neuroplastic events that pharmacological agents could theoretically concentrate. The question for long-term therapy becomes: what does the therapeutic relationship provide that MDMA provides pharmacologically? If the answer is felt safety plus neuroplasticity, and MDMA provides neuroplasticity directly, then the irreducible contribution of the relationship is the felt safety — which returns the argument to Factor 0.

## Intensive delivery formats: dose compression

**The strongest version of the argument.** Sciarrino, Warnecke, and Teng (2020) systematically reviewed 11 studies of empirically supported treatments delivered at intensive frequency (more than twice weekly). The weighted mean effect size was large (d=1.57), and the pooled dropout rate was only 5.51% — dramatically lower than the 18–39% reported for standard weekly delivery [sciarrino-2020-intensive-review]. This finding reframes the duration question: if intensive formats achieve equal or better outcomes with radically lower dropout, then some portion of what appears to be "long-term therapy" may reflect the inefficiency of standard weekly delivery rather than the inherent complexity of the healing process.

**Where the evidence genuinely challenges the thesis.** The dropout finding is devastating for the long-duration position as typically practiced. If 30–40% of trauma patients leave standard weekly therapy, the effective dose for those who drop out is zero. Intensive formats may ensure that patients actually receive a therapeutic dose. The suggestion that standard weekly therapy systematically fails a large proportion of patients through its delivery format — not through insufficient duration — deserves serious engagement.

**Where the evidence runs out.** Eighty-one percent of the included studies were uncontrolled [sciarrino-2020-intensive-review]. The populations were PTSD patients broadly defined; most studies did not include patients with comorbid personality disorders, severe dissociation, or suicidality. Self-selection is a concern: patients who commit to an intensive residential programme differ from those in standard care. And the outcome measures are uniformly symptom-based — whether dose compression works for structural change is unknown.

**What the long-duration position can learn.** Intensive formats may be particularly relevant for the safety prerequisite (Factor 0). The structured, contained environment of a residential intensive programme — where the patient is removed from stressors and immersed in a therapeutic context — may itself provide safety through structure rather than through extended relational time. This could explain why intensive formats work well for some CPTSD patients who would otherwise require months of stabilisation in outpatient settings. The long-duration position should consider whether hybrid models — intensive initial treatment to establish safety and begin processing, followed by extended relational therapy for structural consolidation — could serve patients better than either approach alone.

## Experiential dynamic therapies: the follow-up advantage

**The strongest version of the argument.** Lilliengren et al. (2025) present a meta-analysis of 57 RCTs (n=4,330) of experiential dynamic therapies — ISTDP, AEDP, Affect Phobia Therapy, and Emotional Awareness and Expression Therapy. Against inactive controls, EDTs show large effects (g=-0.96) that grow at follow-up (g=-1.11). Against active controls, effects are non-significant at post-treatment (g=-0.17) but become significant at follow-up (g=-0.40) [lilliengren-2025-edt-meta]. This follow-up advantage — the "sleeper effect" — is the key finding: EDTs may initiate a process during brief treatment that continues to produce change after therapy ends.

Fosha's AEDP contributes the theoretical mechanism. Her concept of "transformance" — the innate psychic force towards growth, positioned as the counterpart to resistance — and "meta-processing" — processing the experience of therapeutic change itself — propose that genuine transformation is accessible "from the first contact with the patient, including the most traumatised person" [fosha-2019-aedp-intimacy]. AEDP does not claim that healing completes rapidly; it claims that healing begins immediately when the therapist provides sufficient relational safety, rather than being deferred behind stabilisation phases.

Lilliengren et al. (2017) provide complementary data: ISTDP for GAD (n=215) produced significant symptom reduction in a mean of 8.3 sessions, with healthcare cost reductions sustained for 4 years [lilliengren-2017-istdp-gad]. Higher in-session mobilisation of unprocessed complex emotions predicted greater change — evidence that emotional breakthrough, not duration, may be the active ingredient.

**Where the evidence genuinely challenges the thesis.** The EDT follow-up advantage is genuinely important for the thesis, because it suggests that brief treatment can install something durable — not just borrowed skills that erode, but a process that grows. If this is confirmed for structural endpoints, it would narrow the thesis considerably. It is also compatible with the thesis: if brief EDT initiates a developmental process that continues for years, the elapsed-time factor (Factor 3) applies — the healing still takes years, but less of that time is spent in formal therapy.

**Where the evidence runs out.** The EDT meta-analysis covers mood, anxiety, personality, and somatic conditions — not complex developmental trauma with dissociative features [lilliengren-2025-edt-meta]. Heterogeneity is substantial (I2 > 75%). The ISTDP literature acknowledges that patients with "fragile character structure, somatization, and severe depression" require more treatment [lilliengren-2017-istdp-gad]. AEDP-specific outcome research remains nascent — Fosha references "about five research projects currently in the works" but these are not yet available [fosha-2019-aedp-intimacy]. The population gap is significant: the emotional mobilisation that EDT models rely on requires a capacity to access and tolerate intense affect — precisely the capacity that is impaired in complex developmental trauma with dissociative organisation.

## Paris's access argument: the ethical counterpoint

**The strongest version of the argument.** Paris (2020) makes the case that long-term therapy for personality disorders has never been shown to be superior to time-limited interventions, that most improvement occurs by 20 weeks with asymptotic flattening (citing Howard's 1986 dose-effect model), and that much of BPD recovery is naturalistic — the McLean longitudinal study shows gradual remission over 24 years regardless of treatment [paris-2020-access-pd]. His ethical argument is the most original contribution: at $220/week for DBT ($10,000+/year), long-term therapy is inaccessible to most personality disorder patients, who disproportionately have "limited education and low-level jobs." Clinicians have "an ethical responsibility to help as many people as possible, and doing so means keeping waiting lists short" [paris-2020-access-pd].

**Where the evidence genuinely challenges the thesis.** The access argument cannot be dismissed. If years of specialised therapy are necessary for structural healing, then the field bears responsibility for making this accessible. A recommendation that healing takes years, without a commitment to funding structures that match, is clinically honest but structurally unjust. Paris is right to place the burden of proof on those who advocate expensive, lengthy treatments.

**Where the evidence runs out.** Paris conflates BPD with the population defined in this review. His evidence is primarily about BPD symptom reduction, not structural change in dissociative organisation. The dose-effect curve (Howard, 1986) was derived from predominantly neurotic populations [paris-2020-access-pd]. The stepped-care model he advocates may work for BPD symptom management, but the question of whether it addresses structural dissociation — the deeper injury that characterises the most severely affected subset of the population — is not engaged. His framing of the endpoint as symptom reduction rather than structural personality change means his "diminishing returns" argument applies to a narrower definition of healing than the one this thesis proposes. Paris does not discuss what happens to patients who do not respond to brief treatment and cannot access the longer treatment his stepped-care model recommends for non-responders.

## Farina's rapid-rare framework and Tilson's evidence critique

Two sources complicate the picture from different angles. Farina et al. (2025) represent the clinical middle ground: experienced trauma clinicians who acknowledge that rapid outcomes occur but insist they are "rare" in severe CPTSD with personality disorder comorbidity [farina-2025-rapid-rare]. Their key contribution is the hypothesis that similar diagnostic presentations mask "different underlying structures" — some patients carry the CPTSD label with relatively preserved adaptive capacity, while others carry it with pervasive developmental disruption. This maps precisely onto the framework's Factor 2 (structural depth) and explains why the same treatment can produce rapid change in one patient and require years in another.

Tilson (2025) mounts a different challenge: not against the duration claim per se, but against the neuroscientific scaffolding frequently cited to explain why healing takes time. Her critique of the triune brain model, polyvagal theory, and unreliable fMRI evidence dismantles specific frameworks invoked by van der Kolk, Porges, and others [tilson-2025-body-not-keeping-score]. If the neurobiological rationale for slow developmental change rests on debunked science, the explanatory mechanism weakens. However, the clinical convergence holds independently: nine independent theoretical traditions observe slow structural change without depending on the triune brain model for their observations. Tilson's critique also overreaches: she conflates van der Kolk's popular science with the entire body-based therapy field, and her endorsement of CBT and Prolonged Exposure as alternatives does not address whether these approaches achieve structural change in complex developmental trauma populations or merely symptom reduction — the same endpoint divergence that limits the other counterpoint models.

## Resolution: the four-factor framework

The apparent contradiction between these counterpoint models and the long-duration thesis resolves through the four-factor framework proposed in the synthesis.

The counterpoint evidence is strongest at Level 1 (symptom reduction) and weakest at Level 3 (structural change). EMDR produces diagnostic remission in 8 days; MDMA eliminates PTSD diagnosis in 18 weeks; intensive formats achieve d=1.57 in 1–3 weeks. These are real achievements with real clinical value. But none of these models measure personality reorganisation, earned attachment security, or integration of dissociated self-states — the endpoints that define structural healing for patients with deep developmental injury.

The resolution is not "the counterpoint is wrong" but "the counterpoint is right about a different question." The models reviewed here answer "how long does symptom reduction take?" with precision and rigour. The thesis answers "how long does structural change take?" with convergent but less rigorous evidence. Both answers matter. For a patient whose primary need is diagnostic remission — a reduction in flashbacks, hyperarousal, and avoidance that allows them to function — concentrated evidence-based treatment may be entirely sufficient, and recommending years of therapy would be harmful. For a patient whose primary need is to reorganise a personality structure built around dissociation, terror of intimacy, and the absence of a coherent self — the patient who can pass a symptom inventory while remaining unable to sustain a loving relationship — symptom reduction is necessary but radically insufficient.

Two findings resist this neat resolution and must be acknowledged honestly. First, de Jongh's CPTSD data. If 88% of CPTSD patients lose their diagnosis in 8 days, and CPTSD includes disturbances in self-organisation (not just core PTSD symptoms), then the endpoint divergence argument may be too clean. CPTSD diagnostic remission is not mere symptom reduction — it implies improvement in affect regulation, self-concept, and relational functioning. The counterargument is that the ITQ measures these domains categorically and briefly, and that falling below diagnostic threshold is not the same as achieving integration. But the honest position is that this remains an empirical question. What the field needs — and does not have — is a study that measures both diagnostic remission and structural endpoints (personality organisation, attachment security, dissociative integration) in the same CPTSD population, at the same time points, comparing brief and extended treatments. Until that study exists, the endpoint divergence argument is a hypothesis, not a conclusion.

Second, the Helsinki convergence. Psychoanalysis (646 sessions) and long-term psychodynamic therapy (232 sessions) produced equivalent outcomes at 10-year follow-up [lilliengren-2025-edt-meta]. If tripling the dose does not triple the outcome, then the thesis must specify where the threshold lies — how many sessions are "enough" for structural change to become self-sustaining, and when additional therapy represents diminishing returns rather than deeper healing. The elapsed-time factor (Factor 3) provides a partial answer: beyond a sufficient therapeutic dose, the patient needs life-time rather than therapy-time for installed capacity to develop. But the framework does not yet specify what that sufficient dose is.

A further finding complicates both positions in the duration debate. The counterpoint argues that less treatment can be sufficient; the long-duration position implies that more treatment is generally better. But the Bateman and Fonagy (2008) TAU data demonstrate that more treatment without a coherent developmental model produced worse outcomes: TAU patients received significantly more services during follow-up yet 87% still met BPD criteria, compared with 13% in the MBT group [bateman-fonagy-2008-mbt-8yr]. The authors concluded that "quantity of treatment may not be a good indicator of improvement and may even prevent patients from taking advantage of felicitous social and interpersonal events." The implication is that the duration debate should not be framed as "more versus less" but as "coherent versus incoherent." A well-designed brief intervention with a clear developmental model may outperform years of unfocused treatment. This does not weaken the case for long-term therapy — it specifies that long-term therapy must be purposeful, developmentally informed, and structured around a model of change, not simply more of the same.

These are genuine limits, not rhetorical concessions. The thesis survives the counterpoint evidence, but it is refined by it — sharpened, qualified, and made more precise about who needs what, for how long, and measured by which endpoint.
