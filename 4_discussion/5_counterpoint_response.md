# Response to accelerated and brief models

## The problem this section solves

If the working thesis is "healing takes years," the strongest challenge comes from models that claim deep change can happen faster. If this section merely rebuts those models, the review fails the CIS standard of honest engagement and the "honest advocacy" stance collapses. If it ignores them, it is intellectually dishonest.

This section must pass a fairness test: a practitioner trained in AEDP or ISTDP should read it and feel that their model was represented accurately and charitably, even if they disagree with the conclusion.

## Structure for each model

*To be populated from Layer 3 source annotations. For each model, address:*

### 1. What is the strongest version of this model's argument?
Represent the model at its best — not a caricature, not the weakest study, but the core theoretical claim and the strongest supporting evidence.

### 2. Under what conditions could this model be right?
For which subpopulation, with which definition of healing, at which level of change (symptom reduction / functional improvement / structural change) does the evidence support faster change?

### 3. What would it mean for our thesis if this model is right?
If AEDP can produce deep structural change in complex developmental trauma in 2 years, what does that do to the argument? Does it invalidate it, narrow it, or refine it?

### 4. What can the long-duration position learn from this model?
Even if the model doesn't fully apply to the population in this review, does it reveal something about the mechanisms of change that the long-duration tradition has missed? (e.g., AEDP's emphasis on positive affect as a change mechanism; ISTDP's focus on breaking through defenses rather than slowly working around them.)

### 5. Where are the genuine limits of applicability?
Not "where does it break down" (prosecutorial) but "where does the evidence run out?" — which population was actually studied, what definition of change was used, how long was follow-up, and what would we need to see to be convinced?

## How we mitigated the risk of dismissal

1. **Dedicated layer (Layer 3)** with its own source budget (5 per model, up to 8)
2. **Steel-man annotation framework** in `2_methods/5_layer_3_counterpoint.md` — 7 questions per model, including "what can the long-duration position learn?"
3. **This discussion section** structured around fairness, not rebuttal
4. **The dose vs. elapsed time limitation** (§3) directly engages with the strongest counterpoint: that intensive formats may compress calendar duration

## What remains unmitigated

- The reviewer's theoretical orientation (to be declared in the reflexivity section) creates a structural bias toward long-duration frameworks. This cannot be eliminated, only acknowledged.
- The population definition may exclude cases where accelerated models genuinely work — if we define the population as "the people for whom it takes years," the counterpoint cannot win by definition. This circularity must be addressed.
- Direct head-to-head comparisons between long-term and accelerated approaches for this specific population do not exist. The counterpoint must be engaged through indirect evidence.
