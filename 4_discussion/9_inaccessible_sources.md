# Inaccessible sources and their impact

## Scale of the problem

Of 89 sources included in this review, four remain fully unresolved — identified as directly relevant but not accessed or only partially accessed at the time of writing. An additional 11 sources were initially inaccessible but later obtained through manual provision, and approximately 10 sources are annotated from abstracts, secondary citations, or partial excerpts only (marked `abstract-only` in the source index). The barrier in most cases is paywall access or the need to obtain a physical book.

The four unresolved sources are all in Layer 1 (academic and clinical literature), concentrating the gap in the review's theoretical foundation:

1. **Yeates et al. (2023), "A systematic review and narrative analysis of the evidence for individual psychodynamically informed psychotherapy for dissociative disorders"** [yeates-2023-psychodynamic-dd]. Paywalled. This source sits at the precise intersection of the review's population (dissociative disorders) and a key modality (psychodynamic). It may contain duration data across multiple reviewed studies that would directly strengthen or qualify the review's claims about psychodynamic treatment length for dissociative presentations.

2. **Freixo Ferreira, Guerra, & Vieira-Coelho (2023), "Predictors of psychotherapy dropout in patients with borderline personality disorder: A systematic review"** [freixo-ferreira-2023-bpd-dropout]. Annotated from abstract and Scholar Gateway excerpts; full text behind paywall. The abstract confirms the treatment-engagement paradox (weaker therapeutic alliance and higher hostility predict dropout) but the full text would provide dropout rate numbers that the survivorship bias discussion currently lacks. Without these numbers, the claim that "the people who most need long-term treatment are systematically missing from the evidence" is well-supported qualitatively but poorly quantified.

3. **Herman (1992), *Trauma and Recovery*** [herman-1992-trauma-recovery]. Book not obtained. This is the foundational text establishing the three-phase model (safety, remembrance and mourning, reconnection) on which all subsequent phase-based treatment literature builds. The review currently relies on secondary descriptions of Herman's model — through Steele et al. (2005), Fisher (2021), the Blue Knot Foundation, and multiple client narratives that reference the phase model. The primary text would provide original quotes, the nuances of Herman's original framework (particularly the duration of Phase 1 for chronic trauma, which she reportedly describes as "months to years"), and any qualifications that secondary citations may have dropped.

4. **Van der Hart, Nijenhuis, & Steele (2006), *The Haunted Self: Structural Dissociation and the Treatment of Chronic Traumatization*** [vanderhart-2006-haunted-self]. Book not obtained. The comprehensive theoretical framework for structural dissociation and its treatment. Part III covers treatment in detail. The review relies on this framework extensively — the structural dissociation model is central to the "structural depth" factor — but through secondary sources (Steele et al., 2005; Steele et al., 2001; Griffiths, 2025). The primary text would provide the full theoretical rationale for why complex trauma treatment is necessarily long-term.

A fifth source merits mention: **Farina, Dimaggio, & Mosquera (2025), "Common Elements and Differences Among Treatment Approaches to Complex Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder"** [farina-2025-rapid-rare]. This was accessible only through Scholar Gateway abstract and excerpts. The "rapid-rare" observation — that quick good outcomes in CPTSD cases are rare and that typical cases are "complex, difficult and time-consuming" — is reconstructed from these excerpts rather than from full-text reading. The synthesis relies on this observation to support the structural depth factor (Factor 2), but the supporting detail and any qualifications in the full text are unavailable.

## Which layers are most affected

The inaccessible sources are concentrated in Layer 1a (trauma-clinical), which is also the review's theoretical backbone. Three of the four unresolved sources are Layer 1a texts; the fourth (Yeates) is also Layer 1a. This means the layer that provides the foundational treatment models — the phase model, the structural dissociation framework, and the clinical rationale for long-term treatment — is the layer most affected by access gaps.

Layers 2 through 6 are comparatively well-covered. All 11 Layer 2 sources (naturalistic studies) are now accessible, most through manual provision. All Layer 3 (counterpoint), Layer 4 (practitioner), Layer 5 (client narrative), and Layer 6 (memoir) sources are fully accessible. The access problem is concentrated in the theoretical literature, not in the empirical or experiential evidence.

This concentration has a specific consequence: the review's empirical findings (endpoint-duration nexus, sleeper effect, safety prerequisite, trajectory shapes) are well-grounded in accessible sources, but the theoretical framework explaining WHY these patterns exist — the phase model, structural dissociation, and the developmental mechanism — relies partly on secondary descriptions of foundational texts.

## Impact on specific claims

The review's central claims and their vulnerability to the inaccessible source gap:

**"The phase model is universally endorsed."** This claim would be substantially strengthened by primary access to Herman (1992), which originated the model. Currently, the claim rests on secondary endorsements from Steele et al. (2005), Fisher (2021), the Blue Knot Foundation (84% expert consensus), Griffiths (2025), and multiple client narratives. The convergence is strong enough that access to the primary text is unlikely to change the claim — but it would provide the original formulation and any caveats Herman attached.

**"Safety establishment takes months to years for chronic trauma."** This specific duration claim is attributed to Herman but is known only through secondary citation. If the primary text qualifies this estimate differently — for example, by specifying that Phase 1 duration depends on specific conditions — the review's synthesis would need to incorporate that nuance.

**"Structural depth determines treatment duration."** This claim relies on the Van der Hart et al. (2006) structural dissociation model, accessed only through secondary sources. The primary text's treatment section (Part III) likely contains specific duration estimates or clinical guidelines that would either support or complicate the review's Factor 2. The Farina (2025) "rapid-rare" observation, which supports this claim from the counterpoint direction, is similarly incomplete.

**"The treatment-engagement paradox is devastating for duration claims."** The dropout discussion cites Freixo Ferreira et al. (2023) for the paradox that the traits defining the population (hostility, alliance problems) predict dropout. Full-text access would provide the systematic review's quantitative findings — pooled dropout rates, effect sizes for predictors — that would give this claim empirical specificity rather than remaining at the level of conceptual argument.

## Could the missing sources change the conclusions?

The direction of impact is assessable even without reading the sources. Herman (1992) and Van der Hart et al. (2006) are foundational texts in the long-term treatment tradition. Their full texts would almost certainly strengthen rather than challenge the review's thesis. The risk is not that they would undermine the argument but that secondary citation has oversimplified their positions — dropping qualifications, overstating certainty, or losing nuance about when long-term treatment is and is not indicated.

Yeates et al. (2023) and Freixo Ferreira et al. (2023) are systematic reviews that could go either way. Yeates might find that psychodynamic treatment for dissociative disorders shows weaker evidence than the review assumes, or that duration data across reviewed studies suggests shorter or longer timelines than predicted. Freixo Ferreira might quantify dropout rates in ways that either sharpen or soften the survivorship bias argument.

Farina et al. (2025) is the most consequential partial access: the "rapid-rare" observation is central to the structural depth factor, and any qualifications in the full text could modify how the review handles the counterpoint position.

The honest assessment is that the missing sources are unlikely to overturn the review's central findings — the endpoint-duration nexus, the safety prerequisite, and the developmental mechanism are each supported by multiple fully accessible sources across multiple layers. But they could modify the precision and confidence of specific claims, particularly regarding the phase model's original formulation, the structural depth framework's clinical specificity, and the quantitative evidence for the treatment-engagement paradox. Obtaining Herman (1992), Van der Hart et al. (2006), and the Farina (2025) full text should be the highest-priority next steps for strengthening the review's evidence base.
