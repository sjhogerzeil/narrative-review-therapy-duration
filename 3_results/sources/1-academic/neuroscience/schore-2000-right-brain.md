## Summary

Theoretical review arguing that attachment theory is fundamentally a regulatory theory. Schore revisits Bowlby's original charting of the attachment landscape and updates it with neuroscience from the "decade of the brain." The central claim is that the orbitofrontal cortex — the "senior executive of the emotional brain" — is the biological control system Bowlby described, that it is experience-dependent in its maturation, that it is expanded in the right hemisphere, and that early relational trauma damages this system in ways that produce enduring affect dysregulation. The paper synthesizes developmental neuroscience, attachment research, and psychoanalytic theory to argue that attachment experiences directly shape brain structure, particularly the right-hemispheric frontolimbic system that mediates affect regulation. The therapeutic implication — never stated as a session count but pervasive throughout — is that repairing these structures requires the same kind of sustained, attuned relational experience that built them in the first place: experience-dependent maturation cannot be compressed.

## Key claims
- The orbitofrontal cortex is the "senior executive of the emotional brain" and the biological substrate of Bowlby's attachment control system — p. 29
- Attachment theory is fundamentally a regulatory theory: "the interactive regulation of biological synchronicity between organisms" — p. 34
- The right hemisphere is dominant in preverbal human infants and is specifically impacted by early social experiences — p. 32
- Attachment experiences are imprinted into implicit-procedural memory in the right cortex, the hemisphere dominant for implicit learning — p. 32
- Internal working models of attachment are stored in implicit-procedural memory and are processed by the orbitofrontal system — they encode strategies of affect regulation — p. 32–33
- The orbitofrontal system is experience-dependent in its maturation: face-to-face transactions of affect synchrony between caregiver and infant directly influence the circuit wiring of this corticolimbic area — p. 30
- Early relational environments that engender insecure attachments "inhibit the growth of this control system" and preclude its adaptive coping function — p. 35
- The right hemisphere "subsequently re-enters into growth spurts" beyond infancy, suggesting neurobiological reorganizations of the attachment system and their functional correlates can be explored throughout development — p. 39
- Developmentally-based, affectively-focused psychotherapy can alter early attachment patterns: "certain attachment patterns can change from 'insecurity' to 'earned security'" — p. 39
- Higher activation of specifically the right prefrontal cortex attenuates emotional responses "at the most basic levels in the brain" — this modulating process is "fundamental to most modern psychotherapeutic methods" — p. 39

## Duration data
- No explicit duration data (session counts, treatment length, timelines). This is a theoretical paper.
- The implicit duration argument is structural: if the orbitofrontal system was shaped by years of early relational experience, and if repair requires experience-dependent maturation of the same system, then repair must also unfold over extended relational experience. The paper provides the mechanism, not the timeline.
- The critical period for orbitofrontal maturation is identified as 10–18 months (p. 30), but Schore notes that the right hemisphere re-enters growth spurts later in development (p. 39) — suggesting plasticity windows remain open, though narrower.

## Trajectory description

The paper does not describe a therapeutic trajectory directly. However, it implies a trajectory through its developmental model: attachment experiences build the orbitofrontal regulatory system from the bottom up, through repeated cycles of attunement, disruption, and interactive repair. The mother "also regulates moments of asynchrony" (p. 34) and the principle of synchrony following stress provides "the mechanism of interactive repair" (p. 35). This maps onto a therapeutic trajectory of repeated relational experiences — not a single corrective emotional experience but an accumulation of regulatory interactions that gradually reshape the neural substrate of affect regulation. The trajectory is inherently nonlinear (repair follows disruption) and inherently extended (neural rewiring is incremental).

## Relevance to thesis

This paper provides the neurobiological mechanism for WHY therapeutic change in complex trauma is slow. It does not answer HOW LONG treatment takes — it explains why it cannot be brief. The argument has three levels: (1) affect regulation is mediated by a specific brain system (orbitofrontal cortex); (2) this system is shaped by early relational experience through experience-dependent maturation; (3) therefore, repairing this system requires sustained relational experience of the same kind — attuned, affect-regulating, right-brain-to-right-brain interaction. The paper's concept of experience-dependent maturation is the neurobiological translation of the clinical observation that complex trauma patients need extended treatment: you cannot install firmware through a single software update.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No data. Not addressed directly. However, the model of interactive repair (synchrony → disruption → re-synchrony) implies that therapeutic rupture and repair is part of the process, not an interruption of it. This suggests continuity of the relationship matters more than continuity of sessions.

### One modality or many?
Not addressed. The paper is modality-agnostic in its therapeutic implications — it argues that any "developmentally-based, affectively-focused psychotherapy" (p. 39) that works at the right-brain level can facilitate the experience-dependent maturation process.

### One therapist or several?
Implicit support for single-therapist continuity. The paper emphasises that the orbitofrontal system develops through sustained dyadic interaction with a psychobiologically attuned caregiver. The therapeutic analogue would be a consistent therapist who functions as a regulatory other. Transferring to a new therapist would presumably require re-establishing the right-brain-to-right-brain attunement from scratch.

### Session frequency?
No data. The developmental analogy suggests frequent interaction matters — the infant-caregiver dyad engages in face-to-face regulatory transactions daily. However, the paper does not address how this translates to psychotherapy session frequency.

### Phase-based or organic?
Not addressed. The developmental model is sequential (brainstem → limbic → cortical maturation), but the paper does not map this onto therapeutic phases.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
No data. Not addressed.

## Quality notes

This is a highly influential theoretical review paper by the leading scholar of right-brain attachment neuroscience. Its strength is the integration of multiple research streams — Bowlby's attachment theory, developmental neuroscience, neuroanatomy, affect regulation theory — into a coherent model with clear therapeutic implications. The argument is rigorous within its theoretical framework and well-supported by the neurobiological evidence cited.

However, several limitations are relevant. First, this is a theoretical synthesis, not an empirical study — there are no data, no subjects, no measurements. The therapeutic implications are inferred from developmental neuroscience, not tested in clinical populations. Second, the paper is from 2000 and some of the neuroscience has been refined or revised since then (though the core framework has held up remarkably well). Third, the translation from developmental neurobiology to psychotherapy is a large inferential leap — the fact that the orbitofrontal system is experience-dependent in infancy does not automatically mean that adult psychotherapy can achieve the same kind of structural rewiring. Fourth, the paper is exclusively about mechanism — it explains WHY change is slow but provides no data on HOW LONG it takes. For our review, this paper provides theoretical scaffolding, not empirical evidence for treatment duration.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Not applicable — this is a theoretical paper, not an outcome study.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The paper's concept of experience-dependent maturation implicitly suggests that elapsed time matters more than session count, because neural maturation requires both repeated experience AND biological time for consolidation. This is an important theoretical contribution to the dose-vs-duration question, even though it provides no empirical data.

### Therapy vs. life
The paper's model suggests that any attuned relational experience — not just therapy — can contribute to right-brain regulatory development. The orbitofrontal system is shaped by "the early social environment" broadly, not specifically by therapeutic interventions. This implies that therapy is one context for regulatory repair, but not the only one. The paper does not discuss this implication, but it follows logically from the experience-dependent maturation framework.

No data. Not addressed.

### Definition of endpoint
The implicit endpoint is structural: a well-functioning orbitofrontal regulatory system that can flexibly shift between interactive regulation (with others) and autoregulation (alone), and that encodes secure internal working models in implicit-procedural memory. This is a neurobiological definition of "earned security" — a structural change, not symptom reduction.
