## Summary

Theoretical review applying the Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience (SCAN) framework to complex PTSD, arguing that cPTSD involves disruption across multiple neural systems, not just the fear-circuitry model of simple PTSD. Lanius and colleagues identify four dimensions of neurocognitive disruption in cPTSD: (1) emotion regulation (undermodulation vs. overmodulation), (2) self-referential processing (disrupted default mode network function), (3) social cognition (altered theory of mind, empathy, and interpersonal processing), and (4) consciousness and awareness (dissociation, depersonalisation, derealisation). The central clinical argument is that emotion dysregulation in cPTSD presents in two distinct neurobiological subtypes: undermodulation (hyperarousal, emotional flooding, amygdala hyperreactivity with insufficient prefrontal control) and overmodulation (hypoarousal, emotional numbing, excessive prefrontal suppression of limbic activation, dissociative responses). These two subtypes require different therapeutic approaches: undermodulation patients need grounding and affect regulation before trauma processing; overmodulation patients need activation and re-engagement with emotional experience. The multi-system nature of the disruption implies that treatment must be comprehensive, sequential, and extended — addressing each system in turn.

## Key claims
- Complex PTSD involves disruption across four neural-system dimensions: emotion regulation, self-referential processing, social cognition, and consciousness/awareness — §SCAN framework
- Emotion dysregulation presents in two neurobiological subtypes: undermodulation (amygdala hyperreactivity, insufficient prefrontal control → emotional flooding, hyperarousal) and overmodulation (excessive prefrontal suppression → emotional numbing, dissociation, hypoarousal) — §Emotion regulation
- Approximately 70% of PTSD patients show the undermodulation (re-experiencing/hyperarousal) pattern; approximately 30% show the overmodulation (dissociative/numbing) pattern — Lanius body of work
- The default mode network (DMN) — involved in self-referential processing, autobiographical memory, and mind-wandering — is disrupted in cPTSD, contributing to altered sense of self, fragmented autobiographical narrative, and difficulties with mentalisation — §Self-referential processing
- Dissociation represents a neurobiological phenomenon (not merely psychological avoidance): it involves measurable changes in brain activation patterns, with overmodulation of emotion and detachment from bodily experience — §Consciousness and awareness
- Social cognition is disrupted: theory of mind, empathic accuracy, and interpersonal trust are all affected, creating difficulties with therapeutic alliance itself — §Social cognition
- Treatment must be matched to the neurobiological subtype: undermodulated patients benefit from initial stabilisation and affect regulation; overmodulated patients benefit from approaches that increase emotional engagement and reduce dissociative avoidance — §Clinical implications
- The multi-system nature of disruption means that single-system treatments (e.g., exposure therapy targeting only fear conditioning) are insufficient for cPTSD — §Clinical implications

## Trajectory description

The SCAN framework implies a treatment trajectory that addresses each disrupted neural system, likely in a sequence constrained by neurobiological necessity. Emotion regulation (undermodulation or overmodulation) must be addressed first — this corresponds to the stabilisation phase. Self-referential processing and consciousness/awareness issues must be addressed concurrently or next — enabling the patient to maintain a coherent sense of self during trauma processing. Social cognition must be functional enough to sustain the therapeutic alliance. Only with these systems adequately functioning can trauma processing (exposure, narrative integration) proceed safely. The trajectory is inherently multi-phase and multi-system, with the two subtypes (undermodulation vs. overmodulation) requiring different entry points and different sequences.

## Relevance to thesis

This paper provides the neural-systems argument for WHY complex PTSD requires longer treatment than simple PTSD. The key insight is the multi-system nature of the disruption: cPTSD is not "more PTSD" but a qualitatively different condition involving four distinct neural-system disruptions. Standard trauma treatments (prolonged exposure, CPT) were developed for the fear-conditioning model of simple PTSD — they address one system (emotion regulation, specifically undermodulation) but may not address the other three (self-referential processing, social cognition, consciousness/awareness). The subtype distinction (undermodulation vs. overmodulation) adds further complexity: the 30% of patients with the dissociative subtype may be actively harmed by exposure-based treatments that increase emotional activation when their system is already overmodulating.

For our review, this paper explains WHY treatment cannot be brief for cPTSD — there are too many systems to address, in a sequence constrained by neurobiological necessity. It does not specify HOW LONG treatment takes.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No data.

### One modality or many?
Strongly supports multi-modal treatment. The four neural-system dimensions may each respond to different therapeutic approaches: emotion regulation to skills-based and body-oriented therapies; self-referential processing to narrative and mentalisation-based approaches; social cognition to relational/interpersonal therapy; consciousness/awareness to grounding and integration techniques. No single modality addresses all four dimensions.

### One therapist or several?
No data. However, the social cognition disruption (altered trust, empathy, theory of mind) implies that therapeutic alliance is itself affected by the condition being treated — establishing trust with even one therapist is a therapeutic achievement for this population, making multiple-therapist transitions potentially costly.

### Session frequency?
No data.

### Phase-based or organic?
Strongly supports phase-based treatment. The neurobiological constraints are clear: emotion regulation must be functional before trauma processing can proceed safely. The two subtypes require different phase-one interventions (grounding for undermodulation; activation for overmodulation). This provides neuroscience-based support for the clinical consensus on phase-based treatment (cf. Herman 1992, Van der Hart 2006, Steele 2005).

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Implicitly supports adjuncts. The body-based dimension (interoception, somatic awareness, dissociative detachment from body) suggests that somatic therapies (sensorimotor psychotherapy, somatic experiencing, yoga) may address neural systems that talk therapy alone cannot reach. Pharmacological support for emotion regulation (especially for the undermodulation subtype) is also implied.

## Quality notes

Lanius is one of the leading neuroimaging researchers in PTSD/cPTSD, and this review represents a sophisticated integration of neuroscience findings with clinical implications. The SCAN framework provides a clinically useful organisation of the neural-systems evidence. The undermodulation/overmodulation distinction has been empirically validated through neuroimaging studies and has been incorporated into the DSM-5 PTSD dissociative subtype.

Limitations for our review: (1) This annotation is based on the abstract and Lanius group publications, not the full text — key claims should be verified. (2) The paper is theoretical — no treatment outcome data. (3) The four-system SCAN framework, while clinically useful, may oversimplify the complexity of neural disruption in cPTSD. (4) The treatment implications (sequential, multi-system) are inferred from the neuroscience, not tested in clinical trials. (5) The paper focuses on mechanism — it does not address treatment duration or timeline.

For our review, this is a mechanism paper. Its role is to explain WHY cPTSD requires multi-system, phased treatment (which inherently takes longer) — not to provide evidence for specific treatment durations.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Not applicable — theoretical review, not treatment outcome study.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The multi-system model implies that dose requirements are multiplicative rather than additive: each neural system requires its own course of targeted intervention. This means that the total treatment dose for cPTSD is not "more sessions of the same thing" but "adequate sessions for each of several different therapeutic targets" — a fundamentally different dose model than the simple dose-effect curve.

### Therapy vs. life
Not addressed directly. The neural-systems framework is therapy-centric — it frames the disruptions in terms of what therapy must address. However, many of the disrupted functions (social cognition, self-referential processing, emotion regulation) are exercised continuously in daily life, and life experiences may contribute to recovery or deterioration independently of therapy.

### Access and context
Not addressed. However, the multi-system, multi-modal treatment model implied by the SCAN framework would be expensive and require highly specialised clinicians — raising significant access concerns for a population that is disproportionately affected by socioeconomic disadvantage.

### Definition of endpoint
The implicit endpoint is restoration of function across all four neural-system dimensions: emotion regulation capacity (flexible modulation rather than under- or overmodulation), coherent self-referential processing, functional social cognition, and integrated consciousness without pathological dissociation. This is a multi-dimensional structural endpoint, far more demanding than simple symptom reduction.
