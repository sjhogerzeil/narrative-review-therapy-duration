## Summary

Comprehensive theoretical review defining intersubjectivity as "rapid, reciprocal, bidirectional right brain-to-right brain visual-facial, auditory-prosodic, and tactile-gestural positively valenced nonverbal communications." Schore integrates recent hyperscanning studies (fNIRS) with his longstanding right-brain attachment model to argue that therapeutic change operates through a "two-person unconscious" — where the relational unconscious of therapist and patient communicate beneath conscious awareness. The right temporoparietal junction (TPJ) is identified as a central hub for detecting socially salient stimuli in face-to-face interaction. Hyperscanning research demonstrates measurable interbrain synchronization between therapist and client, with better working alliances corresponding to tighter neural coupling specifically in the right TPJ. The paper argues that this implicit, nonconscious processing — operating on timescales of fractions of a second — is how therapeutic change accumulates: through moment-to-moment right-brain attunement that gradually reshapes the patient's implicit relational knowing. No explicit treatment duration data is provided, but the framework inherently implies that this accumulation is slow and cannot be rushed.

## Key claims
- Intersubjectivity is defined as "rapid, reciprocal, bidirectional right brain-to-right brain visual-facial, auditory-prosodic, and tactile-gestural positively valenced nonverbal communications" — §Introduction
- The right temporoparietal junction (TPJ) is a central node integrating multisensory (visual, auditory, tactile) information for detecting socially salient and unexpected stimuli in face-to-face interactions — §Right TPJ section
- Hyperscanning studies (Zhang et al., 2018) demonstrate that "better working alliances and increased interbrain synchronization in specifically the right temporo-parietal junction between clients and counselors" emerge during first sessions — §Hyperscanning evidence
- "The more tightly the client and counselor's brains are coupled, the better the alliance" — §Hyperscanning evidence
- Dumas et al. (2010) documented millisecond-scale synchronization of right centroparietal regions during nonverbal social interactions — §Hyperscanning evidence
- Therapeutic change operates through implicit, nonconscious processing: the empathic therapist shifts from left-hemisphere language processing to right-hemisphere emotional reception — §Therapeutic implications
- These moment-to-moment communications operate "in the present moment, a time frame of fractions of a second to 2–3 s" — §Temporal dynamics
- Implicit processing is "repetitive, automatic, provides quick categorization and decision-making, and operates outside the realm of focal attention" (citing Lyons-Ruth) — §Implicit processing

## Duration data
- No explicit duration data (no session counts, no treatment length, no timelines).
- The implicit duration argument is processual: if therapeutic change operates through moment-to-moment right-brain synchronization that gradually reshapes implicit relational knowing, then the process requires many such moments across many sessions. Each individual moment operates on fractions-of-seconds timescales, but the accumulation of enough moments to restructure implicit procedural memory requires extended treatment.

## Trajectory description

The paper does not describe a therapeutic trajectory in terms of phases or timelines. It describes the micro-process: moment-to-moment right-brain-to-right-brain attunement that operates beneath conscious awareness, on timescales of fractions of a second to 2–3 seconds. Accumulated across sessions, these micro-moments gradually reshape the patient's implicit relational knowing — the nonconscious procedural models of what to expect from relationships. The trajectory is one of incremental accumulation rather than dramatic shifts: each session adds another layer of attunement experience to the patient's implicit relational repertoire. This is fundamentally a slow process because it operates at the level of procedural memory (which learns through repetition, not insight) and because it is implicit (not available to conscious acceleration through cognitive effort).

## Relevance to thesis

This paper provides the micro-process mechanism for WHY therapeutic change accumulates slowly. Where Schore (2000) identified the brain system (orbitofrontal cortex) and the developmental process (experience-dependent maturation), this paper describes the session-level mechanism: right-brain-to-right-brain synchronization that operates implicitly and requires repetition to reshape procedural memory. The hyperscanning evidence — showing measurable neural coupling between therapist and client that correlates with alliance quality — provides empirical support for the clinical intuition that therapeutic relationship is the vehicle of change. For our review, this paper explains why treatment cannot be compressed: implicit relational knowing changes through accumulation of many micro-moments of attunement, not through insight or technique. It explains mechanism, not duration.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No data. Not addressed directly. However, the model of gradual accumulation of implicit relational knowing suggests that continuity matters: breaks in treatment would not erase what has been accumulated (procedural memory is robust), but they would pause the accumulation process. Extended breaks might allow some regression of the newly developing patterns before they are consolidated.

### One modality or many?
Not addressed. The framework is modality-agnostic — any approach that facilitates right-brain-to-right-brain attunement can potentially achieve the implicit relational change described.

### One therapist or several?
Implicit support for single-therapist continuity. The neural coupling demonstrated in hyperscanning studies develops within specific dyads. A new therapist would require establishing new right-brain attunement from scratch. The paper does not address this directly, but the dyad-specific nature of the neural synchronization suggests that therapeutic continuity with one therapist allows deeper accumulation of implicit relational knowing.

### Session frequency?
No data. However, the accumulation model suggests that more frequent sessions would provide more opportunities for right-brain-to-right-brain attunement, potentially accelerating the accumulation process. Conversely, if consolidation between sessions is important (as procedural memory consolidation research suggests), then some spacing may be necessary.

### Phase-based or organic?
Not addressed. The accumulation model is inherently organic and continuous rather than phase-based — there is no theoretical reason to expect discrete phase transitions in the accumulation of implicit relational knowing.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
No data. The framework implies that any relational experience that engages right-brain-to-right-brain attunement could contribute to the accumulation process — not only formal therapy. This supports an adjunct role for relational experiences outside therapy (group therapy, peer support, attuned relationships) as additional contexts for implicit relational learning.

## Quality notes

This is a sophisticated theoretical synthesis that brings together developmental neuroscience, hyperscanning research, attachment theory, and clinical psychotherapy theory. The hyperscanning evidence (Zhang et al., 2018; Dumas et al., 2010) adds an empirical dimension that Schore's earlier work lacked — it demonstrates that right-brain synchronization is not merely theoretical but measurably occurs between therapist and client. The framework is internally coherent and consistent with Schore's broader body of work spanning three decades.

Limitations for our review: (1) This remains primarily a theoretical paper — the hyperscanning evidence demonstrates neural coupling but does not demonstrate that this coupling produces lasting change. (2) There is no treatment duration data whatsoever. The paper explains mechanism but provides no timeline. (3) The extrapolation from millisecond-scale neural events to months-and-years-scale therapeutic outcomes involves multiple inferential steps that are plausible but unproven. (4) The paper does not engage with the dose-effect literature or any empirical evidence about how long treatment actually takes.

As a neuroscience source for this review, its role is to explain WHY change is slow (mechanism), not HOW LONG it takes (evidence). This distinction is important: the paper provides theoretical scaffolding, not empirical evidence for specific treatment durations.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The accumulation model suggests that both dose (number of sessions = number of opportunities for right-brain attunement) and elapsed time (biological consolidation of procedural memory between sessions) contribute to change. The paper does not distinguish between these, but the procedural memory framework implies that consolidation time matters — learning through repetition requires spacing for consolidation, not just massed practice.

### Therapy vs. life
The framework implies that right-brain-to-right-brain attunement occurs in all intimate relationships, not only in therapy. Any relational context that engages implicit nonverbal communication could contribute to reshaping implicit relational knowing. The paper does not discuss this, but it follows logically: therapy is one context for this accumulation, but close friendships, romantic partnerships, parent-child relationships, and even group experiences could all contribute.
