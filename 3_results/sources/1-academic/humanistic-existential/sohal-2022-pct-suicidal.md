## Summary

Longitudinal practice-based study examining dose-response patterns in person-centred therapy (PCT) with suicidal clients. N=56 clients presenting with suicidal ideation (scoring in clinical range on CORE-OM at intake) received open-ended PCT. The key finding is that a minimum of 15 sessions was needed before statistically significant clinical change was observed. This finding is contextualised against the Elliott et al. (2013) meta-analysis of humanistic-experiential psychotherapies, which reported average treatment lengths and effect sizes for PCT/EFT across studies. The study provides rare dose-response data for PCT specifically, as most dose-response research comes from CBT and psychodynamic traditions. ## Trajectory description

Based on available information: clients presenting with suicidal ideation did not show significant clinical change in early sessions. The trajectory suggests a slow-onset pattern where the therapeutic relationship and safety must be established before measurable change occurs. The 15-session threshold represents a minimum rather than an expected endpoint — it marks the point at which statistically significant change first becomes detectable, not the point at which treatment is complete. Given that this is a suicidal population, the trajectory likely continues well beyond 15 sessions for many clients. The open-ended nature of the therapy means the full range of treatment lengths in the sample is relevant but was not accessible from the abstract alone.

## Relevance to thesis

This study provides the clearest dose-response data in the humanistic-existential layer. Key relevance: (a) the 15-session minimum directly challenges time-limited service models (IAPT's 6–8 sessions, or even 12-session protocols) — these doses are below the threshold for detectable change in severe presentations; (b) 15 sessions is a *minimum* for significant change, not a sufficient dose — the full trajectory for recovery is likely much longer; (c) this is suicidal clients rather than complex trauma specifically, but there is substantial overlap in severity and complexity; (d) the finding connects to Murphy (2009) where 160 sessions were needed for the complex trauma case — the 15-session minimum and the 160-session case study bracket the dose range for severe presentations in PCT; (e) the reference to Elliott et al. (2013) meta-analysis grounds this in the broader humanistic-experiential evidence base.

## Quality notes
This is a practice-based longitudinal study with N=56, which is a meaningful sample size for PCT research (where most evidence comes from case studies or small samples). The use of CORE-OM as a standardised outcome measure enables comparison with other studies. The longitudinal design tracking change over sessions provides genuine dose-response data. Limitations: (a) no control group — this is naturalistic data; (b) the population is suicidal clients, not complex trauma specifically, though there is likely overlap; (c) full text was not accessible for this annotation, limiting the depth of analysis — exact methods, statistical approaches, effect sizes, and detailed results could not be verified; (d) the study comes from the Murphy research group at Nottingham, which produces most of the PCT trauma research — this is a coherent research programme but not independent replication.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Unknown from available materials. Key question: of the N=56 clients who were included, how many completed 15+ sessions vs. dropped out before the threshold? If the study only analyses completers, the 15-session finding applies only to those who stayed. For suicidal clients, dropout/disengagement is a significant clinical concern.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The study measures dose (session count) rather than elapsed time. The 15-session finding is a dose threshold — it does not tell us whether 15 sessions in 15 weeks produces the same effect as 15 sessions in 30 weeks (if sessions were biweekly). This is a common limitation in dose-response research.

### Therapy vs. life
Unknown from available materials. Practice-based studies capture therapy-as-delivered but typically do not control for extra-therapeutic factors.

### Access and context
Unknown from available materials. However, the study is contextually important because the 15-session minimum directly challenges the session limits imposed by statutory services. If IAPT offers 6–8 sessions and significant change requires a minimum of 15, then the service structure is systematically under-dosing severe presentations.

### Definition of endpoint
Symptom reduction: the CORE-OM measures general psychological distress (well-being, problems/symptoms, life functioning, risk). "Significant clinical change" likely refers to reliable and clinically significant change (RCSC) on CORE-OM — movement from clinical to non-clinical range. This is a symptom-based rather than structural endpoint.
