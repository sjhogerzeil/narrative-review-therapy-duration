## Summary

Single case study of "Mac," a middle-aged white male who experienced severe and prolonged physical, sexual, and emotional abuse in an institutional care setting from infancy through age 18. Mac presented with complex post-traumatic stress: extreme social isolation, hypervigilance, seizure-like episodes, flashbacks, cannabis and alcohol self-medication, inability to form interpersonal relationships, and a self-concept of being "bad at the core." He received 160 sessions of non-directive client-centred therapy over approximately 4 years 11 months — 140 weekly sessions over 3.5 years, then 20 sessions spaced out over a further 12 months. The therapist (the author) is a Chartered Counselling Psychologist working through Immigrant Counselling and Psychotherapy (icap), a UK charity offering open-ended therapy. Four emergent themes were identified through phenomenological analysis: post-traumatic distress, acceptance and understanding, new meaning/accurate symbolisation, and growth and relationship.

## Key claims
- Client-centred therapy can help client adjustment to the consequences of severe and traumatic childhood abuse — p. 3 (Abstract)
- Non-directivity enables the client to accurately symbolise experience at their own pace — p. 4
- Following trauma related to childhood abuse, the therapeutic relationship is "directly and specifically curative" (citing Briere, 2002) — p. 4
- "Client-centred therapy is not concerned with the cure of an illness through the elimination of symptoms" — the endpoint is meaning, understanding, and growth — p. 9
- No grand claims can be made from a single case study, but if the outcomes of meaning and growth are valued, "then the therapy was indeed a success" — p. 9
- Mac's life "remained challenging and at times anxiety and old ways of being returned" — symptoms were not eliminated but quality of life was substantially improved — p. 9

## Duration data

## Trajectory description

The trajectory is nonlinear but has discernible phases. Early sessions (1–26+) focused on disclosure, re-experiencing, and building trust — Mac gradually began to perceive that the threat was no longer present. By session 26, Mac was venturing into a pub, feeling "liked by people" for the first time, shifting from the "twisted rose" to "respected me." By session 68, a qualitatively different recall of a core traumatic memory occurred — full accurate symbolisation of the helplessness and hopelessness for the first time, with intense emotional expression. This was a turning point. By session 93, Mac had reconnected with his son after 17 years. Subsequent growth included employment for the first time in 15 years, reconnection with mother and other family members, and the development of a small circle of friends. The final 20 sessions were spaced out as a tapering process. Even at end of therapy, "anxiety and old ways of being returned" periodically — recovery was not linear or complete in symptomatic terms, but represented profound structural change.

## Relevance to thesis

This is the only published long-term PCT case study with a complex developmental trauma population, making it uniquely valuable despite being an N=1 study. The 160-session / ~5-year duration directly addresses the primary research question. The trajectory illustrates several thesis-relevant patterns: (a) the client needed to set the pace ("go slow, at my pace"); (b) disclosure and trust-building took many months; (c) accurate symbolisation of core trauma (session 68) did not occur until well into the second year; (d) growth in relationships and functioning continued to develop over years 3–5; (e) the endpoint is structural change and quality of life, not symptom elimination. The case also illustrates that weekly frequency may be insufficient for this population — Mac wanted more — and that the tapering phase serves a function.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Therapy was continuous: weekly for 3.5 years, then gradual spacing over 12 months. No interruptions mentioned. The tapering suggests a deliberate winding-down process rather than abrupt ending.

### One modality or many?
Strictly non-directive client-centred therapy throughout. The therapist explicitly worked within a single modality. No adjunct modalities mentioned.

### One therapist or several?
One therapist throughout (the author). Monthly supervision with the same supervisor for the full duration. The single-therapist relationship was central to the work.

### Session frequency?
Once weekly. Mac expressed disappointment at this being the only option — he would have preferred more. Accepted it as "better than nothing." This raises the question of whether higher frequency might have shortened the calendar duration.

### Phase-based or organic?
Organic rather than phase-based, though the four emergent themes map loosely onto a temporal progression: distress/disclosure (early), acceptance/understanding (emerging around sessions 20–30), new meaning/accurate symbolisation (around session 68 and onwards), growth and relationship (sessions 90+). This is an emergent sequence, not a prescribed phase structure. The trajectory spirals — "old ways of being returned" periodically.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Therapy alone. Mac was not on prescribed medication and avoided the GP out of fear. He self-medicated with alcohol and cannabis. No mention of any adjunct treatments, groups, or other support. His social context was extremely isolated — the therapeutic relationship was one of very few relational connections.

## Quality notes
This is a single case study with the therapist as researcher — a dual-role limitation the author acknowledges. The data source is detailed session notes rather than recordings, introducing recall and selection bias. No standardised outcome measures were used — the analysis is phenomenological and thematic. The lack of formal outcome measurement is consistent with the PCT tradition but limits comparability. Despite these limitations, the study provides unusually rich process data across 160 sessions, something almost no other published research offers for this population. The case is not meant to be generalised from, and the author explicitly states "there are no grand claims to be made." Its value lies in the detailed illustration of what a long-term trajectory looks like session by session in client-centred work with complex trauma — a perspective almost entirely absent from the evidence base.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
This is a single case of someone who stayed. The study provides no data on how Mac was recruited, whether others were referred and declined, or what happened to clients who left the service. By definition, an N=1 case study of a completed therapy captures a survivor. The fact that Mac explicitly needed to set the pace and found interpersonal contact terrifying raises the question of how many similar clients never engage or drop out early.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Unusually clear on both dose and elapsed time: 160 sessions over 4 years 11 months. The distinction between weekly sessions (3.5 years) and spaced sessions (12 months) provides specific dose information. The tapering phase suggests that both dose (sessions) and elapsed time (calendar years for life changes to unfold) matter. Mac's growth events — reconnecting with son, entering employment, reconnecting with mother — took real-world time to occur and could not have been compressed by higher session frequency alone.

### Therapy vs. life
The study closely tracks the interplay between therapy and life. Mac's growth events (forming pub friendships, reconnecting with family, entering employment) happened outside sessions but were enabled by the therapeutic relationship. The author notes that Mac used the safety of the therapeutic relationship to take relational risks outside it. Life and therapy are deeply entangled — the therapy provided a base from which life engagement became possible, but the life changes (being liked, being respected, reconnecting with his son) were themselves healing.

### Access and context
Mac was referred through icap, a UK charity offering open-ended, free therapy to Irish immigrants — a highly specific and unusual access pathway. No discussion of financial barriers, though the open-ended charity model meant no insurance or session limits applied. Mac's fear of statutory services (rooted in his institutional abuse) is itself an access barrier — he explicitly asked in the first session if the therapist was going to "lock him up." The paper does not discuss who can and cannot access this kind of treatment, though the existence of a charity willing to fund 160 sessions is clearly exceptional.

### Definition of endpoint
The endpoint is explicitly structural, not symptomatic: "client-centred therapy is about helping the client find a better understanding of themselves and the adversity they have faced and that this is growth." The author acknowledges that "Mac's life remained challenging and at times anxiety and old ways of being returned" — symptoms were not eliminated. Success is defined as growth in self-understanding, formation of non-abusive relationships, increased quality of life, and movement from isolation to connection. This is a fundamentally different endpoint definition than symptom reduction.

### Dropout data
No dropout data. This is a single case of a client who completed therapy. No information about other clients in the service or comparative retention rates.
