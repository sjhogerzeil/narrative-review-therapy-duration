## Summary

Systematic review of 19 studies (screened from 6,088) evaluating phase-oriented treatment (PoT) for trauma-related dissociative disorders (DDs). PoT follows the ISSTD (2011) three-phase model: (1) stabilisation and symptom reduction, (2) treating trauma memories, (3) identity reintegration and rehabilitation. The review finds moderate-to-large effect sizes across outcomes but notes that most participants continued to experience clinically significant symptoms post-treatment. Treatment durations across included studies ranged from 7 days to 5 years. This is the most directly relevant source to our defined population and research question.

## Trajectory description

The review describes a nonlinear, phasic trajectory. Patients cycle through three phases (stabilisation, trauma processing, reintegration), with progress in later phases sometimes producing re-traumatisation as new dissociated material emerges. This cycling means that a patient may appear to regress when they are actually progressing to a deeper layer of processing. The large effect sizes for managing affect/impulses (d=1.61) and depression (d=1.66) suggest that stabilisation-phase work produces measurable gains, while the more modest effect for dissociation itself (d=0.63) suggests that the core structural pathology changes more slowly. Most participants still had clinically significant symptoms at post-treatment, indicating that even multi-year interventions often represent incomplete trajectories.

## Relevance to thesis

This is the most directly relevant source. It addresses our exact population (complex dissociative disorders arising from developmental trauma) and our exact treatment model (phase-oriented). The key contribution is twofold: (1) empirical data on treatment durations, clustering around 1–5 years; (2) the explicit finding that even these long treatments leave most patients with ongoing symptoms requiring further intervention. This directly supports the thesis that healing from complex developmental trauma takes years and that standard endpoints underestimate the true duration needed.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No direct data on interruptions or breaks in treatment. The review reports treatment durations as elapsed time, with no discussion of whether these were continuous courses or interrupted trajectories.

### One modality or many?
The included studies used diverse modalities within the PoT framework: individual psychotherapy, group therapy, inpatient programs, and combinations. The review notes that only 3 of 19 studies specifically evaluated PoT as a defined intervention — the rest used various therapeutic approaches loosely organized around the phase model. This suggests that in practice, PoT is a framework rather than a single modality, and clinicians draw on multiple approaches within it.

### One therapist or several?
No data. The review does not address whether patients had single or multiple therapists across their treatment trajectories.

### Session frequency?
Session frequency varied widely: 1–5 individual sessions per week across studies. Group therapy ranged from 2 daily sessions (inpatient) to 35 hours weekly (intensive program). The range is so wide that "session frequency" is not a meaningful summary variable across studies.

### Phase-based or organic?
Explicitly phase-based by design — the review's inclusion criterion was phase-oriented treatment. The three phases (stabilisation, trauma processing, reintegration) are based on structural dissociation theory (ISSTD 2011). However, the review's finding of nonlinear progress with cycling between phases suggests that while the framework is phase-based, the lived trajectory is more recursive than sequential.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Several included studies involved inpatient programs combining individual and group therapy, which constitute a therapy-plus model. No data on adjuncts such as medication, bodywork, or peer support beyond what was included in formal treatment programs.

## Quality notes

The review itself is methodologically sound as a systematic review (PRISMA-compliant, registered protocol, dual-coded quality assessment). However, the included studies are weak: 58% rated "weak" on the EPHPP quality assessment tool, and only 11% rated "strong." The evidence base consists mostly of uncontrolled case studies and pre-post designs. No RCTs are included. This means the duration data and effect sizes must be interpreted with caution — they describe what happened in these uncontrolled studies, not what PoT causally produces. The review is transparent about these limitations. For synthesis purposes: the duration data are reliable descriptors of what treatment actually looks like in practice, even if causal attributions are weak. The 3–5 year projection is better understood as clinical consensus than as an evidence-based prescription.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Significant concern. Dropout rates were reported in only 4 of 19 studies, ranging from 3% to 23%. Higher dissociation predicted dropout (Baekkelund 2022). This means the duration and outcome data disproportionately reflect patients who stayed in treatment — the very patients most able to tolerate the demands of multi-year phase-oriented work. Patients who dropped out (potentially the most dissociative, per Baekkelund) are invisible in the outcome data.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The review reports both session counts and elapsed time for some studies (Boyd 1997 provides both: 111 sessions over approximately 22 months for phases 2–3). Other studies report only elapsed time (e.g., Brand 2014: 5 years). Session frequency varied widely (1–5/week). This makes it impossible to disentangle dose from duration across studies. The 3–5 year projection is in calendar time, with no specification of the corresponding session count.

### Therapy vs. life
No data. The review attributes all change to treatment. No included studies appear to have measured or discussed non-therapeutic factors contributing to improvement. Given that treatments lasted up to 5 years, the role of life events, maturation, and other non-therapeutic factors is likely significant but entirely unmeasured.

### Access and context
No data. The review does not discuss who can access 3–5 years of weekly-or-more-frequent specialist treatment. Given that this requires sustained access to dissociative disorder specialists, who are rare, the implicit access barriers are substantial. The silence is notable.

### Definition of endpoint
Mixed. The review uses multiple outcome measures: dissociation (DES, MID), psychological distress (SCL-90, BSI), depression (BDI), PTSD (PCL, IES-R), and functional measures (managing affect/impulses). The critical finding is that "most participants across studies continued to experience clinically significant symptoms post-treatment that required further intervention." This means even the studies' own endpoints were not reached for most participants — the treatment durations reported (up to 5 years) were not sufficient for full recovery as defined by symptom measures.

### Dropout data
Yes. Four studies reported dropout rates: 3–23%. Baekkelund (2022) found that higher dissociation predicted dropout. This is an important finding: the patients with the most severe pathology (highest dissociation) were more likely to leave treatment, meaning that the positive effect sizes are partly a function of differential attrition. The remaining 15 studies did not report dropout rates at all.
