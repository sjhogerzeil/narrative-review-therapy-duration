## Summary

Systematic review of predictors of psychotherapy dropout in BPD patients, covering 6 RCTs. Identifies weaker therapeutic alliance and higher hostility as the strongest dropout predictors, while better mindfulness skills and better memory/executive control are protective factors. Sociodemographic variables and treatment history did not influence retention. The review highlights a fundamental treatment-engagement paradox: the interpersonal difficulties that define BPD (hostility, alliance difficulties) are the same factors that predict dropout.

## Trajectory description

The paper does not describe a treatment trajectory per se, but it describes the trajectory of treatment engagement: patients with weaker alliance and higher hostility are at risk of leaving treatment before it can be effective. The recommendations (early alliance-building, mindfulness skill development, attachment-focused interventions) imply a trajectory in which the early phase of treatment must prioritize engagement and retention before other therapeutic goals can be pursued.

## Relevance to thesis

This source is directly relevant to the duration question because dropout is the primary threat to adequate treatment length. If patients leave treatment before completing it, duration data from completers is survivorship-biased. The treatment-engagement paradox is particularly important: the interpersonal deficits that bring BPD patients to treatment (hostility, alliance difficulty) are the same deficits that drive them away. This means that the early phase of treatment must be dedicated to overcoming the engagement barrier — which adds time to the overall trajectory. The finding that sociodemographic variables and treatment history do NOT predict dropout shifts the focus to relational and capacity-based factors, aligning with the structural dissociation literature's emphasis on integrative capacity (Steele et al. 2005) and secure dependency (Steele et al. 2001).

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Indirectly addressed: the paper's central concern is that treatment IS interrupted by dropout. The recommendations (alliance monitoring, attachment-focused interventions) are strategies for maintaining continuity. The implication is that continuity is essential but fragile in this population.

### One modality or many?
The review covers 6 RCTs of various evidence-based BPD psychotherapies — the dropout predictors (alliance, hostility) are consistent across modalities, suggesting they are population-level factors rather than modality-specific.

### One therapist or several?
Not directly addressed. However, the emphasis on therapeutic alliance as the key retention factor implies that therapist continuity matters — alliance is built with a specific therapist, and switching therapists would restart the alliance-building process.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?

## Quality notes

Systematic review of 6 RCTs — a rigorous methodology for the question addressed. The authors report low risk of attrition and reporting bias, but unclear risk of selection, performance, and detection bias across included studies. The main limitation is the small number of included studies (6 RCTs), which limits the robustness of the predictor findings. The review is focused on BPD specifically, not on complex PTSD or dissociative disorders — the predictors may or may not generalize to our target population. ## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
This source is centrally about survivorship bias — it studies the factors that determine who stays in treatment and who leaves. It does not, however, report outcomes for patients who dropped out versus those who completed treatment. The finding that the same interpersonal deficits that define BPD also predict dropout means that all BPD treatment outcome studies are systematically biased toward patients with better alliance capacity, lower hostility, and better executive function — i.e., the less severely affected subset.
