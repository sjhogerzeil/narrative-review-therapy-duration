## Summary

This podcast episode from the National Centre for Mental Health features Professor Jon Bisson (clinical psychiatrist, Cardiff University) and Michael Davitt (author with lived experience of C-PTSD from childhood emotional neglect). Bisson provides a clinical overview of the ICD-11 distinction between PTSD and C-PTSD, explaining the three additional symptom domains that define C-PTSD: emotional regulation difficulties, negative self-concept, and disturbed relationships. He notes that UK epidemiological data shows C-PTSD is more prevalent than PTSD, has lower recovery rates, and requires longer treatment. Bisson describes the STAIR (Skills Training in Affective and Interpersonal Regulation) modular intervention being developed at Cardiff, which addresses emotional regulation, relationships, and self-concept before trauma processing. Davitt's lived experience illustrates the multi-decade trajectory of complex trauma: childhood emotional neglect, decades of repression, a major breakdown approximately 10 years before recording, multiple therapists before finding one who "clicked," ongoing therapy at age 53 with persistent symptoms (e.g., flashback of his mother berating him during touch rugby the day before recording). The episode emphasizes that C-PTSD often involves what did not happen (absence of love, attention, attunement) rather than discrete traumatic events, making it harder to identify and treat.

## Key claims

- C-PTSD is more common than PTSD in the UK population: "complex post-traumatic stress disorder seems to be commoner in the population than post-traumatic stress disorder" — Bisson, epidemiology discussion
- C-PTSD has lower recovery rates than PTSD: "the recovery rates from complex post-traumatic stress disorder are not likely to be as high as they are from post-traumatic stress disorder" — Bisson
- C-PTSD requires the three core PTSD symptoms (re-experiencing, avoidance, hyperarousal) plus disturbances in self-organisation: emotional regulation difficulties, negative self-concept, and disturbed relationships — Bisson, diagnostic overview
- Childhood trauma creates arrested emotional development: "I was an eight-year-old boy, even though I was a 50-year-old man. Because of trauma in childhood, you become, this concept of arrested development, you get stuck in that time, emotionally speaking" — Davitt
- The core wound in developmental neglect is what was absent, not what happened: "it's not a question of what happened to me. I think it was a question of what didn't happen to me" — Davitt
- Self-abandonment follows parental emotional absence: "I effectively abandon my real self just to try and find any way to attach to them... don't be myself, because that's not acceptable to them" — Davitt
- Intergenerational trauma is a key mechanism: Davitt's parents had their own traumatic histories that prevented them from providing emotional attunement — Davitt
- Treatment must address skills deficits (emotional regulation, relationships, self-concept) before or alongside trauma processing — Bisson, STAIR discussion
- EMDR and trauma-focused CBT "can be very helpful for people with complex post-traumatic stress disorder" but treatment must be personalised and co-produced — Bisson
- The therapeutic relationship (being heard, validated, understood) is as important as specific techniques: "you felt heard, you felt somebody understood the issues, and then you had treatment that met your needs moving forward" — Bisson, commenting on Davitt's experience

## Duration data

- **Davitt's timeline:** Breakdown approximately 10 years before recording (so around 2014); ongoing therapy at age 53 (2024); "seeing [therapist] for many years since, on and off"; still experiencing flashbacks during daily activities (touch rugby example from the day before)
- **Multiple therapists:** "the first two therapists I saw, I didn't quite click with... I tried a third time, and I just really clicked with this therapist" — Davitt
- **C-PTSD recovery rates:** Bisson states recovery rates are lower than for PTSD, implying longer treatment is needed, but provides no specific duration figures
- **STAIR intervention:** Currently in development/piloting at Cardiff; modular format addressing skills before trauma processing; no duration data for the programme itself
- **Prevention emphasis:** Bisson emphasises early intervention "at an earlier possible stage... to prevent things developing into more complex conditions" — implying that once C-PTSD is established, treatment is protracted

## Trajectory description

The episode does not describe a formal treatment trajectory but Davitt's personal narrative reveals one: decades of emotional repression following childhood neglect, eventual systemic breakdown ("my whole system just crashed... my body telling me... you've got to stop"), initial psychiatric contact and diagnosis (depression, not C-PTSD specifically), two unsuccessful therapeutic relationships, a successful long-term therapeutic relationship ("many years"), creative expression through writing and drawing (5 books), involvement in research/PPI groups, and ongoing recovery at age 53 with persistent symptoms. The trajectory is markedly non-linear: Davitt describes recovery as ongoing rather than complete, with triggers still active in daily life. Bisson's clinical framing adds that treatment for C-PTSD should be modular (skills first, then trauma processing) and personalised, suggesting a phased but flexible trajectory.

## Relevance to thesis

This source supports the thesis at the epidemiological and clinical level. Bisson's statement that C-PTSD is more prevalent than PTSD and has lower recovery rates directly implies longer treatment timelines. The STAIR approach (skills training before trauma processing) implicitly requires more time than single-focus trauma protocols. Davitt's lived experience demonstrates that even with resources (financial means, supportive employer, good therapist), recovery from childhood emotional neglect remains incomplete after approximately 10 years of active work and a lifetime of prior coping. His flashback during touch rugby at age 53 — decades after the original trauma — illustrates the enduring nature of complex trauma's effects. The episode's emphasis on what was absent (rather than what happened) connects to the theoretical argument that restoring missing developmental capacities (emotional regulation, self-concept, relational skills) is fundamentally more time-consuming than processing discrete traumatic events.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?

Davitt describes seeing his current therapist "for many years since, on and off," indicating an interrupted pattern. He had two unsuccessful therapeutic relationships before finding the current one, representing discontinuities. No systematic data on continuity patterns in C-PTSD treatment is provided by Bisson.

### One modality or many?

Multiple modalities are discussed at the clinical level. Bisson describes STAIR as drawing from CBT, dialectical behavioural therapy, and compassion-focused therapy. He mentions EMDR and trauma-focused CBT as treatments that can help with C-PTSD. The STAIR programme is explicitly modular (emotional regulation, interpersonal relationships, self-concept, and core PTSD symptom processing). Davitt does not specify his therapist's modality. No data on whether individuals typically need multiple modalities sequentially.

### One therapist or several?

Davitt explicitly required multiple therapists: "the first two therapists I saw, I didn't quite click with... I tried a third time." This is framed as a common pattern — the therapeutic alliance is described as critical, and finding the right fit required trial and error. Bisson emphasises co-production of treatment plans with the individual, implying the relationship matters as much as the method.

### Session frequency?

No data (full text reviewed). Neither Bisson nor Davitt specify session frequency. Davitt's "on and off" pattern suggests variable frequency over years. The STAIR programme's frequency is not described.

### Phase-based or organic?

Bisson describes a phase-based approach at the treatment design level: STAIR addresses skills (emotional regulation, relationships, self-concept) before trauma processing. However, Davitt's personal trajectory appears organic — breakdown, trial-and-error with therapists, creative expression, research involvement, ongoing therapy. The tension between structured clinical programmes and the organic nature of individual recovery is implicit but not discussed.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?

Davitt's recovery explicitly involves therapy-plus: writing and drawing (5 published books), involvement in the PPI/research group at Cardiff, journaling, and touch rugby/physical activity. He describes creative expression as essential: "the whole idea is to get all these emotions which I had depressed for so long and out of my mind onto paper." The PPI group membership provides community and purpose. Bisson does not discuss adjuncts to formal therapy for C-PTSD.

## Quality notes

This is a podcast from a university-affiliated research centre (NCMH, Cardiff), lending it institutional credibility. Bisson is a leading figure in traumatic stress research and was involved in developing the ICD-11 C-PTSD diagnostic criteria and the International Trauma Questionnaire (ITQ). His clinical claims are well-grounded in the diagnostic and epidemiological literature. However, the episode is general and introductory in nature — it does not provide specific treatment outcome data, duration figures, or detailed research findings. Davitt's lived experience is moving and illustrative but is a single case. The episode's primary contribution is the authoritative clinical framing that C-PTSD is more prevalent than PTSD and has lower recovery rates, combined with the implicit treatment duration implications. As a Layer 1a source, its value lies in establishing the clinical consensus that C-PTSD requires longer, more comprehensive treatment than PTSD, rather than in providing specific duration data.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias

Moderate. Davitt is someone with resources (financial means, supportive employer, access to good therapists) who has been actively engaged in recovery for approximately 10 years. He explicitly acknowledges this privilege: "I really do feel for people out there, luckily enough, I had a bit of money in the bank." People without these resources are acknowledged but their trajectories are not represented. The PPI group represents a self-selected population of engaged survivors.

### Dose vs. elapsed time

Not addressed. Neither Bisson nor Davitt distinguish between number of sessions and calendar time. Davitt's "many years, on and off" suggests significant elapsed time with variable dose. The STAIR programme is described as modular but its expected dose is not specified. This is a significant gap for a source relevant to duration questions.

### Therapy vs. life

Partially addressed. Davitt's recovery includes significant non-therapeutic elements: creative expression (writing, drawing, publishing 5 books), physical activity (touch rugby), community involvement (PPI group), and the ongoing process of making sense of his childhood. Bisson mentions the importance of "supporting people accordingly" across society (Trauma Inform Wales Framework), suggesting that recovery extends beyond formal treatment. However, the relative contribution of therapy versus these life factors is not discussed.

### Access and context

Explicitly addressed by Davitt: "I really do feel for people out there, luckily enough, I had a bit of money in the bank. So I could see a therapist for many years, which of course, cost money. And then I had the time off work." Bisson frames the first step as consulting a general practitioner (NHS pathway), suggesting awareness of the systemic context. The STAIR programme is being developed within the NHS, indicating attention to accessibility. However, Bisson's acknowledgement that C-PTSD "tends to be more disabling" and has lower recovery rates raises implicit access concerns: the people who most need treatment are least likely to be able to sustain long-term engagement. This is a notable strength of the source for the access cross-cutting theme.

### Definition of endpoint

Mixed. Bisson defines treatment goals in terms of both symptom reduction (PTSD symptom management through exposure/habituation) and functional improvement (skills development in emotional regulation, relationships, self-concept, and sense of control). He states: "having a sense of control is critical to people to feel able to move forward in their lives... that sort of report of feeling more in control of yourself is very much associated with improved health and wellbeing." Davitt's implicit endpoint is more existential: self-understanding, self-compassion, capacity to function despite ongoing symptoms. His ongoing flashbacks at age 53 suggest that "recovery" does not mean symptom elimination. No formal outcome metrics are discussed in the podcast.

### Dropout data

No dropout data (full text reviewed). No mention of attrition from the STAIR programme (which is still in piloting). Davitt mentions terminating with two therapists before finding a good fit, but this is framed as therapist matching rather than dropout. Bisson does not discuss treatment dropout or non-completion rates for C-PTSD interventions.
