## Summary

Challenges the prevailing clinical and cultural assumption that dependency in therapy is pathological or regressive. Argues that dependency is a manifestation of attachment — a necessary, naturalistic occurrence in the treatment of chronically traumatized patients — and distinguishes secure dependency (growth-promoting, autonomy-building) from insecure dependency (chaotic, demanding, extreme). Structural dissociation creates contradictory dependency patterns, as different dissociative parts carry different attachment needs. The paper draws on Bowlby and Janet to argue that the development of secure dependency on the therapist is not iatrogenic but the very mechanism through which cure occurs.

## Key claims
- Dependency is not regressive but a manifestation of attachment — p. 87–88
- Distinction between secure dependency (promotes growth and autonomy) and insecure dependency (extreme, chaotic, demanding) — p. 84–87
- Cultural and clinical bias frames all dependency as pathological; this paper argues against that framing — p. 80–82
- Structural dissociation creates contradictory dependency: different parts have different attachment needs — some desperately seeking attachment, others avoidant — p. 89–93
- Janet (1897/98) insisted that intense dependency on the therapist was necessary and naturalistic, not iatrogenic — "not only a symptom, but the means by which cure took place" — p. 83
- Secure dependency must be developed through each phase of treatment — it cannot be rushed or bypassed — p. 93–96
- The therapist must be able to tolerate and work with dependency rather than pathologize it or push premature autonomy — p. 96–100

## Duration data
- No specific duration numbers are provided
- The core argument has profound duration implications: if secure dependency is the mechanism of cure, and dependency develops over time through consistent relational experience, then treatment inherently requires years — you cannot accelerate the development of secure attachment — implicit throughout
- The need to work with dependency in each dissociative part multiplies treatment time for secondary and tertiary structural dissociation — p. 89–93

## Trajectory description

The trajectory implied is one of gradual relational development within the therapeutic dyad. The patient begins with insecure dependency patterns (chaotic, avoidant, or contradictory across dissociative parts), and through consistent therapeutic engagement develops secure dependency — which in turn becomes the foundation for autonomy. This relational trajectory unfolds alongside the phased treatment model (Steele et al. 2005): dependency work is not a separate phase but a continuous thread running through all phases. The path is complicated by the contradictory attachment needs of different dissociative parts, meaning that dependency dynamics shift and reconfigure as different parts become accessible in therapy.

## Relevance to thesis

This source provides a powerful argument for why treatment cannot be shortened. If the mechanism of cure IS the development of secure attachment to the therapist — and attachment develops through consistent, long-term relational experience — then brief treatment is structurally inadequate for this population. The paper reframes the "how long" question: the relevant duration is not measured in sessions or techniques delivered, but in the time required for a fundamentally new relational pattern to develop. This is biological/developmental time, not therapeutic dose. The paper also directly addresses the one-therapist-or-several question: if dependency on THIS therapist is the mechanism, then therapist continuity is essential.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Strongly implies continuous treatment. The development of secure dependency requires consistency — breaks and interruptions risk reactivating insecure attachment patterns and triggering the contradictory dependency responses of different dissociative parts. The paper does not explicitly discuss interruptions, but the theoretical framework argues against them.

### One modality or many?
No data. The paper does not address modality.

### One therapist or several?
Strongly argues for one sustained therapeutic relationship. The entire paper is premised on the idea that secure dependency on the therapist is the mechanism of cure. This dependency is person-specific — it develops with THIS therapist through THIS relational history. Switching therapists would mean starting the dependency process over. The contradictory dependency patterns of different dissociative parts further argue for one therapist who knows the system.

### Session frequency?
No data. Session frequency is not discussed.

### Phase-based or organic?
Dependency work is presented as running through all phases — it is not a separate phase but a continuous relational thread. This is compatible with the phase model (Steele et al. 2005) but adds a relational dimension that cuts across phases.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?

## Quality notes

This is a theoretical/clinical paper (38 pages, J Trauma & Dissociation) that draws on attachment theory (Bowlby), dissociation theory (Janet), and clinical experience. It does not present empirical data on treatment outcomes or duration. Its strength lies in its theoretical coherence and its challenge to the dominant clinical assumption that dependency is always pathological. The argument is well-grounded in attachment theory and supported by historical references (Janet 1897/98). As a theoretical paper, it should be weighted for its explanatory power (WHY treatment takes long) rather than for empirical evidence (HOW long). It is part of the same theoretical project as Steele et al. 2005 and Van der Hart et al. 2006.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Not directly applicable — this is a theoretical paper. However, the paper implicitly describes patients who stayed long enough to develop secure dependency. Patients who dropped out due to intolerable dependency feelings (a common clinical phenomenon) are not discussed. The treatment-engagement paradox identified in the Layer 2 sources is relevant here: the patients who most need to develop secure dependency may be the least able to tolerate the dependency feelings that arise early in treatment.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The paper's core argument implies that elapsed time is more important than dose. The development of secure attachment is a developmental process that unfolds over months and years of consistent relational experience — it cannot be compressed by increasing session frequency. This aligns with the emerging pattern from Layer 2 (Lindfors 2019: calendar time matters more than session count for personality-level change).

### Therapy vs. life
The paper is therapy-centric — the development of secure dependency occurs within the therapeutic relationship. However, the ultimate goal (autonomy) is a life outcome, not a therapy outcome. The paper implicitly acknowledges that therapy creates the relational foundation from which the patient can then engage with life — but the non-therapeutic factors that contribute to healing (relationships, community, etc.) are not discussed.

### Access and context
No data. The paper does not address who can access long-term specialized trauma treatment. This is a significant gap given that the treatment model requires sustained, uninterrupted therapy with a single specialized clinician — a resource that is scarce, expensive, and geographically concentrated.

### Definition of endpoint
The endpoint is implicitly structural: the development of secure dependency, which becomes the foundation for autonomy. This is not symptom reduction but a fundamental shift in relational capacity — the patient moves from insecure/chaotic dependency patterns to secure dependency that supports autonomous functioning. The endpoint is open-ended and developmental rather than categorical.

### Dropout data
No dropout data. The paper does not discuss premature termination, although the dependency dynamics it describes are clinically recognized as a major driver of dropout in this population.
