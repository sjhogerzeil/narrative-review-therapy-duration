## Summary

A MyPTSD forum thread that directly addresses secondary research question 4 (session frequency). The thread collects self-reported session frequencies and reveals wide variation: from monthly (Teamwork, pro bono — described as "pure hell") to twice-weekly (somerandomguy, grit). The most striking finding is that frequency decisions are shaped by attachment dynamics, not by clinical protocol or treatment manuals. Skywatcher describes "major attachment" to the therapist as a factor in maintaining weekly sessions. somerandomguy tried reducing from twice-weekly to once-weekly and failed — the gap was too large. RussellSue reports that the anxiety about reducing frequency exceeded the actual difficulty. This is dose-response data filtered through the therapeutic relationship.

## Key claims
- Session frequency is highly individual — no consensus, no "correct" frequency
- Weekly is the most common self-reported frequency, but twice-weekly is needed by some (somerandomguy, grit)
- Monthly is insufficient for active trauma work — Teamwork describes it as "pure hell"
- Attachment anxiety shapes frequency decisions — reducing frequency triggers attachment fears that may exceed the actual clinical impact
- Progressive reduction (weekly to biweekly to less) can work — piratelady's trajectory
- Frequency too high can also be a problem — coraxxx finds twice-weekly excessive

## Duration data
- Skywatcher: weekly for 4 years, considering biweekly
- somerandomguy: twice weekly, tried reducing, failed, returned to twice
- grit: twice weekly psychoanalysis
- Teamwork: monthly (pro bono)
- coraxxx: weekly (preferred); monthly too infrequent; twice-weekly too much
- piratelady: progressive reduction from weekly
- RussellSue: frequency reduction attempted

## Trajectory description

The frequency trajectories described here follow a developmental pattern. The typical arc: start at higher frequency (weekly or twice-weekly) when the work is intensive, then gradually reduce as stability increases. But this reduction is not smooth. somerandomguy tried reducing and had to go back. RussellSue found the anxiety about reducing was worse than the actual experience. piratelady is the success case for tapering.

grit's formulation is the most theoretically grounded: "trauma cannot be processed halfheartedly" — psychoanalytic twice-weekly as a minimum dose for deep work. Teamwork represents the floor: monthly sessions are survivable but not therapeutic, constrained by pro bono availability rather than clinical judgment.

## Relevance to thesis

Directly relevant to secondary question 4. The thread provides client-perspective data on session frequency that complicates any simple prescription. The key insight: frequency is not a clinical variable that can be optimised in isolation — it is entangled with attachment, affordability, therapist availability, and the client's window of tolerance for between-session processing. grit's claim that "trauma cannot be processed halfheartedly" aligns with the intensive-format literature (Sciarrino 2020) but from a psychoanalytic rather than EMDR perspective. Teamwork's monthly pro bono situation illustrates how access constraints force clinically inadequate dosing.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
somerandomguy's failed attempt to reduce frequency is a form of interrupted engagement that was immediately reversed. The thread suggests that continuity at the right frequency is protective, and that premature reduction can destabilise.

### One modality or many?
grit explicitly names psychoanalysis (twice-weekly); Skywatcher is in EMDR-based therapy. Frequency norms differ by modality.

### One therapist or several?
Not addressed.

### Session frequency?
This is the primary topic. Summary: Weekly is modal. Twice-weekly is needed for deep psychoanalytic or intensive trauma work. Monthly is inadequate for active processing. The optimal frequency is individual, shaped by clinical need, attachment dynamics, and access constraints. Reduction is possible but triggers attachment anxiety.

### Phase-based or organic?
piratelady's progressive reduction (weekly to biweekly to less) implies a phase-like tapering, but driven organically by clinical progress rather than by protocol.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Not addressed, though the forum community itself serves as between-session support — particularly for those at lower frequencies who need processing space between sessions.

## Quality notes

Self-selected forum sample with all the usual limitations. The specific value is the diversity of frequencies reported and the candour about attachment dynamics shaping frequency decisions. This is data that clinical trials do not collect: the subjective experience of frequency changes, the anxiety about reduction, the attachment to the rhythm of sessions. The sample likely overrepresents people in active therapy (not those who have completed or dropped out).

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
High. All posters are currently in therapy. People who stopped due to cost, dissatisfaction, or resolution are not represented.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
This thread is directly about dose (frequency). grit's twice-weekly psychoanalysis over multiple years represents a high cumulative dose. Teamwork's monthly sessions represent a low dose despite potentially long elapsed time. The thread makes clear that "years in therapy" means very different things at different frequencies.

### Therapy vs. life
Not addressed. The between-session interval is discussed as a clinical variable (too long = destabilising; too short = overwhelming) but not in terms of what happens in that interval outside the therapy room.

### Access and context
Teamwork's pro bono monthly sessions are the clearest access data in the thread: frequency is constrained by affordability, not by clinical judgment. The therapist sees Teamwork pro bono but can only offer monthly slots. This is the access-frequency nexus: the "correct" frequency may be clinically irrelevant if the client cannot afford or access it.

### Definition of endpoint
Not defined. The thread is about process (how often), not outcome (to what end).

### Dropout data
somerandomguy's failed reduction attempt is informative: reducing frequency too soon can feel like a form of abandonment or loss, even when clinically rational. This suggests that some "dropout" in the broader literature may be triggered by frequency changes that were experienced as relational ruptures.
