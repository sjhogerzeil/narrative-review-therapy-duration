## Summary

A MyPTSD forum thread in which community members share how long they have been in therapy. The thread produces a naturalistic cross-section of therapy durations for people with trauma histories. Durations range from 8 months (MisterCatLady, still early) to 27 years (Crow, with 16 years with the same psychiatrist). The most consistent theme across posters is not about technique or modality but about the therapeutic relationship — finding "the right one" after cycling through multiple therapists. Several posters describe an escalation gap: years spent in therapy that felt unhelpful because the fit was wrong, followed by rapid progress once the relational match was made.

## Key claims
- The therapeutic relationship is the decisive variable in treatment effectiveness, not modality — multiple posters state this independently
- Duration is measured in years, not sessions, for this population — the shortest reported engagement is 8 months and still ongoing
- "The right therapist" is a recurring concept: many posters describe cycling through therapists before finding a productive match
- Initial misdiagnosis (depression instead of PTSD/CPTSD) can waste years — One step at a time's trajectory exemplifies this

## Duration data
- Crow: 27 years total therapy, 16 years with current psychiatrist, 6 years intensive weekly
- One step at a time: 6 years on-and-off, initially misdiagnosed as depression
- Panda Bear: nearly 5 years with same therapist
- MisterCatLady: 8 months current, 4 total therapists
- Range across thread: 8 months to 27 years

## Trajectory description

No single trajectory emerges; instead, the thread reveals multiple trajectory types. Crow's trajectory is one of gradual intensification — moving from sporadic engagement to 6 years of intensive weekly work. One step at a time's trajectory was disrupted by misdiagnosis, losing years to depression treatment before the trauma was identified. Panda Bear describes a terrifying but sustained engagement: "scared shitless for nearly three years but he never backed down." MisterCatLady is still in the early-cycling phase, having gone through 4 therapists in a relatively short time.

The common pattern is not linear but iterative: trial, mismatch, cycling, eventual relational match, then sustained engagement.

## Relevance to thesis

Directly relevant. This thread provides ground-truth data on how long therapy actually takes from the client perspective — and the answer is: years, often many years, with a significant portion of that time spent in the escalation gap (searching for the right therapist, being misdiagnosed, receiving mismatched treatment). The emphasis on relationship over modality aligns with the academic common-factors literature and with the practitioner layer. The durations reported here are consistent with the naturalistic studies (Helsinki, TADS) but told from the inside.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Mixed. One step at a time describes on-and-off engagement over 6 years. Others describe continuous engagement. The on-off pattern appears to be driven by misdiagnosis and poor therapeutic fit, not by therapeutic design.

### One modality or many?
Not foregrounded. Multiple posters have experienced multiple therapists, which likely means multiple modalities, but the thread focuses on the relationship rather than the technique. The implicit message: modality matters less than the relational fit.

### One therapist or several?
Several. MisterCatLady has had 4 therapists in a short period. Crow has had multiple over 27 years but 16 with the current one. The pattern is cycling-then-settling: multiple early therapists, followed by a long sustained engagement once the match is found.

### Session frequency?
Crow mentions 6 years of intensive weekly therapy. Other posters do not specify frequency.

### Phase-based or organic?
No explicit phase model. The trajectories described are organic and iterative, shaped by therapist availability, fit, and the client's own readiness rather than by a formal treatment plan.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Not addressed directly. The forum context itself is a form of peer support — these are people supplementing professional therapy with community engagement.

## Quality notes

This is a forum thread with self-selected participants — people who are active in an online PTSD support community. The sample is biased toward people who have been in therapy (not those who have never accessed it), who are literate and motivated enough to participate in online discussion, and who are at a point of sufficient stability to reflect on their trajectories. Despite these biases, the data are valuable as naturalistic self-report: these are durations as experienced by clients, not as reported in research protocols. The range of voices (8 months to 27 years) provides genuine heterogeneity. No researcher prompted these answers; they are spontaneous peer-to-peer sharing.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
High. Every poster is someone currently in therapy or reflecting on therapy they have had. People who dropped out, who were harmed, or who could never access therapy are absent from this thread. The durations reported are durations of people who persisted.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Crow distinguishes these: 27 years elapsed, but only 6 years of intensive weekly work. The distinction matters — "27 years in therapy" and "6 years of intensive therapy" describe very different doses despite the same person. One step at a time's 6 years on-and-off similarly confounds elapsed time with dose.

### Therapy vs. life
Not addressed. All change is attributed to therapy. The forum context (peer support, community) is not acknowledged as a factor, though its existence suggests it plays a role.

### Access and context
Not directly addressed. No poster mentions cost, insurance, or financial barriers. This is notable — it may reflect the demographics of this particular forum, or it may be that the thread prompt did not invite that discussion. The absence is itself a data point: see the separate affordability thread (myptsd-2015-cant-afford-therapy) for the access dimension.

### Definition of endpoint
Implicit and varied. Panda Bear's "scared shitless but he never backed down" implies the endpoint is relational trust, not symptom reduction. Crow's 27 years suggest that therapy has become a sustained practice rather than a means to an endpoint. No poster defines "healed" or "recovered."

### Dropout data
The escalation gap is a form of data about cycling (not dropout per se): multiple posters describe leaving therapists who were not the right fit. This is framed as adaptive (searching for the right match), not as dropout. Whether the therapists they left would have described it as dropout is an open question.
