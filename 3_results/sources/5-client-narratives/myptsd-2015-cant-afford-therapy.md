## Summary

A MyPTSD forum thread in which community members discuss the inability to afford therapy. This thread shifts the entire frame of the review's primary question. The duration literature asks "how long does healing take?" This thread reveals that for a significant population, the prior question — "can I access healing at all?" — remains unanswered. sugnim describes losing insurance and facing a high deductible: "I cannot afford therapy, but I believe that it could help me." Friday provides a system-level analysis: $4,000-6,000 deductibles, employer avoidance of mental health coverage, and "about 500 work-arounds for being poor" — but notes the steep learning curve for navigating these alternatives. The Albatross describes completing whatever therapy was affordable, then pivoting to self-study and peer support, reporting significant progress: "come light years since therapy."

## Key claims
- Cost is a fundamental barrier to therapy access, not merely a complication — for some, it prevents treatment entirely
- Insurance structures (high deductibles, employer avoidance of coverage) systematically exclude people who need long-term treatment
- Workarounds exist (pro bono, sliding scale, community mental health, peer support, self-study) but require knowledge and persistence that people in crisis may lack
- Self-directed healing through peer support and self-study can produce significant progress — The Albatross: "come light years since therapy"
- The system fails the people who need it most: those with chronic, complex trauma requiring extended treatment are exactly those least likely to afford it

## Trajectory description

The trajectory here is not a therapy trajectory but an access trajectory. sugnim's trajectory: had therapy, lost insurance, cannot afford to continue. This is forced termination by financial constraint — a form of interruption that the continuity literature rarely addresses. The Albatross's trajectory: some therapy, then a pivot to self-directed work using peer support and self-study. This is not "dropout" — it is adaptation to a system that does not provide sufficient resources. Friday's description of "500 work-arounds for being poor" suggests a parallel trajectory: navigating the system as a full-time effort that competes with the therapy itself for energy and attention.

## Relevance to thesis

This source reframes the entire review. The duration literature implicitly assumes that treatment is accessible and that the question is about optimal length. This thread reveals that for many, the question is about access, not duration. The review must acknowledge this: any discussion of "how long does healing take?" must be contextualized by "for whom is healing even possible?" The Albatross's self-directed healing trajectory also challenges the therapy-centrism of the review: people heal through multiple channels, and therapy is one of them, not the only one.

This thread connects to Paris (2020) on access to personality disorder treatment and to Teamwork's pro bono monthly sessions (myptsd-2020-session-frequency) — the access constraint shapes not just whether therapy happens but at what dose.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Forced interruption by financial constraint. sugnim's therapy was interrupted not by clinical judgment but by loss of insurance. This is the most common form of interruption in real-world practice, and the one least addressed in the clinical literature.

### One modality or many?
Not relevant — the question is access to any modality.

### One therapist or several?
Not relevant.

### Session frequency?
Constrained by cost. See Teamwork (myptsd-2020-session-frequency) for the experience of financially-constrained frequency.

### Phase-based or organic?
The Albatross's trajectory — some therapy, then self-directed work — could be read as an involuntary phase model: professional treatment for what was affordable, then self-directed integration work. But this was not planned; it was forced by circumstance.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
The Albatross explicitly relies on peer support and self-study after therapy ended. Friday's "500 work-arounds" include non-therapeutic resources. This is the strongest evidence in the client layer that therapy-plus (or therapy-instead) is the reality for many.

## Quality notes

A forum thread about a structural problem, not a clinical question. The value is not in the clinical data (there is little) but in the frame shift: the duration question is moot when access is impossible. The thread is from 2015 and the US insurance landscape may have shifted somewhat, but the fundamental dynamic (long-term therapy is expensive, insurance is inadequate, the most traumatised people are often the least resourced) is structural and persistent. The thread likely underrepresents the most affected population: people who cannot afford therapy AND are not active in online support communities.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Inverted. This thread captures people who have NOT been able to sustain therapy — the opposite of the survivorship bias in the other threads. sugnim is not a therapy survivor but a system casualty. The Albatross survived despite the system, not because of it.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
sugnim's dose is zero. The Albatross's therapeutic dose was whatever was affordable, followed by non-therapeutic self-directed work. The elapsed time of the condition continues regardless of dose. This is the sharpest illustration: elapsed time of suffering is not the same as duration of treatment.

### Therapy vs. life
The Albatross explicitly attributes healing to self-study and peer support after therapy ended. This is the clearest evidence in the client layer that healing happens outside of therapy as well as within it.

### Access and context
This is the primary topic. Key access barriers described: loss of insurance, high deductibles ($4,000-6,000), employer avoidance of mental health coverage, lack of knowledge about alternatives, steep learning curve for navigating workarounds. The thread describes a system that structurally excludes people who need long-term treatment from accessing it.

### Definition of endpoint
The Albatross's "come light years since therapy" is an implicit endpoint definition: significant qualitative progress, not symptom elimination or diagnostic remission. sugnim's endpoint is more basic: being able to access any therapy at all.

### Dropout data
sugnim's situation is "dropout" from the system's perspective but forced termination from the client's perspective. This is the most important distinction in the dropout literature: not all termination is volitional. Financial forced termination is a form of structural violence against people who need continued care.
