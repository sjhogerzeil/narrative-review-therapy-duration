## Summary

A MyPTSD forum thread that directly asks the question the counterpoint layer's RCTs claim to answer: does EMDR resolve CPTSD, and how long does it take? The responses provide a striking naturalistic test of the EMDR evidence base. Even among clients who find EMDR helpful and effective, the reported durations are years (2-5+), not the 8-16 sessions reported in RCTs like Voorendonk et al. (2020). Freida explicitly names the discrepancy: "8-12 sessions applies to single trauma, but CPTSD involves multiple traumas requiring extended treatment." This thread is the client-perspective counterweight to the De Jongh (2024) counterpoint.

## Key claims
- EMDR is helpful for CPTSD but takes years, not sessions — Freida: "Five years and counting"
- The 8-12 session figure applies to single-incident trauma, not CPTSD — multiple posters make this distinction
- Standard EMDR protocols can be too intense for CPTSD — Skywatcher uses modified Flash EMDR after finding traditional EMDR overwhelming
- Stabilisation before processing is necessary in practice — Bodhi: 6 months stabilisation first, then EMDR
- The process "can and does take years" — somerandomguy explicitly warns newcomers it is not a quick fix

## Duration data
- Freida: 5+ years of EMDR, still ongoing, describes it as "working well"
- Skywatcher: 4 years in, using modified Flash EMDR
- somerandomguy: years (unspecified exact duration), warns it is not quick
- osiris: 2 years of EMDR + inner child work + tapping
- Bodhi: 6 months stabilisation + ongoing EMDR (total duration unspecified)
- Chris-duck: 7 months active processing

## Trajectory description

The trajectories described follow a pattern that the counterpoint literature claims is unnecessary: stabilisation first, then paced processing over an extended period. Bodhi explicitly describes 6 months of stabilisation before EMDR processing could even begin — directly contradicting Van Vliet et al. (2021), who found no benefit from stabilisation exercises in an RCT context. Skywatcher's trajectory involves modifying the EMDR protocol itself (switching to Flash EMDR) because standard EMDR was too activating — suggesting that the protocol as tested in RCTs does not match the protocol as used in clinical practice with complex cases.

The overall pattern: extended stabilisation and/or relationship-building, followed by paced processing (not the intensive daily format of Voorendonk et al.), with ongoing work measured in years.

## Relevance to thesis

This is one of the most important sources in the entire review. It directly tests the counterpoint claim (EMDR resolves CPTSD in 8-16 sessions) against lived experience and finds an enormous gap. The gap is not because EMDR does not work — these clients report it does — but because CPTSD involves multiple layered traumas that each require processing, because stabilisation is needed in practice (whatever the RCTs say), and because the relationship and pacing that make processing tolerable take time to develop.

**Critical finding:** Even clients who endorse EMDR as effective report treatment durations of YEARS, not the 8-16 sessions from RCTs. This is the RCT-reality gap in action. The RCTs may be measuring what happens in a controlled protocol with selected participants; the forum captures what happens in real clinical practice with complex presentations.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Not directly addressed. The durations reported (2-5+ years) suggest continuous engagement, but the thread does not explicitly discuss breaks or interruptions.

### One modality or many?
Primarily EMDR, but with significant modifications: Skywatcher uses Flash EMDR, osiris combines EMDR with inner child work and tapping. Even within the "EMDR" label, the actual clinical practice is multi-modal and adapted.

### One therapist or several?
Not addressed.

### Session frequency?
Not specified by most posters. The distinction is important: 5 years of weekly EMDR is approximately 250 sessions — vastly more than the 8-16 sessions in RCTs. Without frequency data, the actual dose remains uncertain.

### Phase-based or organic?
Bodhi's trajectory is explicitly phase-based: 6 months stabilisation, then processing. This matches the Herman/Van der Hart model and contradicts the van Vliet et al. (2021) finding that stabilisation adds nothing.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
osiris combines EMDR with inner child work and tapping, suggesting adjuncts are common. The forum itself is a form of peer support.

## Quality notes

Same self-selection biases as the other forum threads: these are people who are in therapy, active in online community, and able to reflect on their process. The specific value of this thread is its direct topical relevance: it asks exactly the question the counterpoint literature claims to answer, and the answers diverge dramatically from the RCT data. The thread is not evidence in the scientific sense, but it is a powerful illustration of the gap between controlled research and clinical reality. The posters who caution newcomers ("it's not a quick fix") are performing a function the RCT literature does not: managing expectations based on lived experience.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
High. These are people who have stayed in EMDR for years. People who tried EMDR and left after a few sessions — whether because it did not work, was too overwhelming, or for other reasons — are not represented. The thread may undercount treatment failures.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Critical issue. Freida's "five years and counting" is elapsed time. Without session frequency, the dose (total sessions) is unknown. If weekly, that is 250+ sessions. The RCTs report 8-16 sessions in intensive formats (sometimes daily). The comparison between "5 years" and "8 sessions" is misleading if the former represents weekly sessions and the latter represents daily sessions. But even adjusting for frequency, the gap is enormous: 250 sessions vs. 8-16.

### Therapy vs. life
Not addressed. All progress is attributed to EMDR/therapy. No poster discusses the role of life changes, relationships, or other non-therapeutic factors.

### Access and context
Not addressed in this thread.

### Definition of endpoint
Mixed. The thread title asks whether EMDR "largely resolved" CPTSD, which is a high bar (near-remission). Freida describes EMDR as "working well" after 5 years but does not claim resolution. Chris-duck's 7 months of active processing may suggest faster progress, but the endpoint is not defined. The implicit endpoint is "able to function and manage," not "symptom-free."

### Dropout data
Chris-duck cautions against discouraging newcomers with long timelines. This suggests awareness that the long durations reported could cause prospective clients to give up before starting — a form of information-driven non-engagement. No actual dropout data.
