## Summary

Foo's memoir chronicles her journey from childhood abuse (by both parents, who abandoned her as a teenager) through decades of therapy, a Complex PTSD diagnosis at age 30 (after 8 years with her first therapist), and the subsequent search through multiple modalities and therapists. The book is exceptionally relevant to the research question because Foo explicitly tracks duration, modality-switching, and what worked vs. what didn't — making it the closest thing to a naturalistic single-case study of complex trauma recovery in memoir form.

## Key claims

- Foo was in therapy with her first therapist, Samantha, for **eight years** before receiving a C-PTSD diagnosis — illustrating how long even accurate identification can take. — reviews/interviews
- After diagnosis, she tried multiple modalities: talk therapy, EMDR, support groups, yoga, meditation, somatic techniques. — reviews/interviews
- The turning point came when she found Dr. Jacob Ham, a relational psychoanalytic therapist specializing in trauma, who worked with her using IFS and EMDR, and critically, modeled co-regulation, attunement, and repair. — reviews/interviews
- Ham provided $6,400 worth of pro bono therapy — an explicit acknowledgment of the access/cost barrier. — reviews/interviews
- Intergenerational trauma is a major theme: Foo traces her parents' trauma histories in Malaysia and investigates how trauma transmits across generations. — reviews/interviews

## Trajectory description

The trajectory is nonlinear and marked by several distinct phases: (1) years of conventional talk therapy that helped with surface functioning but didn't address the core wound; (2) a diagnostic breakthrough that reframed everything; (3) a period of modality-shopping and therapist-switching after diagnosis; (4) finding a relational therapist (Ham) who could work at the level of co-regulation and attachment repair; (5) integration of multiple approaches including EMDR, IFS, and relational work. The memoir ends not with "cured" but with a hard-won, ongoing capacity for connection and self-compassion.

## Relevance to thesis

This is the single most relevant memoir for the blog. Foo directly addresses: the length of healing (decade-plus), the necessity of modality-switching (not as failure but as developmental progression), the role of the therapeutic relationship beyond technique, the access barrier (pro bono therapy as turning point), and the endpoint question (not symptom-free but relationally capable). Her story challenges the notion that any single modality "works" for complex trauma and supports the thesis that healing is measured in years to decades.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Foo's therapy was continuous but involved breaks between therapists. The switch from Samantha to Ham was a deliberate shift, not a forced interruption. The narrative suggests continuity of intent (always in some form of healing work) even when specific therapeutic relationships ended.

### One modality or many?
Many. Talk therapy, EMDR, IFS, support groups, yoga, meditation, somatic work. Foo's story is the paradigm case for modality-switching as developmental progression rather than failure. Different modalities served different needs at different stages.

### One therapist or several?
Several. At least Samantha (8 years), then Dr. Jacob Ham. The switch was necessary: Samantha provided stability but couldn't offer the trauma-specific relational repair that Ham could. Different therapists served different developmental functions.

### Phase-based or organic?
The trajectory is roughly phase-consistent (stabilization with Samantha → diagnosis → modality exploration → deep relational repair with Ham → integration) but the phases were not planned — they emerged organically and were only visible in retrospect.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Therapy-plus. Foo explicitly tried yoga, meditation, support groups, and journalistic investigation of her family history as adjuncts. The writing of the memoir itself appears to have been part of the healing process (narrative integration). Community and chosen family are described as essential, not supplementary.

## Quality notes

As a memoir, this source has the inherent strengths and limitations of first-person narrative: deep access to subjective experience and temporal arc, but no external validation of outcomes and inevitable retrospective reconstruction. Foo's journalistic training (she is a former producer for This American Life and Snap Judgment) gives the narrative unusual rigor — she interviews researchers and clinicians, grounds her experience in the literature, and is self-critical about her own interpretive biases. The book was widely reviewed and praised by both clinicians and trauma survivors.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
This is a recovery memoir — by definition, it captures someone who made it through. Foo acknowledges that her journey required resources (eventually pro bono therapy, a supportive partner, journalistic skills that gave her access to experts) that many trauma survivors lack. The memoir does not claim universality.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The 8-year figure with Samantha is calendar time; actual session frequency is not specified in available reviews. The distinction matters — 8 years of weekly therapy is ~400 sessions; 8 years of monthly therapy is ~96. The elapsed-time figure alone is insufficient without dose information. [Requires full text.]

### Therapy vs. life
Foo explicitly credits non-therapy factors: chosen family, her relationship with her partner, the investigative journey to Malaysia, the writing process itself. The memoir resists attributing change solely to therapy — it presents healing as an ecology of relationships, practices, and meaning-making.

### Access and context
Directly addressed. The $6,400 pro bono therapy from Dr. Ham is flagged as a turning point — and as something most people cannot access. Foo's story implicitly raises the question: what happens to people who can't find a Jacob Ham willing to work for free?

[upgraded 2026-03-19, secondary source obtained] The $6,400 figure is confirmed across multiple interview sources. Ham provided pro bono therapy explicitly because he recognized the clinical and educational value of recording sessions for the book — a unique access pathway that is not replicable for most survivors.

### Definition of endpoint
Not symptom-free. Foo's endpoint is relational: the capacity to love and be loved, to regulate in connection with others, to hold her history without being consumed by it. This is a functional/structural endpoint, not a symptom-reduction endpoint. The memoir's title itself — "What My Bones Know" — suggests the endpoint is embodied knowledge, not the absence of symptoms.

### Dropout data
Not applicable in the traditional sense, but Foo describes "breaking up with therapists" — voluntary terminations that could be read as dropout from the therapist's perspective but were necessary developmental moves from her perspective. This reframes dropout as potentially adaptive.
