## Summary

Spring defines recovery not as a destination but as "a journey, an orientation, a direction" — heading north, at whatever pace, by whatever route. She describes her own 15-year transformation from "regularly trying to kill myself, having full-blown dissociative identity disorder" to "living a life that's certainly not perfect, but it is undeniably good." She explicitly acknowledges that many survivors have been in therapy 10-30 years without comparable results, attributing this not to personal failure but to systemic inadequacy of therapeutic support. Recovery is defined functionally: living mostly in the "green zone," remembering trauma without reliving it, experiencing joy alongside grief, and making choices from the "front brain." She strongly critiques both the "you'll never recover" and the "just be positive" camps as caricatures.

## Key claims

- 15-year arc from suicidal DID to a good life: "In the last fifteen years I've gone from regularly trying to kill myself, having full-blown dissociative identity disorder ... to now living a life that's certainly not perfect, but it is undeniably good" — opening section
- People in therapy 10-30 years without recovery are not failing; they lack adequate support: "I've been in therapy for ten, twenty, thirty years and I still haven't experienced anything like what you're talking about" — survivor voices section
- Recovery is direction, not destination: "recovery is a journey, an orientation, a direction, not a specific location" — journey metaphor section
- Slow or absent progress reflects systemic failure, not individual failure: "a lot of it is our failure as a society to adequately provide public transport to enable people to head north" — societal responsibility section
- Arbitrary timescales for recovery are harmful: objects to DSM-5's "Persistent Complex Bereavement Disorder" and its 6-month threshold — timescales section
- Recovery means resolving effects of trauma, not erasing history: "I'm talking about resolving our trauma, not pretending it didn't happen" — redefining recovery section
- Gratitude and grief must coexist: "gratitude without grief is simplistic twaddle" — gratitude section
- Joy expands to fill empty spaces over time and is "the best antidote to pain" — joy section
- Physical consequences of trauma are permanent (infertility from childhood abuse) and recovery means living meaningfully despite them — childlessness section

## Trajectory description

Spring describes her trajectory through an extended journey metaphor (London to Scotland): nonlinear, with detours, stops, periods of going the wrong way, and stretches of crawling on hands and knees. At any given point an observer could have questioned whether she was heading north at all ("Actually I was on the M23 heading south towards Brighton"). The trajectory includes: (1) years of severe dysfunction and suicidality; (2) a long period of slow, often invisible progress; (3) arrival at a life characterised by joy, self-compassion, calm nervous system regulation, and the capacity to choose rather than react. She emphasises that different people travel at different speeds, by different routes, and that stops to "pitch a tent" are adaptive given inadequate support — not failure.

## Relevance to thesis

This source powerfully supports the thesis on multiple fronts: (1) it provides a 15-year first-person duration data point; (2) it reframes slow/long recovery as normal and expected rather than pathological; (3) it shifts blame for "failure to recover" from the individual to the system; (4) it defines recovery functionally rather than as symptom absence, which has direct implications for how we measure duration (if the endpoint is "a good life" rather than "no symptoms," the timeline is necessarily longer); (5) it acknowledges that some people spend decades in therapy without recovery, attributing this to access rather than personal deficiency. The source complicates simplistic claims about "how long therapy takes" by insisting that the question cannot be separated from the adequacy of the therapy provided.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
The journey metaphor explicitly includes interruptions: "If we stop for a while in Watford, or Nottingham, or York en route, then that's okay too. Sometimes we need to stop and refuel. Sometimes the car breaks down." Spring normalises interruption as part of the recovery trajectory rather than a failure of it. Her own journey included periods of "heading south" and doubling back. No specific data on her own therapy interruptions in this episode.

### One modality or many?
The journey metaphor implies multiple modalities: "Some parts of the journey we do by train, some by car, some we hitch-hike, and some we walk. And yes, some of them we crawl forwards almost imperceptibly on our hands and knees." No specific modalities named. The emphasis is on adequacy and availability of support rather than on specific therapeutic approaches.

### One therapist or several?
No data in this episode. The focus is on systemic provision of support ("public transport") rather than individual therapeutic relationships.

### Session frequency?
No data.

### Phase-based or organic?
Organic. The journey metaphor is explicitly non-prescriptive: "I decided what I wanted recovery to look like, and I decided how to get there." No phase model is described or implied. Recovery is self-directed and idiosyncratic.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Implicitly therapy-plus. Spring describes building joy through deliberate exposure to pleasurable experiences (nature, sunsets, her dog), holding grief alongside gratitude, and campaigning/advocacy work as part of her recovered life. The emphasis on "building good things in the gaps" (where trauma left empty spaces) suggests that non-therapeutic activities are integral to recovery, not supplementary. She also credits "rich and mutual relationships" (from the falling-down episode) as part of the trajectory.

## Quality notes

First-person podcast monologue; single case with rhetorical appeals to others' experience. Spring's account is deeply phenomenological and internally consistent across multiple episodes. The source's primary value is in its redefinition of recovery (direction, not destination; functional, not symptom-free) and its systemic critique (people don't fail to recover; systems fail to provide adequate support). The 15-year figure is reliable as self-report but represents elapsed time, not dose. The references to other survivors in therapy 10-30 years are anecdotal and unverifiable but reflect a common clinical reality. The source is strongest as advocacy and weakest as evidence — but its reframing of the recovery question is analytically valuable for the synthesis.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Explicitly acknowledged and partially addressed. Spring names herself as someone who recovered and recognises that this creates a selection effect. She attempts to mitigate it by attributing others' lack of recovery to systemic failure rather than personal inadequacy: "their journey speed wasn't all down to them." However, the fundamental survivorship bias remains — she is telling this story because she recovered.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The 15-year figure is elapsed calendar time from breakdown to current state. No session frequency, total session count, or intensity data is provided in this episode. The journey metaphor explicitly includes periods of stasis, detour, and crawling — implying that much of the elapsed time was not spent in active therapeutic work. This makes the dose-duration distinction especially important for this source.

### Therapy vs. life
Strongly acknowledged. Spring credits non-therapeutic factors: building joy through nature, hobbies, creativity, relationships, her dog. The childlessness section describes building "good things in the gaps" through deliberate life-construction. Her advocacy work ("making a difference to people's lives through my work") is presented as both a consequence and a driver of recovery. The source resists attributing recovery solely to therapy and frames it as an ecology of therapeutic and non-therapeutic growth.

### Access and context
Central theme of this episode. Spring explicitly frames inadequate recovery as a systemic access problem: survivors "crawling through mud" with "heavy rucksacks" because society fails to provide "public transport" (adequately funded therapy). She critiques the implicit blame placed on individuals for not recovering when the real barrier is availability of adequate support. She names NHS services, the voluntary sector, and social services as responsible for providing resources. This is the most access-conscious of the three episodes.

### Definition of endpoint
Functional and experiential, explicitly defined. Recovery means: (1) living mostly in the "green zone" (window of tolerance); (2) remembering trauma as "something that happened" without reliving it; (3) experiencing increasing moments of joy; (4) managing stress effectively; (5) self-compassion and self-acceptance; (6) making choices from the "front brain" rather than reacting from fear. Recovery does *not* mean: erasing history, achieving a pain-free existence, restoring lost childhood, or eliminating all struggle. Spring explicitly states: "recovery is about movement, about a direction that we're headed in, not a particular location."

### Dropout data
No dropout data. Spring references survivors who have been in therapy 10-30 years without recovery, but frames this as inadequate support rather than dropout. No data on people who left therapy, abandoned the journey, or stopped seeking help.
