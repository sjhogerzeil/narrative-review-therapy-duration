## Summary

Spring documents a year in which she returned to therapy after approximately three years of stable, joyful post-therapy functioning (2017-2019). The precipitant was a violent rape by a stranger, followed immediately by the COVID-19 pandemic, which blocked trauma processing through isolation. She had not been in therapy since 2016, when she completed work related to her 2015 divorce. The episode provides a uniquely documented case of episodic therapy for complex trauma: prior therapeutic gains were not erased by new trauma, but new trauma required a new episode of therapeutic work. Crucially, the second episode was qualitatively different from the first — Spring entered therapy with psychoeducation, self-compassion, and active engagement strategies that were entirely absent during her 2005 breakdown.

- Left therapy in 2016 after completing divorce-related work; 2017-2019 was a period of stable, generative functioning — timeline section
- Raped by a stranger shortly before the pandemic; brain immediately dissociated the event as a survival mechanism — opening section

- Physical symptoms (chronic pain, weight gain, gut problems, infections, lethargy) preceded psychological awareness of the trauma — body section
- GP suspected ovarian or bowel cancer based on symptom presentation — GP section

- Active therapeutic engagement (notes before sessions, write-ups after, diagrams, reflections, plans) characterised the second episode — engagement section
- "Recovery is my best revenge" — repeated refrain throughout

## Trajectory description

The trajectory documented here is explicitly episodic and nonlinear:

1. **First episode (2005-2016)**: Breakdown, ~9 years of therapy, processing childhood trauma, integration of dissociative parts, divorce work. Ended with stable functioning.
2. **Stable period (2017-2019)**: "Comfortable relationship with my past," no overt dissociativity, focus on building joy — nature, hobbies, creativity, relationships, work, physical health. "Recovery is my best revenge."
3. **New trauma (pre-pandemic)**: Rape by a stranger. Immediate dissociation. Amnesia for the event.
4. **Pandemic destabilisation**: Isolation prevented processing; physical symptoms emerged; dissociated trauma began pressing for consciousness.
5. **Second therapy episode (summer 2020-)**: Return to therapy. Parts re-emerged. The rape narrative surfaced through dissociative parts. Compounded by house move, falls, broken ribs, isolation in the Highlands.
6. **Qualitative difference**: Unlike the first episode, Spring understood what was happening, had self-compassion, and actively engaged with the therapeutic process. "I knew what was happening. My trauma reactions made sense to me."

The key finding is that the trajectory is not a single arc but a series of episodes, and that prior therapeutic work provides a foundation that makes subsequent episodes qualitatively different — faster, more informed, less shame-laden.

## Relevance to thesis

This source is uniquely valuable because it documents what happens *after* the long therapy described in the other episodes. It shows that: (1) healing from complex trauma does not immunise against future trauma; (2) new trauma can reactivate dissociative processes even after years of integration; (3) prior therapeutic gains are retained and transform the quality of subsequent crises; (4) the total healing timeline extends beyond any single episode of therapy. The ~15-year total elapsed time (2005-2021) with therapy in two distinct episodes supports the thesis that healing takes years-to-decades. The episodic pattern complicates any simple "X years of therapy" framing — the question is not just "how long" but "how many episodes, over what total span?"

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Explicitly interrupted. Therapy occurred in two distinct episodes: (1) ~2005-2016 and (2) summer 2020-ongoing. The gap (2017-2019) was a period of stable functioning without therapy, not a crisis-driven interruption. The return was precipitated by new trauma, not by relapse of old symptoms. This is the clearest case in the dataset of episodic therapy as a pattern rather than continuous long-term treatment.

### One modality or many?
No specific modalities named in either episode. The second episode is described in terms of active client engagement (notes, diagrams, journalling, reflections, plans) and relational processing. The first episode involved processing childhood trauma and integrating dissociative parts. The contrast between the two episodes suggests different therapeutic approaches or at least different client stances within therapy.

### One therapist or several?
Different therapists for each episode. The 2020 therapist is new: Spring describes the first session as an assessment ("why are you here?") with a therapist who does not know her history. The first-episode therapist is referenced separately in other episodes. This represents a deliberate choice to begin anew with a different therapist after a multi-year gap.

### Session frequency?
No explicit data. The 2020 therapy appears to be at least weekly, based on the level of engagement described (notes before each session, write-ups after). No frequency data for the first episode in this source.

### Phase-based or organic?
The two-episode structure is unplanned and organic — the second episode was precipitated by unpredictable new trauma, not by a phased treatment plan. Within the second episode, Spring describes an initially exploratory phase ("I don't quite know what the matter is with me") that was overtaken by the emergence of dissociative parts and the rape narrative. The active engagement (notes, plans, diagrams) suggests some self-directed structure, but the overall shape was determined by the trauma surfacing on its own timeline.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Strongly therapy-plus. During the stable period (2017-2019), Spring built recovery through non-therapeutic means: "the joy of nature, of hobbies, of creativity and productivity and generativity, of making a difference to people's lives through my work, of nurturing rich and mutual relationships, of improving my physical health, and of course white beach walks with my dog." During the second therapy episode, she used journalling, diagramming, and self-directed analysis as active adjuncts. Advocacy work and writing are described as integral to recovery: "recovery for other people too."

## Quality notes

This is the most phenomenologically rich of the three Spring episodes, documenting the real-time experience of a trauma crisis in a previously-recovered survivor. The account is internally consistent with Spring's other episodes and provides rare longitudinal data: a documented stable period between therapy episodes, followed by a return precipitated by identifiable new trauma. The source's primary limitation is that it was recorded during the second episode (January 2021), before completion — the outcome is unknown. Spring acknowledges this: "It's early days." The account of physical symptoms being mistaken for cancer is clinically significant and illustrates the somatic presentation of dissociated trauma. The vulnerability and specificity of the account (rape, broken ribs, isolation) lend credibility but also raise the question of generalisability — this is an extreme set of compounding adversities.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Present but complicated. Spring is telling a recovery-in-progress story, not a completed recovery story. She recovered from the first episode but is mid-crisis in the second. The account is more honest about uncertainty than a pure survivorship narrative: "It's early days" and "There is still much healing work to be done." The prior recovery provides credibility ("therapy works") but the new crisis complicates any simple success narrative.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The episodic structure makes dose-duration especially important. Total elapsed time from first breakdown: ~15 years. Actual time in therapy: ~9 years (first episode) + ongoing (second episode). Stable period without therapy: ~3 years. The 15-year elapsed figure includes substantial non-therapy time. The first episode's dose is approximately 450 sessions if weekly; the second episode's dose is unknown.

### Therapy vs. life
Strongly acknowledged. The 2017-2019 stable period was built through life, not therapy: relationships, nature, creativity, work, physical health. The new destabilisation was caused by life (rape, pandemic), not by therapeutic failure. Spring explicitly credits both therapy and non-therapeutic factors in recovery. The writing/advocacy work is presented as both a product of recovery and a contributor to it. The entanglement is total — therapy and life are inseparable components of the trajectory.

### Access and context
Explicitly acknowledged: "I count myself to be one of the lucky unlucky ones ... one thing I am grateful for is the support and the resources I have at my disposal." Spring distinguishes herself from "the unlucky unlucky ones, who don't have the support and the resources that I do." She also notes the practical barrier of "starting therapy during lockdown is no easy task." The ability to access a new therapist in summer 2020, to take time off work, to retreat to the Highlands, and to maintain a platform for advocacy all reflect significant privilege that she names directly.

### Definition of endpoint
The first episode's endpoint was functional: "a comfortable relationship with my past and with no overt dissociativity — living mostly joined-up rather than internally segregated." The second episode's endpoint is not yet defined — Spring is mid-process. The stable period's functioning is described in terms of joy, generativity, relationships, and reduced need for dissociation — a functional/experiential endpoint rather than symptom-based. The account implicitly suggests that the endpoint is not permanent and may need to be re-established after new trauma.

### Dropout data
No dropout data. Spring's return to therapy was voluntary and self-initiated, not prompted by a system. No data on others' experiences with episodic therapy or therapy return after stable periods.
