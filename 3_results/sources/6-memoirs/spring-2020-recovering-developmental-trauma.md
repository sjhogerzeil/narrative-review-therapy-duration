## Summary

Spring argues that recovery from complex developmental trauma follows the same logic as child development: it is slow, proceeds through sequential stages that cannot be skipped, and looks like "nothing is happening" from the inside even as enormous progress is accumulating. She uses her own 9.5-year therapy journey as the anchor case. The core claim is that survivors missed key developmental stages (affect regulation, distress tolerance, emotional labelling) during childhood abuse, and therapy provides the environment to go back and build those capacities from scratch — a process that necessarily takes years. Progress is invisible week-to-week, just as a baby's daily bath or mealtime doesn't look like "learning" but is developmental work.

## Key claims

- Therapy for complex developmental trauma took 9.5 years, was "highly, highly effective" but "at the time it often felt ineffective" — developmental analogy section
- Survivors must re-learn developmental stages missed during childhood abuse: being soothed, calming down, tolerating distress, labelling emotions — "what's happened after trauma" section
- Progress follows a developmental rate unique to each individual; benchmarking against others is both harmful and meaningless — foster children analogy / Health Visitor section
- The gaps between therapy sessions are "as developmentally important as the therapy sessions themselves" — feeding/waiting analogy section
- Therapy is fundamentally about building neural networks for affect regulation through repeated relational experience, not about unlocking specific traumatic memories — "session after session" section
- Recovery requires a "zone of proximal development" (Vygotsky): not too much comfort, not too much overwhelm — toddler/walking section

## Duration data

- **9.5 years of therapy** — explicit, repeated: "I had therapy for 9.5 years – that's a very long time – and I worked very hard for the whole of that time"
- Developmental analogy implies 18-25 years as the natural arc from "infant" to "adult" functioning: "it takes 18, 25, more years!"
- Week-to-week invisibility of progress: "Week in, week out, session after session it didn't really feel that I was doing anything"

## Trajectory description

The trajectory is described as a developmental arc — slow, cumulative, and invisible in real-time. Spring compares it directly to infant-to-adult development: from inability to coordinate basic movements, through toddling, to abstract thought and strategic planning. Key features of the trajectory:

1. **Long period of apparent stasis**: "I didn't feel like I was improving. I didn't feel like I was growing. I didn't feel like I was moving. Just rooted to the spot, stuck, and liable to burst into tears."
2. **Invisible micro-progress**: Each session builds neural networks for affect regulation, like a baby playing with a spoon — enormous learning that doesn't look like learning.
3. **Spiral/cumulative**: Skills build on each other in sequence; you cannot skip stages ("you can't do the next stage until you've conquered this stage").
4. **Individual rate**: Different people develop at different speeds, all within the normal range; comparing is meaningless.
5. **Retrospectively visible**: The progress is enormous when viewed from outside or in hindsight, but invisible from within.

## Relevance to thesis

This source directly and powerfully supports the thesis that healing from complex developmental trauma takes years. Spring provides both first-person testimony (9.5 years) and a theoretical framework (developmental-rate metaphor) that explains *why* it takes so long: survivors are literally rebuilding missed developmental stages, which cannot be rushed any more than a baby can be rushed to walk. The source also addresses the subjective experience of "nothing happening" during long-term therapy — a key reason people (and their therapists) give up prematurely. The developmental metaphor reframes slow progress as normal rather than pathological.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No data on interruptions during the 9.5-year therapy period. The framing implies continuous engagement ("week in, week out, session after session"), suggesting uninterrupted weekly therapy throughout. No mention of breaks, pauses, or resumptions within this period.

### One modality or many?
No specific modality named. The description emphasizes relational affect regulation ("having the therapist just patiently and gently and softly saying, 'Slow it down' and calming everything down again") and the building of neural networks through repeated co-regulation. This suggests a relational/psychodynamic approach, but Spring does not name it. No mention of modality-switching within the 9.5 years.

### One therapist or several?
Implicit: one therapist for the 9.5 years. Spring refers to "my therapist" (singular) who discussed her with "her supervisor." No mention of switching therapists during this period.

### Session frequency?
Implied weekly: "Week in, week out, session after session" and the discussion of "having to wait a whole week between therapy sessions." No explicit statement of frequency, but the weekly rhythm is treated as the default frame throughout.

### Phase-based or organic?
Organic/developmental. Spring explicitly rejects the idea of spectacular breakthroughs or technique-driven processing in favour of a gradual developmental model. The phases are the universal developmental stages (affect regulation, distress tolerance, mentalisation) rather than a planned therapeutic phase model. The sequence is inherent in the developmental logic ("you can't do the next stage until you've conquered this stage") rather than imposed by a treatment protocol.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Primarily therapy-focused in this episode. Spring mentions the importance of practicing self-soothing between sessions and being in "the right environment" with "the right people," but does not describe specific adjuncts (writing, community, medication, body work). Other episodes in the series address adjuncts more directly.

## Quality notes

This is a first-person podcast monologue — a single case, self-reported, with no external validation of outcomes or timeline. Spring's account is internally consistent and detailed, drawing on extensive foster-care experience to construct the developmental analogy. As memoir/advocacy, it prioritizes lived experience and accessibility over methodological rigour. The developmental metaphor is compelling but unfalsifiable — it can accommodate any duration as "normal." The source is most valuable for its phenomenological description of what long-term therapy *feels like* from the inside (invisible progress, frustration, the subjective experience of stasis) and for the 9.5-year duration data point with the explicit framing that this was "highly, highly effective."

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Explicitly present: Spring is telling this story because she recovered. She acknowledges that others may feel "too far out of reach" when seeing her current functioning, and tries to mitigate this by emphasizing that she "was in a wheelchair" and started from the same place. But the fundamental selection effect remains — this is a recovery narrative by someone who recovered.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Duration is reported as calendar time (9.5 years). Session frequency is implied weekly but not stated. If weekly, that represents approximately 475-500 sessions — an enormous dose. Spring does not distinguish between calendar time and session count; the developmental metaphor frames elapsed time as the relevant unit (just as child development is measured in months/years, not in "hours of parenting").

### Therapy vs. life
Spring attributes change primarily to the therapeutic relationship and the between-session practice it enables. She does not discuss life events, relationships, work, or other non-therapeutic factors as contributors to healing in this episode. The developmental metaphor frames the therapist as the "parent" and therapy as the "environment" — other environmental factors are not addressed.

### Access and context
Not addressed in this episode. No mention of cost, insurance, NHS waiting lists, geographic access, or who can and cannot access 9.5 years of therapy. This is a notable absence given that the duration she describes would be financially prohibitive for most people.

### Definition of endpoint
Functional: Spring describes recovery as being able to "regulate your emotions, to be able to think complex thoughts and empathise with others and compute multiple potential scenarios" — developmental capacities, not symptom absence. She uses the metaphor of progressing from baby to adult: the endpoint is adult-level functioning (emotional regulation, mentalisation, self-compassion), not the removal of a disorder. She now does "regular 10Ks" emotionally — but the emphasis is on capacity, not on the absence of distress.

### Dropout data
No dropout data. Spring mentions that therapists are "tempted to look around at everyone else's client, and measure their milestones" in peer supervision, implying that some clients don't progress — but she does not discuss people who left therapy or for whom therapy did not work.
