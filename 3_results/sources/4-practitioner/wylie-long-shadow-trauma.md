## Summary

Mary Sykes Wylie profiles Bessel van der Kolk's campaign to establish Developmental Trauma Disorder (DTD) as a distinct diagnostic category. The article argues that children exposed to chronic interpersonal trauma present fundamentally differently from PTSD — with pervasive affect dysregulation, identity disturbance, and relational impairment — yet are currently diagnosed with a patchwork of labels (ADHD, ODD, bipolar, etc.) that miss the underlying aetiology. The key implication for treatment duration: programmes designed for this population (TARGET, ARC) "tend not to be simple, short, or cheap." The article does not provide specific duration data but constructs the argument for WHY different, longer treatment is needed by establishing that the population has qualitatively different needs.

## Key claims
- Nearly 80% of surveyed children in the study had been exposed to multiple and/or prolonged interpersonal trauma.
- "These kids have serious problems with affect regulation... but they don't have PTSD" — developmental trauma produces a distinct clinical presentation that does not fit standard PTSD criteria.
- Treatment programmes designed for developmental trauma (TARGET, ARC) "tend not to be simple, short, or cheap."
- "There are 10 times as many kids getting abused in America than soldiers fighting in Afghanistan and Iraq" — developmental trauma is a larger public health problem than combat PTSD, yet receives far less attention and research funding.
- The PTSD diagnosis, designed around single-incident adult trauma, is inadequate for capturing the effects of chronic developmental trauma.

## Duration data
- No explicit duration data (session counts, year ranges, or dose-response findings).
- Implicit duration argument: the statement that appropriate programmes are "not simple, short, or cheap" acknowledges that adequate treatment exceeds brief therapy. The comparison to combat PTSD (which itself requires extended treatment) and the framing of injuries as developmental and pervasive imply multi-year treatment needs.

## Trajectory description

No specific trajectory described. The article establishes the nature of the problem (developmental, pervasive, affecting multiple domains simultaneously) rather than mapping the healing process. The implication is that treatment must address affect regulation, identity, relational capacity, and physiological dysregulation — multiple domains that cannot be treated sequentially in a brief format.

## Relevance to thesis

Relevant as a foundational argument piece. While it provides no duration data, it constructs the diagnostic and conceptual case for why complex developmental trauma requires longer treatment than standard PTSD. The argument that these children/adults are routinely misdiagnosed (given ADHD, ODD, or bipolar labels) implies that treatment duration estimates based on PTSD protocols are applied to the wrong population. The scale argument (10x more abuse victims than combat veterans) frames extended treatment as a public health necessity, not a luxury. Supports the thesis indirectly: by establishing that the population is distinct, it establishes that duration estimates derived from PTSD research are inapplicable.

## Quality notes

This is a practitioner-focused magazine article (Psychotherapy Networker), not a peer-reviewed paper. It profiles van der Kolk's advocacy rather than presenting original research. The 80% figure comes from an unspecified survey; the "10 times as many" comparison is rhetorical. The article's value lies in framing — it establishes the conceptual case for distinct treatment needs — rather than in evidence. Should be weighted as practitioner journalism that accurately represents the DTD debate, not as empirical evidence for treatment duration.
