## Summary

Shedler and Gnaulati synthesise three independent large-scale datasets to argue that effective psychotherapy takes substantially longer than managed care and insurance systems allow. Their key evidence: (1) the Consumer Reports study (N=4,000+) showing dose-dependent improvement up to 2+ years; (2) Drew Westen's survey of 270 experienced therapists (avg 18 years experience) reporting 52-75 sessions for successful treatment; (3) Lambert's OQ-45 dataset (N=10,000+) showing 50% clinically significant change at 21 sessions and 75% at 40+ sessions. They frame the discrepancy between research evidence and clinical practice as the "evidence-based therapy myth" — a managed-care-driven narrative that misrepresents research findings to justify session limits. The article includes a case study of a combat veteran (Ryan) whose real therapeutic work could not begin until after a year of trust-building.

## Key claims
- "The longer people stayed in therapy, the more they improved" — Consumer Reports finding, dose-dependent with no ceiling at 2 years.
- Experienced therapists (including CBT therapists) report 52-75 sessions (1-1.5 years weekly) for successful treatment; CBT therapists specifically report 33-44 sessions (6-12 months).
- 50% of clients show clinically significant change at 21 sessions (~6 months weekly); 75% at 40+ sessions (~1 year weekly).
- "A basic flaw in the logic of instruction-manual therapies is that clients come to therapy primarily for symptom reduction" — real clients have attachment insecurities, personality quirks, and psychological defenses.
- "Four decades of psychotherapy research have shown that, after treatment completion, more than half of patients remain depressed, and of those who improve by the end of treatment, about 40 percent experience a relapse."
- "7 out of 10 people who receive so-called evidence-based therapy for depression do not improve, or relapse quickly."
- With traumatised clients, "the real story only comes out after about a year of weekly therapy sessions, because, honestly speaking, it takes that long to establish the sort of trust traumatized vets need to start opening up."

## Duration data
- **Consumer Reports study (N=4,000+):** Meaningful change began at ~6 months. Clients at 1 year did substantially better. Those at 2 years improved still more. No ceiling effect reported.
- **Westen survey (N=270 therapists, avg 18 years experience):** Median successful treatment 52-75 sessions (~1-1.5 years weekly). CBT therapists reported 33-44 sessions for successful real-world CBT (6-12 months weekly).
- **Lambert OQ-45 study (N=10,000+):** 50% clinically significant change at 21 sessions (~6 months weekly); 75% at 40+ sessions (~1 year weekly).
- **Case study (Ryan, combat veteran):** Real therapeutic engagement began after ~1 year of trust-building. Total treatment duration not specified but implied to extend well beyond 1 year.
- **Depression relapse data:** >50% remain depressed after treatment completion; ~40% relapse among those who improve. 7 in 10 receiving EBT for depression do not improve or relapse quickly.

## Trajectory description

The article describes a trajectory in which the early phase of therapy (first 6 months) produces some measurable change, but the most meaningful work occurs later. For trauma clients specifically, the first year may be entirely devoted to trust-building — the "real story" does not emerge until sufficient relational safety is established. The trajectory is dose-dependent and open-ended: improvement continues for as long as treatment continues, with no ceiling reported at 2 years. The Ryan case study illustrates this: surface-level symptom management preceded the emergence of the actual traumatic material, which required a qualitatively different kind of therapeutic engagement.

## Relevance to thesis

Central to the thesis. This article directly answers the primary research question ("how long does healing take?") with multiple independent datasets converging on the same answer: 6 months minimum for measurable change, 1 year for the majority, 2+ years for continued improvement. The practitioner-survey data (Westen) is particularly valuable because it captures what experienced clinicians — including CBT clinicians — observe in real-world practice, as opposed to the artificial conditions of RCTs. The critique of the "evidence-based therapy myth" provides rhetorical framing for the gap between what research shows and what systems allow. The depression relapse data (7 in 10 fail or relapse) directly challenges the adequacy of brief treatment for complex presentations.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Implicitly continuous. The dose-response data assumes weekly attendance without interruption. The Ryan case study describes sustained weekly therapy over 1+ years. No discussion of breaks, pauses, or interrupted treatment.

### One modality or many?
Mixed. The article synthesises data across modalities and argues that the duration finding holds regardless of modality — including CBT, which practitioners report takes 33-44 sessions in real-world practice. The argument is modality-agnostic: all approaches take longer than systems allow.

### One therapist or several?
No data. The Ryan case study implies a single sustained therapeutic relationship, but this is not generalised.

### Session frequency?
Implicitly weekly. All session-to-outcome calculations assume weekly frequency (21 sessions = ~6 months; 40 sessions = ~1 year). No discussion of higher or lower frequency.

### Phase-based or organic?
Organic/emergent rather than structured. The Ryan case study describes a trajectory (trust-building → emergence of real material → deeper work) but this is not formalised as a phase model.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
No data. Treatment is discussed as standalone psychotherapy.

## Quality notes

This is a practitioner-focused magazine article, not a peer-reviewed paper. However, it synthesises three independent empirical datasets (Consumer Reports, Westen survey, Lambert OQ-45), all of which are well-known in the literature. The authors are credentialled (Shedler is a widely cited psychotherapy researcher). The rhetorical framing is polemical — "the evidence-based therapy myth" — which may overstate the case. The Consumer Reports data is self-report and self-selected; the Westen data is therapist-reported, not outcome-measured; the Lambert data is the strongest (N=10,000+, session-by-session OQ-45). The relapse statistics (7 in 10) aggregate across studies in a way that may overstate failure rates for specific disorders or modalities. Despite these limitations, the convergence across three independent sources lends credibility. Should be weighted as a well-sourced practitioner synthesis, not as primary research.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Present in all three datasets. The Consumer Reports study captures people who stayed in therapy and chose to respond to a survey. Lambert's data captures clients who continued attending sessions (those who dropped out contribute fewer data points). The Westen survey captures therapist perspectives on successful treatments (selection on the outcome). The conclusion "the longer people stayed, the more they improved" cannot distinguish between "staying longer causes more improvement" and "people who are improving are more likely to stay."

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Partially distinguished. Lambert's data counts sessions (dose); the Consumer Reports data reports elapsed time (months/years). Westen's data reports sessions. The implicit assumption is weekly frequency (1 session = 1 week), which conflates dose and elapsed time. No discussion of whether 40 sessions over 1 year produces the same outcome as 40 sessions over 6 months (twice weekly) or 2 years (biweekly). The article does not theorise whether elapsed time itself is part of the healing process.

### Therapy vs. life
Not addressed. All improvement is attributed to therapy. The Consumer Reports finding ("the longer they stayed, the more they improved") could also reflect the passage of time, natural recovery, life circumstances, or the non-specific effects of having a regular supportive relationship.

### Access and context
Directly addressed. The central argument is that managed care and insurance systems impose arbitrary session limits that contradict research evidence. The article frames this as a systemic problem: therapists are pressured to produce results in 6-8 sessions when the evidence shows meaningful change takes 21-40+ sessions. The "evidence-based therapy myth" is presented as an ideology serving cost containment rather than patient welfare. However, the article does not address the countervailing access argument (Paris 2020): that unlimited treatment for some means no treatment for others when resources are finite.

### Definition of endpoint
Mixed. Lambert uses "clinically significant change" on the OQ-45 (a standardised symptom measure). The Consumer Reports study uses self-reported improvement (subjective). Westen's therapists reported on "successful" treatment (undefined). The Ryan case study implies a structural endpoint (trust, authentic engagement, processing of traumatic material) rather than a symptomatic one. The article implicitly argues that symptom-based endpoints are inadequate — "clients come to therapy primarily for symptom reduction" is presented as a "basic flaw."

### Dropout data
No explicit dropout data. The relapse statistics (40% of improvers relapse; 7 in 10 do not improve or relapse) are presented as evidence of treatment failure rather than as dropout data per se. No information on who leaves therapy before completion or why.
