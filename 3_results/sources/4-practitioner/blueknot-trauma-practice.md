## Summary

The Blue Knot Foundation (an Australian practitioner organisation focused on complex trauma) provides clinical guidance for working with complex trauma clients. The guide endorses a three-phase treatment model (stabilisation, processing, consolidation) supported by 84% of expert clinicians surveyed. The most striking feature is the explicit acknowledgment of treatment duration: "treatment may last for decades, whether provided continuously or episodically." This is paired with a minimum dose: treatment "rarely can be meaningful if completed in less than 10-20 sessions." The guide also provides important contraindications: standard PTSD exposure-based treatments should be avoided with complex trauma clients, and both bottom-up (somatic/body-based) and top-down (cognitive/insight) approaches should be employed.

## Key claims
- "Treatment may last for decades, whether provided continuously or episodically" — the most expansive duration claim in the practitioner layer.
- Treatment "rarely can be meaningful if completed in less than 10-20 sessions" — a minimum dose threshold.
- Three phases endorsed by 84% of expert clinicians: (1) Stabilisation, (2) Processing, (3) Consolidation.
- "These phases are NOT linear — safety must be re-established repeatedly" — explicit acknowledgment of recursive nonlinearity.
- Some individuals benefit from therapy "on and off over their lifetime" — episodic engagement as a valid treatment model.
- Avoid standard PTSD exposure-based treatments with complex trauma clients — these approaches may retraumatise rather than heal.
- Employ both bottom-up (somatic, body-based) and top-down (cognitive, insight-based) approaches.

## Duration data
- **Minimum dose:** 10-20 sessions. Below this threshold, treatment is "rarely meaningful."
- **Maximum duration:** "Decades, whether provided continuously or episodically." No upper bound specified.
- **Lifetime engagement:** Some individuals benefit from therapy "on and off over their lifetime" — this is the most expansive duration claim in the entire review.
- **84% expert consensus** on the three-phase model. The figure comes from an unspecified survey of expert clinicians.

## Trajectory description

Three-phase model with explicit recursiveness:
1. **Stabilisation** — Establishing safety, emotional regulation, grounding. Must be re-established repeatedly as deeper material emerges or life crises occur.
2. **Processing** — Working through traumatic material. Requires adequate stabilisation as a foundation.
3. **Consolidation** — Integration, meaning-making, rebuilding life.

Key trajectory features:
- Phases are NOT linear. The patient cycles between phases as needed. Processing may destabilise, requiring return to stabilisation. Consolidation may surface new material requiring processing.
- Safety must be "re-established repeatedly" — not established once and then maintained.
- The trajectory may span decades, with periods of active engagement and periods of pause.
- Episodic engagement (therapy "on and off over their lifetime") is normalised as a valid treatment pattern, not as failure.

## Relevance to thesis

Highly relevant. This source provides three critical contributions: (1) the widest duration range in the practitioner layer ("decades"), which may even exceed the academic literature's estimates; (2) the normalisation of episodic treatment (therapy on and off over a lifetime), which directly addresses the "continuous or interrupted?" secondary question; (3) the explicit contraindication of standard PTSD treatments for complex trauma, which supports the thesis argument that this population requires different treatment, not just longer treatment. The 84% expert consensus on the phase model confirms that the Herman (1992) framework is the dominant clinical paradigm. The 10-20 session minimum provides a useful lower bound: anything shorter is not treatment, it is assessment.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Directly addressed. "Whether provided continuously or episodically" explicitly normalises interrupted treatment. Some clients benefit from therapy "on and off over their lifetime." This is the strongest statement in the review that episodic engagement is valid and expected — not a sign of treatment failure.

### One modality or many?
Implicitly multi-modal. The recommendation to employ both "bottom-up and top-down approaches" implies that no single modality is sufficient. The contraindication of standard PTSD exposure treatments suggests that modality selection must be adapted to complex trauma specifically.

### One therapist or several?
Not addressed. The episodic treatment model ("on and off over their lifetime") raises the question of whether the same therapist is maintained across episodes, but this is not discussed.

### Session frequency?
No data.

### Phase-based or organic?
Explicitly phase-based with 84% expert consensus. Three phases (stabilisation, processing, consolidation) with explicit recursiveness. The emphasis on nonlinearity and repeated return to safety makes this a recursive spiral rather than a linear progression.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Partially addressed. The recommendation for both bottom-up and top-down approaches implies integration of somatic/body-based work alongside cognitive/insight-based work. Whether these are delivered within a single therapeutic framework or as separate adjuncts is not specified.

## Quality notes

This is a practitioner guide from a reputable specialist organisation (Blue Knot Foundation is Australia's primary complex trauma support and education organisation). It is not a peer-reviewed paper but represents a synthesis of clinical expertise, endorsed by the organisation and referencing the ISTSS Expert Consensus Guidelines. The 84% expert consensus figure is cited without detailed methodology. The "decades" claim is striking but should be understood as describing the outer boundary of what some clients need, not as the median or expected treatment duration. The contraindication of exposure-based treatments is consistent with the ISTSS guidelines (Cloitre et al., 2011) and with the arguments in Steele (2005) and Van der Hart (2006). Should be weighted as authoritative practitioner guidance that aligns with and extends the academic consensus.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Partially addressed through the episodic model. By normalising therapy "on and off over their lifetime," the guide implicitly acknowledges that not all clients maintain continuous engagement. However, it does not discuss clients who leave treatment entirely, or outcomes for those who disengage before reaching the processing or consolidation phases.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Partially distinguished. The "decades, whether provided continuously or episodically" claim explicitly separates elapsed time from dose: a decade of episodic therapy involves far fewer sessions than a decade of continuous therapy. The minimum dose (10-20 sessions) is about session count, but the "decades" claim is about calendar time. This is one of the few sources that explicitly acknowledges that elapsed life-time — not just session count — is part of the healing process.

### Therapy vs. life
Implicitly addressed through the episodic model. If clients benefit from therapy "on and off over their lifetime," the periods between therapy episodes are presumed to involve living, processing, and integrating — life itself is part of the healing arc. The recursive phase model (returning to stabilisation after life crises) also acknowledges the interaction between therapy and life events.

### Access and context
Implicitly addressed. If treatment "may last for decades," the access implications are staggering — who can access decades of therapy? The guide does not address this directly, but its Australian context (where mental health services are partially publicly funded) may assume a different access landscape than the US or UK.

### Definition of endpoint
Structural. The third phase (consolidation) implies integration and meaning-making as endpoints, not symptom reduction. The episodic model (therapy on and off over a lifetime) implies that there may not be a single endpoint — healing is an ongoing process with no definitive completion.
