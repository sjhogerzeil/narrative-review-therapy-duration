## Summary

Janina Fisher, a clinical psychologist who trained under both Judith Herman and Bessel van der Kolk, describes her career trajectory from 1989 to the present and her development of TIST (Trauma-Informed Stabilisation Treatment). The interview covers the conceptualisation of complex PTSD/DESNOS as distinct from simple PTSD, the structural dissociation model as a framework for understanding fragmentation in chronically traumatised individuals, and Fisher's integration of multiple therapeutic modalities (Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, IFS, clinical hypnosis, CBT) into a stabilisation-first approach. Fisher argues against narrative-focused or abreactive approaches to complex trauma, emphasising that traumatic activation shuts down the prefrontal cortex and that treatment must work with the body and with parts rather than with the story. She explicitly avoids EMDR with highly functioning but internally destabilised complex trauma clients due to overwhelm risk. BPD is reframed as a trauma presentation driven by structural dissociation rather than a personality defect.

## Key claims
- Complex PTSD (DESNOS) is fundamentally different from simple PTSD: it encompasses mood disorders, personality disorder symptoms, and "disorders of meaning" including negative cognitive distortions and self-blame.
- The structural dissociation model explains how children in traumatic environments split into a "keeping on" part (left-brain, daily functioning) and trauma-reactive parts (right-brain survival responses: fight, flight, fear, submission).
- TIST integrates concepts from IFS, Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, clinical hypnosis, and CBT — not as a sequence of methods but as a coherent framework for developing a different relationship with symptoms.
- Traumatic activation shuts down the prefrontal cortex — treatment must help clients identify triggers through "suddenness, intensity, and duration" rather than revisiting trauma narratives.
- Fisher explicitly avoids EMDR with highly functioning complex trauma clients because of overwhelm risk — "emotions, images, and even cognitions originate somatically — the body is where it all starts."
- BPD diagnosis is iatrogenically harmful: labelling trauma survivors as "manipulative" and "attention-seeking" increases suicidality.
- Clients seek help to feel better, not to receive a particular methodology — the clinician should integrate body-based approaches across all interventions.
- "I never focus on the events" — Fisher's approach centres on the living legacy (symptoms, parts, somatic activation) rather than the trauma narrative.

## Trajectory description

Fisher does not describe an explicit treatment trajectory or timeline. However, the implicit trajectory can be reconstructed from her clinical philosophy:

1. **Psychoeducation and normalisation** — The client learns to understand symptoms as survival adaptations, not pathology. The structural dissociation model is taught as a framework. This de-shames and creates cognitive scaffolding.
2. **Stabilisation through somatic and parts awareness** — The client learns to identify triggers (through suddenness, intensity, duration), regulate the nervous system, and develop a mindful observer relationship with parts rather than being hijacked by them.
3. **Integration (implied, not detailed)** — The endpoint is a changed relationship with the self, where fragmented parts are understood as adaptive rather than pathological, and the prefrontal cortex can remain online during activation.

This is a stabilisation-dominated model. Processing of trauma memories is de-emphasised or removed entirely ("I never focus on the events"). The trajectory is therefore one of gradual capacity-building rather than cathartic breakthrough — inherently long-term.

## Relevance to thesis

Highly relevant. Fisher represents the clinical position that complex trauma cannot be treated quickly because the injury is structural (fragmentation, dissociation, identity distortion) rather than event-specific. Her refusal of EMDR for this population — despite it being the gold-standard rapid processing tool — is a powerful statement that brief protocols are contraindicated when the injury is developmental. The TIST framework, which integrates four modalities, implicitly argues that no single modality is sufficient and that the multi-modal integration itself takes time. Fisher's work bridges the academic literature (Herman, Van der Kolk, Van der Hart) and clinical practice, confirming that the consensus on extended treatment is not merely theoretical but shapes how experienced clinicians actually work. The emphasis on stabilisation-first directly supports the thesis that healing takes years: if you cannot even begin processing until the client is stabilised, and stabilisation itself is a long process, the total treatment arc extends accordingly.

## Secondary questions

### One modality or many?
Explicitly multi-modal. TIST integrates Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, IFS, clinical hypnosis, and CBT into a single framework. Fisher's clinical philosophy is that no single modality suffices for complex PTSD. She explicitly avoids EMDR (a single-modality rapid-processing tool) with this population. The integration is not sequential (first one, then another) but concurrent — different modalities are woven into each session as needed.
