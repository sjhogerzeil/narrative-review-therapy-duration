## Summary

Lauren Auer, LCPC, introduces the neologism "traumalescence" to describe a specific phase of trauma recovery that she argues is under-recognised: the transition period when a person begins to experience the world from a new lens and shift their identity away from trauma-organised survival. She draws an explicit analogy to adolescence — both involve identity confusion, neural reorganisation, an uncomfortable in-between state, and the tension between old and new ways of being. The article's most valuable contribution is an explicit developmental timeline: 3-6 months for emotional awareness, 6-18 months for neural pathway strengthening, 18 months-3 years for integration of new patterns. Auer reframes apparent regression during this period as progress: "This isn't regression. This is progress."

## Key claims
- "Traumalescence" = "The transition period of the trauma healing process when a person starts to experience the world from a new lens and shift their identity."
- The process is analogous to adolescence: identity confusion, neural reorganisation, uncomfortable in-between states, emergence of a new self.
- Apparent regression during recovery is actually progress — old patterns destabilise before new ones consolidate.
- "This isn't regression. This is progress."
- "Like adolescence, this phase is about becoming."
- "I've been trying to earn the right to exist my whole life" — client example illustrating identity-level work.
- The timeline is developmental, not protocol-driven: neural reorganisation takes the time it takes.

## Duration data
- **Months 3-6:** Emotional awareness increases. The person begins to notice their trauma responses as trauma responses (rather than as "just who I am"). This parallels the recognition phase described in mia-injured-not-broken.
- **Months 6-18:** Neural pathways strengthen. New patterns of response begin to consolidate, but old patterns co-exist — creating the destabilisation and apparent regression that Auer reframes as progress.
- **Months 18-36 (1.5-3 years):** Integration of new patterns. The new identity becomes the default rather than an effortful alternative.
- **Total implied trajectory:** 3 months to 3 years from the onset of the "traumalescence" transition. Note that this timeline describes the transition phase itself — it does not include the stabilisation work that precedes it or the ongoing integration that may follow.

## Trajectory description

Three-phase developmental trajectory explicitly mapped onto a timeline:

1. **Emotional awareness phase (3-6 months):** The person begins to see their survival patterns as patterns rather than as reality. Emotional range expands. This is disorienting — "like adolescence," the old self is being outgrown before the new self has arrived.

2. **Neural strengthening phase (6-18 months):** New neural pathways begin to consolidate. Old and new patterns coexist, creating instability, apparent regression, and confusion. The person may feel worse during this phase because they are now aware of their trauma responses but not yet able to consistently override them. "This isn't regression. This is progress."

3. **Integration phase (18 months-3 years):** New patterns become the default. Identity reorganisation completes. The person operates from a post-trauma identity without sustained effortful self-monitoring.

The trajectory is explicitly nonlinear — movement between phases is expected, and apparent regression is reframed as a sign of deeper processing. The adolescence analogy is structural: just as adolescence involves a period of destabilisation between childhood and adulthood, traumalescence involves a period of destabilisation between trauma-organised and post-trauma identity.

## Relevance to thesis

Highly relevant. This is one of the few sources that provides an explicit developmental timeline for the recovery process: 3 months to 3 years for the traumalescence transition alone. The adolescence analogy is powerful — it normalises the extended timeline by connecting it to a universally recognised developmental process. The reframing of regression as progress directly addresses the clinical challenge of maintaining therapeutic engagement during periods of apparent deterioration. The timeline aligns with other sources: the 6-month awareness onset corresponds to Lambert's 50% change point (Shedler 2020); the 18-month-3-year integration corresponds to the phase models in Herman (1992) and Steele (2005). The neologism itself ("traumalescence") is a contribution — it gives a name to a phenomenon that multiple sources describe but none have previously labelled.

## Quality notes

This is a practitioner blog post (Substack). No empirical data, no sample, no measurements. The three-phase timeline is presented as clinical observation, not as research findings. The neural pathway language ("neural pathways strengthen") is used loosely — it invokes neuroscience without citing specific studies. The adolescence analogy is illustrative and pedagogically effective but should not be taken as a literal scientific comparison. The value of this source is conceptual and clinical: it names a phenomenon (traumalescence), provides a developmental framework for understanding it, and normalises the extended timeline. Should be weighted as informed practitioner observation with a useful conceptual contribution, not as evidence.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Not addressed. The timeline describes clients who are actively in recovery — it does not account for those who drop out during the destabilising phases (6-18 months) or who never enter the traumalescence transition.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Explicitly distinguished — and the distinction favours elapsed time. The timeline is presented in calendar months, not sessions. The neural and developmental framing implies that the passage of time (with its attendant neural reorganisation) is the active ingredient, not the accumulation of session hours. This is consistent with the Lindfors 2019 convergence finding (calendar time > session count) and with the neuroscience layer's argument (Porges, Schore, Hambrick/Perry) that biological change operates on developmental timelines.

### Therapy vs. life
Partially addressed through the adolescence analogy. Adolescence happens in life, not just in sessions — and the analogy implies that traumalescence similarly unfolds across all domains of living. The integration phase (18-36 months) presumably involves living differently in relationships, work, and daily life, not just processing in sessions. This is one of the sources that most clearly acknowledges healing as a lived process rather than a purely therapeutic one.

### Access and context
Not addressed.

### Definition of endpoint
Structural. The endpoint is identity integration — operating from a post-trauma identity without sustained effortful self-monitoring. This is an identity-level endpoint, not a symptom-reduction endpoint. No operational criteria are given.

### Dropout data
No data. The destabilising middle phase (6-18 months) is acknowledged as a period of apparent regression — this is precisely when dropout risk would be highest, but this is not discussed.
