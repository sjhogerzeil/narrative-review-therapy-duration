## Summary

Katherine Woodward Thomas argues that trauma healing requires two distinct orientations: a backward-looking phase (grief, processing, understanding what happened) and a forward-looking phase (creating a new identity, living into a positive future). The central claim is that many clients and therapists remain stuck in the backward-looking phase — endlessly processing the past — without transitioning to the generative work of future-focused transformation. Until clients recognise that they themselves are now perpetuating old patterns through habitual ways of relating, they cannot "graduate" from the trauma story. This frames healing as having a specific developmental arc with a turning point: from victim of the past to author of the future.

## Key claims
- "While healing requires we look backward, transformation requires we lean in, listen for, and live into a positive, possible future."
- "Until you recognize that you yourself are now the one who is perpetuating the story inside of habitual ways of relating, you will not graduate from that story."
- Healing has two orientations: grief-focused (looking backward) and creation-focused (leaning forward). Both are necessary; neither is sufficient alone.
- The transition from past-focused to future-focused work represents a maturation point in the healing process — not an abandonment of the past but a reorientation toward the future.

## Trajectory description

Two-phase trajectory:
1. **Looking backward** — Grief, processing, understanding the trauma history. Necessary but not sufficient. Clients may remain here indefinitely without therapeutic guidance toward the second phase.
2. **Leaning forward** — Creating a new identity, living into a positive future, recognising and changing habitual relational patterns. This is where "graduation" from the trauma story occurs.

The turning point is recognition of agency: the moment the client sees that they are now perpetuating old patterns, not just suffering from past injuries. This is both a cognitive insight and an identity shift — from "this was done to me" to "I am now doing this to myself and can choose differently."

## Relevance to thesis

Moderately relevant. Woodward Thomas adds a dimension to the trajectory question: healing is not just about processing the past but about constructing a future. This extends the treatment timeline — if adequate treatment must include both past-processing and future-creation, then even clients who have "finished" processing their trauma still have work to do. However, the article is more self-help/personal development than clinical, and does not engage with the clinical literature on treatment duration. It is less directly relevant than other Layer 4 sources but adds a useful conceptual distinction (backward-looking vs. forward-looking work) that connects to post-traumatic growth literature (Joseph 2012, Ferentz).

## Quality notes

This article reads more as self-help/motivational writing than clinical practice literature. No empirical data, no case studies with treatment parameters, no citations of research. Woodward Thomas is known primarily as a relationship coach and author of popular self-help books, not as a clinical researcher. The two-phase model (processing → creation) has face validity and connects to established concepts (post-traumatic growth, meaning-making), but it is presented without clinical specificity. Should be weighted as conceptual framing from the self-help/coaching tradition rather than clinical evidence. Least directly relevant of the 10 Layer 4 sources.
