## Summary

Robert Grossmark, author of *The Unobtrusive Relational Analyst*, presents "companioning" as the central therapeutic stance for work with deeply dissociated patients. The analyst works from an "unobtrusive relational position" — accompanying, being with, and quietly attuning to inchoate (pre-verbal, unformed) psychological states rather than interpreting, directing, or processing in any active sense. The core argument is that some patients need extended periods of being accompanied before any therapeutic processing can occur. This is not a therapeutic technique but a relational posture — and by its nature, it cannot be time-limited. You cannot companion someone on a schedule.

## Key claims
- "Companioning. Accompanying. These are the terms that he draws on to engage us in thinking about clinical work, perhaps with all patients, but certainly with patients often in dire states of dissociation."
- "The analyst works from within an unobtrusive relational position" — not interpreting or directing but being present with pre-verbal, inchoate states.
- Companioning applies "perhaps with all patients, but certainly with patients often in dire states of dissociation" — it is especially necessary for the most severely traumatised/dissociated.
- The unobtrusive stance implies that the therapist must tolerate not-knowing, not-acting, and not-interpreting for extended periods — the pace is set by the patient's capacity to emerge from dissociative states, not by the therapist's framework or the system's timeline.

## Duration data
- No explicit duration data. No session counts, year ranges, or dose-response findings.
- Implicit: companioning is inherently open-ended. The stance of "accompanying" inchoate states cannot be compressed — the therapist must wait for the patient to gradually become present, and this process has no predictable timeline. The emphasis on dissociative states (which are by definition states of absence and non-integration) implies that the early phase of treatment may involve long periods where nothing appears to be happening — yet the relational presence itself is the treatment.

## Trajectory description

No formal trajectory model, but the implicit trajectory is:
1. **Accompaniment** — The therapist is simply present. The patient is in dissociative, inchoate states. No processing occurs in any conventional sense.
2. **Gradual emergence** — Over time, the patient begins to be more present in the room. The relational field becomes more alive. Dissociative barriers gradually soften.
3. **Availability for deeper work** — Only after extended companioning can the patient engage in the more active therapeutic work (interpretation, processing, integration) that other models begin with.

This trajectory implies that companioning IS the first phase of treatment for severely dissociated patients — and it may take months or years before the patient is available for what most models consider "treatment proper."

## Relevance to thesis

Relevant as a theoretical argument for why treatment with dissociated patients cannot be time-limited. Grossmark describes a population (severely dissociated patients) and a therapeutic stance (companioning) that are inherently incompatible with brief therapy. The argument is not "more sessions produce more change" (dose-response) but "the patient's dissociative organisation determines the pace, and the therapist must subordinate their agenda to the patient's readiness." This connects to Steele (2001) on dependency-as-mechanism and to the BCPSG (2010) on implicit relational knowing — change operates at a pre-verbal, procedural level that cannot be accelerated through technique. The population described (dire dissociative states) maps directly onto the thesis target population.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No data explicitly. However, the relational nature of companioning implies continuous engagement — interruptions would disrupt the very relational presence that constitutes the treatment.

### One modality or many?
Single modality — relational psychoanalysis. The companioning stance is specific to a psychoanalytic relational framework and would not easily translate to structured or manualised approaches.

### One therapist or several?
Implicitly one therapist. Companioning depends on a specific relational attunement that develops over time between two particular people. Transferring to a new therapist would reset this process entirely.

### Session frequency?
No data. Psychoanalytic practice typically involves 2-3 sessions per week, but this is not specified.

### Phase-based or organic?
Organic. The process of companioning does not follow predetermined phases — the therapist responds to whatever state the patient brings, without a structured sequence. However, there is an implicit developmental arc from dissociative absence to increasing relational presence.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
No data.

## Quality notes

This is a colloquium description from IARPP (International Association for Relational Psychoanalysis and Psychotherapy), offering 3 continuing education credits. It describes Grossmark's theoretical and clinical framework rather than presenting empirical data. No sample, no outcomes, no measurements. The concept of "companioning" is theoretically rich and clinically resonant but unfalsifiable in its current form — there are no criteria for when companioning has succeeded or failed, and no way to distinguish effective companioning from ineffective waiting. Should be weighted as a compelling clinical concept from within the relational psychoanalytic tradition, not as evidence for treatment duration. Its value is in articulating what therapists DO during the long, apparently static early phases of treatment with dissociated patients.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Not addressed. Companioning assumes a patient who remains in treatment. No discussion of patients who leave during the long phase of apparent non-progress, or how to distinguish "productive companioning" from "patient not benefiting."

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Not distinguished. The concept of companioning implies that elapsed time IS the treatment — mere presence over time, rather than specific interventions delivered in sessions. This is perhaps the most extreme version of the "time itself heals" argument in the review: the therapist's job is to be there while time does its work.

### Therapy vs. life
Not addressed. All change is attributed to the therapeutic relationship. No acknowledgment of life events, other relationships, or non-therapeutic factors.

### Access and context
Not addressed. Relational psychoanalysis at 2-3 sessions per week is among the most expensive and least accessible forms of treatment. The silence on access is notable given that the population described (severely dissociated patients) often has limited financial resources due to the functional impairments caused by their condition.

### Definition of endpoint
Structural but implicit. The endpoint would be the patient's capacity to be present — to emerge from dissociative states and engage relationally. This is not defined operationally. Beyond the initial companioning phase, the broader treatment endpoint (integration, symptom resolution, relational capacity) is not discussed.

### Dropout data
No data. The concept of companioning does not address what happens when patients leave treatment or how the therapist evaluates whether companioning is working.
