## Summary

Saj Razvi, director of education at Innate Path in Denver and clinical researcher in the MAPS MDMA-assisted psychotherapy trials, provides a comprehensive framework for understanding trauma through the autonomic nervous system. He describes three escalating tiers of trauma response: mild stress (anxiety, hypervigilance), high stress/panic (fight-flight), and dissociative shutdown (parasympathetic collapse with endogenous opioid release). His central argument is that dissociation — not anxiety — is the core challenge in complex trauma, and that conventional therapies (including EMDR and talk therapy) fail to reach dissociative states because they rely on conscious self-report and top-down cognitive processing. He then describes how psychedelic medicines (cannabis, ketamine, MDMA) can bypass cognitive defenses to access dissociative material. Razvi shares his own trajectory of healing from profound early childhood neglect, providing specific duration data for each phase: talk therapy (intellectual understanding only, no structural change), 2.5 years of somatic/nervous system work, 1.5-2 years of attachment/self-parenting work, followed by a life-changing MDMA session in 2014 during MAPS training.

## Key claims

- Dissociation, not anxiety, is the central clinical challenge in complex trauma: "I think the ball game is all in the dissociated realms" — Razvi, trauma framework discussion
- The deepest trauma level produces outwardly calm, cognitively functional people who are internally numb, making their condition invisible: "our cognitive capacity is online and actually more unencumbered when people are in this final state of trauma... they can talk fine, they can do math fine, they just can't feel fine, they can't relate to anybody" — Razvi
- Conventional therapy, including EMDR, cannot reach dissociative states: "any kind of therapy that relies completely on self-report is not effective here because the last person to know that they're deeply traumatized is the traumatized person" — Razvi
- Talk therapy provides intellectual understanding but no structural change in dissociative trauma: "[talk therapy] was helpful in terms of helping my mind orient to certain things. I don't think it fundamentally changed anything in terms of the patterns, in terms of the reactivity" — Razvi, personal narrative
- Good somatic therapy can crack dissociative material but takes 6-12 months per core issue — Razvi, comparative discussion
- MDMA can accomplish in a single day what takes 6-12 months of somatic therapy: "something that would have taken six months to a year can be cracked open in something like two hours, or in a day long MDMA session" — Razvi
- Cannabis may be even more effective than MDMA at cracking dissociation — Razvi
- Allan Schore's research shows two pathways to behavioral calm in infants: relational calming (secure attachment -> auto-regulation) vs. dissociative calming (insecure attachment -> numbing); "100% of addicts do not have auto-regulation on board" — Razvi
- Neglect without overt abuse can produce the deepest dissociative trauma because there is no identifiable traumatic event to process — Razvi, personal narrative
- Human relational wounds require human relational healing: "these are human relational wounds that happened... We think human relational wounds requires human relational contact" — Razvi, on psychedelic therapy approach

## Duration data

- **Razvi's personal healing timeline:**
  - Talk therapy (starting age 28): Duration unspecified, characterized as providing understanding only, no structural change
  - Somatic/nervous system work (Trauma Dynamics, Boulder): "a pretty solid two-and-a-half, three years" — one-on-one with a somatic therapist
  - Attachment/self-parenting work (Gestalt, with Don Larson): "another year and a half to two years"
  - First MDMA session: 2014, during MAPS training — described as profoundly transformative, exceeding all prior work in depth
  - Total pre-MDMA conventional therapy: approximately 4-5 years of intensive somatic and attachment work, after initial talk therapy
  - Post-MDMA: Part 2 of podcast (not in this source) covers psilocybin work

- **Clinical observations (Innate Path):**
  - Conventional somatic therapy: 6-12 months to crack a core dissociative issue
  - MDMA session: 8 hours, 6 of which are active processing; can accomplish what takes 6-12 months conventionally
  - Cannabis/ketamine sessions: 2 hours each; can be administered more frequently than MDMA (which requires 3-5 weeks between sessions)
  - Between MDMA sessions: "a lot of therapeutic contact hours... holding people together, supporting people"
  - Current clinic patients: "people that have gone through years of psychotherapy" and are using psychedelic work as "a last line response"

## Trajectory description

Razvi's personal trajectory illustrates a sequential, multi-modality healing path spanning decades. Childhood neglect (0-2 years) from a depressed immigrant mother produced a dissociative attachment pattern that was invisible to him until graduate school (approximately age 28). Key recognition moments: the boundary exercise in training ("why would I ever say yes to anybody?") and the eye contact incident with a girlfriend that triggered pure rage. He began talk therapy, which provided cognitive mapping but no structural change. He then moved to somatic/nervous system work (Trauma Dynamics), which produced embodied shifts: "your entire body would go through a wave. It would go through a process and come to the other side of it." After 2.5 years of this, he recognized that releasing traumatic charge was insufficient without building new relational patterns, so he shifted to attachment/self-parenting work with a Gestalt therapist for 1.5-2 years. After completing this conventional trajectory, he described himself as "functional" and "more stable" but uncertain about relational capacity. The MDMA session in 2014 then broke through to a level that all prior work could not reach: the experience of love itself, described as seeing "the nature of existence is to love." Razvi explicitly frames the prior work as necessary scaffolding, not wasted effort.

## Relevance to thesis

This source supports the thesis from multiple angles. First, Razvi's personal timeline of 4-5+ years of intensive therapy before MDMA directly demonstrates that healing from early developmental trauma takes years. Second, his theoretical framework explains why it takes years: dissociative trauma is the hardest to reach, conventional therapies have limited access to dissociative states, and even the best somatic approaches take 6-12 months per core issue. Third, the source complicates any simplistic time claim by introducing psychedelics as potential accelerators — but even with MDMA, the total healing trajectory still spans years (the prior somatic and attachment work is framed as necessary scaffolding, not replaceable). Fourth, the description of clients arriving at Innate Path after "years of psychotherapy" as a "last line response" confirms that complex dissociative trauma routinely outlasts conventional treatment timelines. The source is particularly valuable for its articulation of why talk therapy alone is insufficient for dissociative presentations and for providing a theoretical rationale (autonomic nervous system tiers) for the multi-year timeline.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?

Both. Razvi's personal trajectory involved sequential phases with different therapists and modalities, implying transitions between providers. The conventional therapy phase appears continuous within each modality (2.5 years somatic, then 1.5-2 years attachment). A significant gap likely existed between the conventional therapy phase and the MDMA session in 2014 ("fast-forward many years... I'd been teaching this work and having a full private practice"). The overall trajectory from first therapy (age 28) to MDMA (2014) spans many years with unclear continuity between phases.

### One modality or many?

Multiple modalities, sequentially. Razvi explicitly describes moving through: (1) talk therapy, (2) somatic/nervous system work (Trauma Dynamics), (3) attachment/self-parenting work (Gestalt), (4) MDMA-assisted psychotherapy. Each modality addressed a different layer. Talk therapy provided cognitive understanding; somatic work released traumatic charge from the nervous system; attachment work built new relational patterns; MDMA accessed depth that none of the prior modalities could reach. This sequential multi-modality path is one of the most clearly articulated in the review.

### One therapist or several?

Multiple therapists. At minimum: (1) talk therapist at college counseling, (2) somatic/Trauma Dynamics therapist in Boulder, (3) Gestalt therapist Don Larson for attachment work, (4) MDMA session conducted as part of MAPS training. Razvi does not discuss whether this was by design or circumstance, but the different modalities required different practitioners.

### Session frequency?

No explicit data on session frequency for Razvi's personal therapy. Innate Path clinical data: MDMA sessions are 8 hours, spaced 3-5 weeks apart. Cannabis/ketamine sessions are 2 hours and can be administered more frequently. Between MDMA sessions, "a lot of therapeutic contact hours" are needed.

### Phase-based or organic?

Both. Razvi's personal trajectory was organic (he moved to a new modality when he recognized the limitations of the current one), but his theoretical framework is implicitly phase-based: (1) nervous system stabilization and traumatic charge release, (2) attachment repair and new relational patterning, (3) psychedelic-facilitated access to dissociative core. He explicitly articulates the transition between phases: "just because I don't have trauma, it doesn't mean that I have this new [capacity]... I still need to pattern what it's like to be raised by a competent parent." The prior work is framed as "scaffolding" for the psychedelic work.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?

Therapy-plus, explicitly. Razvi's trajectory involved conventional therapy (talk, somatic, Gestalt) supplemented by psychedelic medicines (MDMA, and in Part 2, psilocybin). At the clinical level, Innate Path combines psychedelic sessions (cannabis, ketamine, MDMA) with ongoing psychotherapy between sessions. The source argues that psychedelic medicines are necessary adjuncts for dissociative trauma that conventional therapy alone cannot resolve.

## Quality notes

This is an expert clinical interview combined with personal narrative, recorded as a podcast. Razvi brings three lenses: (1) theoretical expertise in trauma neurobiology and the autonomic nervous system, (2) clinical research experience from the MAPS MDMA trials, and (3) his own lived experience of complex developmental trauma and healing. The theoretical framework (three-tier trauma response) is well-articulated and grounded in established neuroscience (Porges, Schore, Levine). The personal narrative is specific and detailed, with concrete duration data. However, the source is limited by several factors: Razvi's claims about cannabis superiority over MDMA for dissociation are based on clinical observation at a single center, not controlled research. The MDMA efficacy claims are supported by the MAPS trials but the specific cannabis claims are not yet evidence-based. The personal narrative is a single case. The therapeutic stance is explicitly advocacy-oriented (Razvi is director of education at a psychedelic therapy organization). These limitations are appropriate for a Layer 4 practitioner source — the value lies in clinical wisdom and experiential detail rather than controlled evidence. The source is particularly strong on explaining mechanisms of treatment resistance.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias

Moderate. Razvi's personal narrative is a success story — he went from profoundly dissociated and relationally impaired to becoming a functional trauma therapist and clinical researcher. The clinical observations from Innate Path describe patients who have sought psychedelic therapy as a "last line response" after failing conventional therapy, introducing selection bias. There is no mention of patients for whom psychedelic therapy also failed, or of clients who dropped out of Razvi's conventional therapy phases.

### Dose vs. elapsed time

This source provides unusually clear data on dose vs. elapsed time. Razvi specifies: 2.5 years of somatic work, 1.5-2 years of attachment work. For MDMA: 8-hour sessions (6 hours active), spaced 3-5 weeks apart. For cannabis/ketamine: 2-hour sessions. The key comparison — "something that would have taken six months to a year can be cracked open in... two hours" — directly addresses dose compression while preserving elapsed time (the prior years of conventional work were necessary scaffolding). The source suggests that both dose and elapsed time matter, but psychedelics may dramatically reduce the dose needed per therapeutic unit.

### Therapy vs. life

Partially addressed. Razvi's recognition of his own trauma came through graduate school training (life-as-catalyst) rather than through therapy itself. His capacity for animal connection is described as a natural relational capacity that existed outside therapy. His career as a trauma therapist is a form of "research is me search" that blurred therapy and life. However, the source primarily attributes structural change to specific therapeutic interventions (somatic work, attachment work, MDMA) rather than to life events, maturation, or non-therapeutic relationships.

### Access and context

Not addressed. Razvi describes accessing "some of the most high-end work in Boulder that I could find," suggesting privileged access. The MDMA session occurred through MAPS training (clinical researcher access, not patient access — MDMA remains Schedule I). Innate Path provides legal cannabis and ketamine therapy, but no mention is made of cost, insurance coverage, or accessibility for lower-income patients. The source does not acknowledge that the multi-modality sequential trajectory it describes is financially prohibitive for most people.

### Definition of endpoint

Implicit and layered. After conventional therapy (4-5 years), Razvi described himself as "functional" and "more stable" but uncertain about relational capacity ("I don't know if I could still have been successful at doing a relationship"). The MDMA session produced the experience of love — "the nature of existence is to love" — which implies an endpoint beyond functional improvement toward a fundamental shift in capacity for connection. No formal outcome measures are referenced. The overall framing suggests healing is a cumulative process with multiple partial endpoints rather than a single recoverable moment.

### Dropout data

No dropout data. No mention of attrition from the MAPS trials (though these are published elsewhere), from Innate Path clinical programs, or from Razvi's own therapy phases. The patients described at Innate Path are characterized by treatment persistence (having gone through "years of psychotherapy" before arriving), implying extreme selection for non-dropouts.
