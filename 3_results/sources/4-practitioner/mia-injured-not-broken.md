## Summary

This practitioner blog (republished on Mad in America) explains why CPTSD is so difficult for people to recognise in themselves. The central argument: when trauma is chronic and developmental, the trauma responses (hypervigilance, emotional dysregulation, relational avoidance) become so familiar that they are experienced as "just who I am" rather than as symptoms of injury. The alarm state becomes the person's baseline. Shame, fear, and distrust are not recognised as trauma responses because they have been present as long as the person can remember. The reframe "injured, not broken" positions CPTSD as treatable injury rather than fixed identity — but acknowledges that untangling trauma from identity is "the complex piece."

## Key claims
- "Because living in a triggered state of alarm is so familiar, many don't realize trauma is the source" — the chronicity of developmental trauma makes it invisible to the person experiencing it.
- "It's hard to untangle one's trauma from one's identity. That's the complex piece" — the central challenge of CPTSD treatment is not symptom reduction but identity differentiation.
- "They are injured, and these injuries are treatable" — reframes CPTSD from character flaw or fixed condition to treatable injury.
- CPTSD "changes the way you view life" and leaves "an imprint on your nervous system" — the injury is simultaneously cognitive (worldview), identity-level, and physiological.

## Duration data
- No explicit duration data. No session counts, year ranges, or dose-response findings.
- Implicit: the fact that trauma is woven into identity ("hard to untangle one's trauma from one's identity") implies that treatment must engage with identity-level restructuring, which is inherently a long-term process. If the patient's alarm state is their baseline, establishing a new baseline requires extended nervous system retraining. The emphasis on nervous system imprint aligns with the neuroscience layer's argument (Porges, Schore) that autonomic reorganisation is slow.

## Trajectory description

No formal trajectory model. The implicit trajectory is:
1. **Recognition** — The person must first recognise that their baseline state IS a trauma response, not "just who they are." This recognition may itself take months or years, as it requires developing enough distance from the survival state to see it as a state rather than as reality.
2. **Untangling** — Differentiating between "who I am" and "what happened to me." The "complex piece" that constitutes the core therapeutic work.
3. **Healing** — Living from an identity that is no longer organised around threat. The injuries are "treatable" — implying that repair is possible, even if the timeline is long.

## Relevance to thesis

Moderately relevant. This source does not provide duration data but adds an important dimension to the "why is it long" argument: CPTSD is difficult to treat partly because the patient cannot see the injury. If recognition itself takes months or years, and the subsequent untangling of trauma from identity takes further time, the total treatment arc is necessarily extended. The "injured, not broken" reframe is therapeutically important (countering internalised hopelessness) and connects to the endpoint question: the goal is not to fix something broken but to heal an injury, which implies recovery is possible but takes time. Less directly relevant to the primary question than sources with duration data but useful for the conceptual framing.

## Quality notes

This is a practitioner blog post republished on Mad in America (a critical psychiatry website). No empirical data, no case studies with treatment parameters, no citations. Its value is in the clinical description of why CPTSD is hard to recognise and treat — the "untangling trauma from identity" observation is clinically resonant and theoretically coherent. Should be weighted as practitioner wisdom — a clinician articulating what they see in their practice — rather than as evidence.
