## Summary

This Mad in America blog post summarises a study (published in the British Journal of Psychiatry, attributed to Krakau et al.) comparing long-term psychoanalytic therapy (PAT) with CBT for individuals with chronic depression and a history of severe childhood trauma. The key finding: PAT (median 242 sessions) significantly outperformed CBT (median 59 sessions) at 5-year follow-up. The study is presented as evidence that for trauma-linked depression, longer and deeper treatment produces superior long-term outcomes — particularly that the PAT advantage emerged at follow-up rather than at treatment completion, consistent with the sleeper effect pattern.

## Key claims
- Long-term psychoanalytic therapy (PAT) outperformed CBT for chronic depression with childhood trauma history at 5-year follow-up.
- PAT involved a median of 242 sessions; CBT involved a median of 59 sessions — a 4:1 dose ratio.
- The superiority of PAT was evident specifically for individuals with severe childhood trauma, suggesting that trauma history moderates the relative effectiveness of different modalities.
- The advantage appeared at follow-up rather than immediately post-treatment — consistent with the sleeper effect pattern observed in multiple other sources.

## Duration data
- **PAT dose:** median 242 sessions. At weekly frequency, this equates to approximately 4.5 years. At 2x/week, approximately 2.3 years.
- **CBT dose:** median 59 sessions. At weekly frequency, approximately 1.1 years.
- **Follow-up period:** 5 years post-treatment.
- **Differential outcome:** Individuals with severe childhood trauma showed significantly greater improvement in the PAT group over the 5-year follow-up.
- **Dose-response implication:** The 4:1 dose ratio (242 vs. 59 sessions) means this study cannot distinguish between "PAT is a better modality for this population" and "more sessions produce more improvement regardless of modality." The modality and dose variables are confounded.

## Trajectory description

The trajectory implied by the 5-year follow-up design: treatment completion is not the endpoint. The PAT advantage emerged during the post-treatment years, suggesting that PAT initiates structural changes that continue to develop after therapy ends — the patient continues to build on what was established during treatment. This is consistent with the "sleeper effect" pattern: the therapeutic work lays down new relational and psychological capacities that take time to manifest fully in the patient's life.

## Relevance to thesis

Directly relevant. This source provides one of the most concrete data points in the practitioner layer: 242 sessions of PAT produced superior outcomes to 59 sessions of CBT for trauma-linked depression at 5-year follow-up. The population (chronic depression with childhood trauma) overlaps substantially with the thesis target population. However, the confounding of modality and dose limits the interpretive strength — the finding could support either "psychoanalytic therapy is better for trauma" or "more therapy is better for trauma." Both interpretations support the thesis. The follow-up design is particularly valuable, connecting to the sleeper effect pattern identified across multiple layers.

## Quality notes

This is a practitioner blog summarising a peer-reviewed study. The primary study (Krakau et al., British Journal of Psychiatry) should carry more weight than this summary. The MIA summary highlights the headline finding without discussing limitations, confounders, or methodological details. The dose confound (4:1 session ratio) is the most significant limitation — the study cannot separate the effect of modality from the effect of dose. The population (chronic depression with trauma) is well-defined but may not fully overlap with complex developmental trauma involving dissociative features. Should be cross-referenced with the primary Krakau 2023 source already in Layer 2 (krakau-2023-melas-protocol).

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Unknown from this summary. The primary study's dropout rates and handling of missing data are not reported. In a study with a 4:1 dose ratio, differential dropout is a significant concern — patients in the PAT group who completed 242 sessions are a highly selected subgroup.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Not distinguished in the summary. Session count (dose) is reported, but calendar duration and session frequency are not specified. The 4:1 dose ratio (242 vs. 59) makes it impossible to determine whether the PAT advantage reflects more sessions, longer calendar duration, a different modality, or all three.

### Therapy vs. life
Not addressed. The 5-year follow-up period includes substantial life experience that could contribute to or interfere with therapeutic gains. The study design attributes follow-up change to the therapy that preceded it, but life events during the 5-year period are not controlled.

### Access and context
Not addressed. A treatment requiring a median of 242 sessions is expensive and inaccessible to most people. The summary does not acknowledge this. The practical question — who can access 242 sessions of psychoanalytic therapy? — is unaddressed.
