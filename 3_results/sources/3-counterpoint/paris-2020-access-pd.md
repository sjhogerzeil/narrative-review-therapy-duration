## Summary

The strongest counterpoint to the long-treatment thesis in this review. Paris argues that long-term treatment for personality disorders has never been shown to be superior to time-limited interventions, that most improvement occurs by 20 weeks with asymptotic flattening, and that the burden of proof lies with those who favor lengthy and costly treatments. He advocates for stepped care (brief treatment first, longer only for non-responders), cites cost and access barriers as ethical concerns, and invokes the McLean longitudinal study to argue that much of BPD recovery is naturalistic. He calls for "less intensive and less costly" treatment models (citing Zanarini and McMain).

## Trajectory description

Paris describes two trajectories. The first is the dose-effect curve (Howard 1986): rapid early improvement that flattens asymptotically by 20 weeks, with diminishing returns thereafter. The second is the McLean longitudinal trajectory: BPD remission rates increasing gradually over 24 years with decreasing relapse frequency. The key move in Paris's argument is attributing the long-term trajectory to natural course (maturation, life experience) rather than to therapy. If most BPD improvement happens naturally over decades, then therapy's role is to provide early stabilization — not to accompany the patient through the full multi-decade recovery arc.

## Relevance to thesis

This is the essential counterpoint source. Paris makes the strongest available case against long-term therapy for personality disorders, and any honest advocacy for long treatment must engage with his arguments. His key challenges for our thesis: (1) the asymptotic flattening argument (more therapy does not produce proportionally more change), (2) the naturalistic recovery argument (BPD improves over time regardless of therapy), (3) the access/ethics argument (long treatment is expensive and keeps others waiting), and (4) the evidence-quality argument (LTPP meta-analyses have weak databases and small effects).

**The key weakness in Paris's argument for our response:** he conflates BPD with our defined population (complex developmental trauma with dissociative features). His evidence is primarily about BPD symptom reduction, not about structural change in dissociative organization. The dose-effect curve (Howard 1986) was derived from predominantly neurotic populations and has questionable applicability to complex trauma. The McLean longitudinal study tracks BPD diagnosis (categorical) and symptoms, not structural dissociation or integrative capacity. The stepped-care model may work for BPD symptom management but may not address the deeper structural dissociation that characterizes our target population. Additionally, his framing of the endpoint as symptom reduction rather than structural personality change means his "diminishing returns" argument applies to a different (narrower) definition of healing than the one our thesis proposes.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Not directly addressed. The stepped-care model implicitly allows for interruption — brief treatment, followed by reassessment, followed by more intensive treatment if needed. This is a planned discontinuity rather than forced interruption.

### One modality or many?
Implicitly advocates stepped care across modalities: GPM or brief therapy first, then DBT, MBT, or TFP for non-responders. This is sequential modality-switching driven by response, not by the patient or the natural course of healing.

### One therapist or several?
Not directly addressed. The stepped-care model implies potential therapist changes when escalating from generalist to specialist treatment.

### Session frequency?
DBT frequency is referenced indirectly through cost ($220/week implies at least weekly sessions plus skills group). No data on other frequencies.

### Phase-based or organic?
The stepped-care model is implicitly phase-based: brief treatment (Phase 1), reassessment (decision point), intensive treatment for non-responders (Phase 2). However, this is a service-delivery model rather than a clinical phase model — the "phases" are administrative (treatment-level decisions) rather than clinical (stages of psychological change).

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Not explicitly discussed. The reference to DBT (which includes individual therapy + skills group) implies a therapy-plus model for the intensive treatment tier. Medication is not discussed.

## Quality notes

This is a polemical review/opinion piece (8 pages, Personality and Mental Health), not a systematic review or original study. Paris is a prominent and influential voice in the BPD field, and his arguments are well-referenced. However, the paper has significant rhetorical weaknesses: it selectively cites evidence supporting brief treatment while dismissing LTPP meta-analyses as having "small samples and small effects" without engaging with their specific findings; it conflates BPD with all personality disorders; it treats the Howard 1986 dose-effect curve as definitive despite its being based on a different population; and it does not engage with the complex PTSD/dissociative disorders literature at all. The paper's greatest strength is its ethical argument about access — this is a genuine and important concern that the long-treatment literature largely ignores. Weight: essential as a counterpoint, but its evidentiary claims should be critically examined rather than taken at face value. Its strongest contribution is the ethical/access argument, not the clinical evidence argument.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Paris indirectly addresses survivorship bias by invoking the McLean longitudinal study, which tracked patients over 24 years regardless of treatment status. This is one of the few sources that captures long-term outcomes independently of treatment completion. However, Paris does not address survivorship bias in the brief treatment literature — studies of brief therapy for BPD also have dropout, and the "rapid responders" who improve in brief treatment may be a self-selected subgroup.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Paris explicitly invokes the dose-effect relationship (Howard 1986): improvement per session diminishes after approximately 20 weeks (roughly 20 sessions at weekly frequency). However, he also cites the McLean study's 24-year trajectory, which is entirely about elapsed time rather than dose. The tension between these two data points is not resolved: if improvement flattens by 20 sessions but continues naturally over 24 years, then elapsed time (life) does the work that therapy does not. Paris does not explicitly frame this as a dose-vs-time distinction, but the logic is present.

### Therapy vs. life
This is Paris's strongest argument. He explicitly argues that much of BPD recovery is naturalistic — the McLean study shows improvement over 24 years that may reflect maturation, life experience, and natural developmental processes rather than therapy effects. This directly challenges the attribution of long-term improvement to long-term therapy. The argument is powerful but incomplete: it does not address whether therapy accelerates natural recovery, prevents deterioration during the recovery period, or enables the life changes that drive naturalistic improvement.

### Access and context
This is the paper's most original and compelling contribution. Paris directly addresses cost ($220/week for DBT, $10,000+/year), socioeconomic barriers ("most PD patients have limited education and low-level jobs"), and the ethical dimension of waiting lists ("clinicians have an ethical responsibility to help as many people as possible"). He cites Zanarini and McMain calling for "less intensive and less costly" treatment models. This is the most thorough engagement with access barriers in the entire review and must be taken seriously regardless of one's position on treatment duration.

### Definition of endpoint
Paris defines the endpoint implicitly as symptom reduction and diagnostic remission. The McLean study data he cites tracks BPD diagnosis (categorical remission) and symptom frequency (dimensional improvement). He does not engage with structural endpoints (personality integration, resolution of dissociative organization) or functional endpoints beyond symptom measures. This narrower endpoint definition is central to his argument: if the goal is symptom reduction, diminishing returns by 20 weeks is plausible; if the goal is structural personality change, the timeline is fundamentally different.

### Dropout data
Not directly reported. The reference to high dropout rates in BPD treatment is implicit (through the access/cost argument) rather than explicit. Paris does not discuss what happens to patients who drop out of brief treatment — whether they recover naturally, seek treatment elsewhere, or deteriorate.
