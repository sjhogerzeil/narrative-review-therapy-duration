## Summary

Tilson, a licensed clinical social worker (LICSW), mounts a multi-front critique of van der Kolk's *The Body Keeps the Score* (BKS), the third installment in a series debunking ableist pseudoscientific pop psychology (after Mate and Levine). She argues that BKS relies on three debunked neuroscientific frameworks — the triune brain model (disproved in the 1970s), polyvagal theory (shown "untenable or highly implausible"), and overinterpreted fMRI scans. She documents van der Kolk's fabrication of cited research (a "50x asthma rate" that does not appear in the cited paper), his promotion of pseudoscientific interventions (Somatic Experiencing, Internal Family Systems) over evidence-based treatments (CBT, Prolonged Exposure), and the harm his framework inflicts on chronically ill people by implying their diseases result from failure to process trauma. The essay also addresses van der Kolk's firing for creating a hostile work environment and his sympathetic portrayal of war criminals.

For this review's research question, Tilson's critique is relevant primarily as a counterpoint to body-based trauma frameworks that imply indefinite somatic processing is necessary. She does not directly address therapy duration, but her argument that "the body keeps the score" is an atmospheric threat rather than a testable hypothesis challenges the foundational logic of approaches that claim trauma is "stored" in the body and must be somatically "released" — approaches that by their nature tend to imply extended or open-ended treatment timelines.

## Key claims

- BKS relies on three debunked neuroscientific frameworks: the triune brain model (disproved in the 1970s), polyvagal theory (shown "untenable or highly implausible" per Grossman), and unreliable fMRI interpretation (a dead salmon showed "brain activity" in fMRI; results are not replicable on a second scan) — §Debunking van der Kolk's Scientific Claims
- Van der Kolk fabricates or mischaracterizes cited research: he claims "traumatized children have fifty times the rate of asthma as their nontraumatized peers," but the cited paper (Noll) does not contain the words "asthma," "breathing," or any related terms — §Debunking
- The phrase "the body keeps the score" is never coherently defined as a testable theory; it functions as atmospheric fear-mongering through "unverifiable statements like 'the memory of trauma is encoded in the viscera'" — §How Is Your Body Keeping the Score?
- BKS promotes pseudoscientific interventions (Somatic Experiencing, Internal Family Systems) over evidence-based treatments (CBT, Prolonged Exposure) — §Emphasis on Personal Responsibility
- The book increases suffering for chronically ill people by implying disease results from failure to process trauma, writing with "disdain about trauma survivors seeking medical care" — §Impacts for People with Chronic Illness
- Women are disproportionately harmed because they represent 4 out of 5 autoimmune disease cases and are already subjected to centuries of medical gaslighting — §Impacts for People with Chronic Illness
- The words "racism," "sexism," and their variations never appear in BKS; the book places all emphasis on individual responsibility — §Emphasis on Personal Responsibility
- McNally warns that the "body keeps the score" reasoning "inspired so-called 'recovered memory therapy,' arguably the most serious catastrophe to strike the mental health field since the lobotomy era" — §How Is Your Body Keeping the Score?
- "People who have traumatic experiences are often not irrevocably damaged by it, but instead use internal and external resources to overcome it" — §Trauma Does Not Guarantee Dysfunction
- Readers' fierce attachment to BKS may mirror a trauma bond: "Van der Kolk has created the fear that your body is storing up your traumatic experiences, and that he is the only one who can keep readers safe from this threat" — Introduction

## Relevance to thesis

This source **challenges** one strand of the "healing takes years" thesis — specifically, the strand built on somatic/body-based frameworks that argue trauma is encoded in the body and must be slowly released. By dismantling the neuroscientific foundations of BKS (triune brain, polyvagal theory, unreliable fMRI), Tilson removes the theoretical scaffolding that would justify long-term body-oriented trauma therapy.

However, this challenge is narrower than it first appears. Tilson does not argue that healing from complex trauma is quick; she argues that a specific *framework* for understanding trauma is pseudoscientific. Her endorsement of evidence-based treatments (CBT, Prolonged Exposure) is not accompanied by duration claims. The critique is more about *mechanism* (the body does not "keep the score" in the way van der Kolk claims) than about *timeline* (healing can happen quickly).

The source is most useful for the review as a steel-man of the counterargument: if the body-based rationale for extended treatment is scientifically unfounded, what remains to justify long treatment? The answer would need to come from other frameworks — relational, developmental, structural — rather than from somatic encoding narratives.

## Quality notes

As a critical essay (not a systematic review or empirical study), this source must be assessed by different criteria than a research paper. Tilson brings clinical experience (LICSW credentials) and builds her case by synthesizing investigative journalism (Nietfeld in *Mother Jones*), scientific debunking literature (McNally, Barglow, Grossman, Martin), and close reading of BKS itself.

**Strengths of the critique:**
- The fabricated-citation argument (50x asthma claim) is verifiable and devastating if accurate — it is a factual claim about what a cited paper does and does not say, checkable by anyone.
- The debunking of triune brain theory and polyvagal theory relies on established scientific literature, not Tilson's opinion alone.
- The fMRI reliability problem (dead salmon study; non-replicable scans) is well-documented in the neuroscience literature.
- The ethical concerns (firing, hostile work environment, war-criminal sympathy) are sourced to Boston Globe reporting.

**Limitations:**
- The trauma-bonding speculation (readers are trauma-bonded to BKS) is provocative but unfalsifiable — acknowledged by Tilson as a "hunch."
- Tilson's endorsement of CBT and Prolonged Exposure as evidence-based alternatives is stated but not argued in detail. These approaches have their own limitations for complex developmental trauma (as opposed to single-incident PTSD), which Tilson does not address.
- The essay conflates van der Kolk's popular book with the entire body-based trauma therapy field. Sensorimotor psychotherapy (Ogden), for example, has a more nuanced theoretical basis than BKS, and Tilson does not distinguish between responsible and irresponsible versions of body-oriented work.
- As Part 3 of a series, some arguments depend on Parts 1 (Mate) and 2 (Levine), which are not included here.

**Weight in synthesis:** Moderate-to-high as a counterpoint source. The critique of the neuroscientific foundations is well-sourced and largely aligns with mainstream scientific consensus. However, the essay overreaches when it implies that body-based approaches are uniformly pseudoscientific, and it does not engage with the question of therapy duration directly. Best used to represent the strongest version of the "BKS is pseudoscience" argument, while noting that this does not automatically undermine all arguments for extended treatment.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Tilson explicitly invokes the opposite of survivorship bias: she argues that the trauma industry focuses disproportionately on those who are damaged, while ignoring that "people who have traumatic experiences are often not irrevocably damaged by it." This is a form of reverse survivorship bias argument — the claim that the literature overrepresents suffering and underrepresents resilience.

No direct data on access barriers. Tilson notes the "expensive training certificate" and "motivational speaking gigs" economy but does not develop the access argument.
