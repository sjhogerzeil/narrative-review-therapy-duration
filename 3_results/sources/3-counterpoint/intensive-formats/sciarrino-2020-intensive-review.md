## Summary

Systematic review following PRISMA guidelines, searching four major databases for studies of empirically supported treatments (ESTs) delivered in intensive format (more than twice weekly) for adults with PTSD. 11 studies met inclusion criteria (from 408 initially identified). Studies used various ESTs: prolonged exposure (PE), cognitive processing therapy (CPT), EMDR, and cognitive therapy (CT), delivered in formats ranging from daily sessions over consecutive days to 2-3 week intensive programs. Some programs combined multiple ESTs with adjunctive interventions (yoga, physical activity, mindfulness). The weighted mean effect size was d=1.57 (95% CI [1.24, 1.91]) -- large. The pooled dropout rate was only 5.51%, dramatically lower than the 18-39% reported in meta-analyses of standard delivery. Key finding for this review: intensive delivery appears to both improve outcomes AND reduce dropout, suggesting that the standard weekly delivery format may itself be a barrier to effective treatment.

## Key claims
- Weighted mean effect size d=1.57, 95% CI [1.24, 1.91] (large) across 11 studies -- Fig. 2, p. 5
- Individual study effect sizes ranged from d=1.15 to d=2.93 -- Fig. 2
- Pooled dropout rate: 5.51% -- Table 3, p. 8
- Dropout ranged from 0% to 13.6% across studies -- Table 3
- Standard EST dropout rates: 18-39% -- p. 2 (citing meta-analyses)
- "Intensive treatment delivery may contribute to both short- and long-term symptom change and reduced treatment dropout" -- discussion
- Studies with long-term follow-up showed maintenance of gains (ds=0.74-2.59) -- p. 8
- Ehlers (2014) RCT: 7-day intensive CT vs standard weekly = comparable outcomes -- Table 2

## Duration data
- Intensive formats: 1-3 weeks of calendar time, multiple sessions per day
- Specific examples:
  - Ehlers (2010, 2014): 18 hours over 5-7 business days
  - Foa (2018): 10 consecutive weekdays (massed PE) vs 8 weeks (spaced PE)
  - Hendriks (2018): 3 daily PE sessions within 1 week, then 4 weekly booster sessions
  - Harvey (2018): daily PE or CPT for 2-week day-treatment program
  - Hurley (2018): daily EMDR x2 over 10-day period (18-20 total sessions)
- Follow-up data available for 6/11 studies (range: 6 weeks to 1 year)

## Trajectory description

The intensive format compresses what would normally be weeks or months of treatment into days or weeks. The trajectory is steep and concentrated: patients engage in multiple hours of trauma-focused work per day, with minimal time between sessions for avoidance to reassert itself. Several studies offered boarding/lodging, removing patients from daily stressors. The finding that this concentrated approach produces larger effect sizes AND lower dropout challenges the assumption that therapeutic change requires extended time between sessions for processing and integration. The trajectory also includes follow-up data showing maintenance of gains in most studies that measured it.

## Relevance to thesis

**Endpoint divergence**: All outcomes are symptom-based (CAPS-5, PCL-5, and other PTSD measures). No structural personality change, attachment security, or identity measures. The large effect sizes (d=1.57) describe symptom reduction, which is the level of change these treatments target.

**Population mismatch**: Mixed PTSD populations. Several studies include childhood trauma survivors: Hendriks (2018) studied adults with interpersonal trauma (sexual abuse, physical abuse); Wagenmans (2018) focused specifically on childhood sexual abuse. However, the PTSD diagnosis required for inclusion does not equate to complex developmental trauma with dissociative features. Patients with severe dissociation, personality disorders, or suicidal behavior were likely excluded from most studies.

**Strongest version of the counterpoint**: Intensive delivery may be a better format for trauma treatment than the standard weekly model. The low dropout rate is particularly important: if 30-40% of patients drop out of standard weekly therapy, the standard format may be systematically failing a large proportion of patients. Intensive delivery reduces avoidance between sessions, maintains therapeutic momentum, and removes practical barriers to attendance. The large effect sizes suggest that the "dose" of therapy matters more than the "elapsed time."

**Where it breaks down for our population**:
1. 81.2% of included studies were uncontrolled -- only 2 RCTs. This severely limits causal inference.
2. PTSD symptom reduction is not the same as structural change in complex developmental trauma.
3. Most studies did not include patients with complex presentations (comorbid PD, severe dissociation, suicidality).
4. Programs that offered boarding may have achieved results partly by removing patients from stressful environments -- a confound.
5. The intensive format requires patients to take time off work/life, access a specialized program, and potentially travel -- significant practical barriers.
6. The very low dropout rate in intensive programs may partly reflect self-selection: patients willing to commit to an intensive program may be more motivated than those in standard care.

**What the long-duration position can learn**: The dropout finding is genuinely important. If standard weekly therapy loses 30-40% of trauma patients, the effective dose for those who leave is zero. Intensive formats may ensure that patients actually receive a therapeutic dose. The long-duration position should consider whether some patients who "need years" actually need fewer, more concentrated sessions -- and whether the years partly reflect inefficiency in standard delivery, not the inherent complexity of the problem.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Intensive by definition: concentrated delivery over days or weeks. Some programs had booster sessions after the intensive phase.

### One modality or many?
Mixed: 5 studies used standalone ESTs, 6 used combination treatments. EMDR, PE, CPT, CT all represented.

### One therapist or several?
Not consistently reported. Some combination programs likely used multiple providers.

### Session frequency?
By definition more than twice weekly. Typically daily or multiple times daily.

### Phase-based or organic?
EST protocols are structured and manualized. Not phase-based in the Herman/Cloitre sense, but have clear protocol phases.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Several programs included adjunctive interventions: yoga, mindfulness, physical activity, sports. Some offered boarding/lodging.

## Quality notes
Well-conducted systematic review following PRISMA guidelines with high inter-rater agreement (97-100%). The key limitation is that 81.2% of included studies were uncontrolled, limiting causal inference. The weighted mean effect size calculation is appropriate (random-effects model), but heterogeneity across study designs, interventions, and populations is substantial. Quality assessment showed a mean score of 26.4/32 (range 22-32). The pooled dropout calculation is straightforward and arguably the most reliable finding. For this review, the main limitation is the symptom-focused outcome measurement and the PTSD (rather than complex trauma) population.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Dropout rate of 5.51% means survivorship bias is minimal WITHIN intensive treatment. However, the patients who enter intensive programs are self-selected -- they agreed to participate in an intensive format, which already selects for motivation, resources, and capacity.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
This review directly addresses the dose-vs-time question. Intensive delivery preserves the dose (same number of total therapy hours) while compressing elapsed time. The finding that outcomes are comparable or better with intensive delivery suggests that elapsed time between sessions is not necessary for, and may even hinder, therapeutic change in PTSD treatment. However, this applies to symptom-focused treatment -- whether the same applies to structural personality change is unknown.

### Therapy vs. life
Programs offering boarding explicitly separate treatment from daily life. This is both a strength (controls for life events) and a limitation (results may not generalize to outpatient settings where patients remain in stressful environments).

### Access and context
Several programs offered boarding/lodging, which addresses geographic access but creates cost and time barriers. "Regular attendance at therapy appointments can be challenging, particularly for individuals who live in rural areas or cities in which access to providers with training in trauma-focused interventions and PTSD treatment is not readily available" (p. 10). Intensive programs may actually IMPROVE access for some patients by reducing total number of trips to treatment.

### Definition of endpoint
PTSD symptom severity on validated measures (CAPS-5, PCL-5, IES, CAPS). All symptom-based. Loss of PTSD diagnosis reported for some studies (range: 25-84.1% at post-treatment). No structural or relational endpoints.

### Dropout data
Central finding: pooled dropout rate 5.51% (range 0-13.6%). This is substantially lower than standard delivery rates of 18-39%. The 13.6% outlier (Foa 2018 massed PE) included patients who never initiated treatment. Among those who started, dropout was 3.6%. This is a genuinely important finding for the field.
