## Summary

Secondary analysis of pooled data from three Phase 2 MDMA-assisted psychotherapy trials measuring posttraumatic growth (PTG) using the Posttraumatic Growth Inventory (PTGI). 60 participants with treatment-resistant PTSD (CAPS-IV >50, inadequate response to prior treatment) were aggregated into active MDMA (75-125mg, n=45) vs placebo/low-dose control (0-40mg, n=15). At primary endpoint, the MDMA group showed large PTG effects (Hedges' g=1.14, 95% CI [0.49, 1.78]) and large PTSD symptom reduction (g=0.88, 95% CI [-0.28, 1.50]). At 12-month follow-up, PTG was significantly higher than baseline and 67.2% no longer met PTSD criteria. The finding that PTG occurred alongside symptom reduction -- positive changes in self-perception, interpersonal relationships, and philosophy of life -- suggests MDMA facilitates change beyond mere symptom reduction. However, a 2024 correction (DOI: 10.1002/jts.23097) removed data from 4 participants treated by a therapist pair involved in an ethical violation. The corrected analysis "does not compromise the conclusions" according to the authors, but the ethical context is relevant.

## Trajectory description

The trajectory described goes beyond symptom reduction to positive psychological change. PTG reflects a qualitative shift in how participants relate to themselves, others, and life after trauma processing. The MDMA-enhanced therapy appears to facilitate not just the removal of symptoms but the development of new capacities -- greater self-understanding, deeper relationships, altered life priorities. This suggests that some forms of accelerated treatment may produce the kind of structural change that the long-duration thesis associates with years of therapy.

## Relevance to thesis

**Endpoint divergence**: This study is unusual among counterpoint sources because it measures something BEYOND symptom reduction. The PTGI captures positive changes in self-perception, relating to others, appreciation of life, new possibilities, and spiritual change. These domains overlap significantly with what the long-duration position would call "structural change." If MDMA-assisted therapy produces genuine PTG -- not just symptom relief but positive growth -- then the endpoint divergence argument is weakened.

**Population match**: Treatment-resistant PTSD (prior treatment failure) with CAPS-IV >50. The specific trauma types are not detailed in the pooled analysis. This is a severe, treatment-resistant population, but the degree of developmental trauma, dissociation, and personality structure disruption is not characterized.

**Strongest version of the counterpoint**: MDMA-assisted therapy may facilitate genuine structural growth -- not just symptom removal but positive transformation in self-perception and relationships -- in a matter of weeks. If PTG is a valid measure of the kind of change that long-term therapy aims to produce, this would fundamentally challenge the duration thesis.

**Where it breaks down**:
1. PTG as measured by the PTGI is self-reported and may reflect changed self-narrative rather than actual structural change. The PTGI has been criticized for capturing "perceived growth" that may not correspond to observable behavioral change.
2. The ethical violation in one of the included trials and the subsequent data correction raise questions about the integrity of the research program.
3. Small sample (n=60, pooled from 3 trials) with unequal group sizes (45 vs 15).
4. The 12-month follow-up is encouraging but still relatively short for assessing lasting structural change.
5. FDA rejection of MDMA in 2024 casts doubt on the entire research program.
6. Correlation between PTG and PTSD reduction was not significant -- they may be independent dimensions, meaning PTG is not necessarily a result of effective trauma processing.

**What the long-duration position can learn**: The concept of posttraumatic growth -- positive change emerging from trauma processing -- is relevant regardless of treatment modality or duration. Long-term therapy may also produce PTG, but it is rarely measured. Adding PTG measures to long-term therapy outcome research would help bridge the endpoint divergence between brief and long-term treatment research.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Phase 2 crossover design: participants could cross over to the other condition after primary endpoint. Complex design makes continuity assessment difficult.

### One modality or many?
MDMA-assisted psychotherapy -- combined pharmacological and psychotherapeutic.

### One therapist or several?
Two-therapist teams.

### Session frequency?
MDMA sessions spaced weeks apart. Total therapy sessions not specified.

### Phase-based or organic?
Structured protocol (preparation, experimental, integration).

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Therapy-plus: pharmacological enhancement is central.

## Quality notes
Secondary analysis of pooled Phase 2 data. Crossover design complicates interpretation. Small, unequal groups. The 2024 ethical violation correction is a concern. PTGI is a validated measure but captures self-reported perception of growth, not objectively measured change. Published by MAPS-affiliated researchers -- potential bias. The finding that PTG and PTSD reduction were not significantly correlated at primary endpoint is important: it suggests these may be partially independent outcomes.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Pooled from Phase 2 trials with strict inclusion/exclusion criteria. The most complex cases were excluded by design.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
2-3 MDMA sessions over several weeks, with 12-month follow-up. Very compressed active treatment phase followed by extended observation.

### Therapy vs. life
The 12-month follow-up cannot distinguish between treatment-induced growth and natural post-treatment growth facilitated by symptom reduction. If PTSD symptoms decrease, this alone may facilitate positive life changes that register as PTG.

### Access and context
Research setting only. MDMA not available outside clinical trials.

### Definition of endpoint
PTGI (posttraumatic growth) and CAPS-IV (PTSD symptoms). The PTGI is the key measure -- it captures self-perceived positive change in 5 domains: relating to others, new possibilities, personal strength, spiritual change, appreciation of life.

### Dropout data
Not reported in the pooled analysis.
