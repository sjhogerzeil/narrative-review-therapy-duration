## Summary

Open-label clinical trial at Mount Sinai combining 6 intravenous ketamine infusions (0.5mg/kg, 3x/week for 2 weeks) with 5 sessions of written exposure therapy (WET) for chronic PTSD. Rationale: ketamine enhances neuroplasticity, potentially creating a window for more effective exposure-based psychotherapy. WET is a brief, scalable exposure therapy (5 sessions, no between-session assignments). 14 patients began treatment, 13 completed (1 withdrawn for behavioral reasons). All completers were female (mean age 38.3, mean PTSD duration 18.1 years). 62% presented with childhood sexual assault/molestation as primary trauma; high comorbidity (77% MDD, 77% GAD, 38% social anxiety). Results showed large CAPS-5 reductions (d=1.9, CAPS-5 from 41.6 to 20.8). At Week 12: 69% treatment responders, 54% lost PTSD diagnosis. Response was rapid -- 77% were responders at Week 3 (1 week post-treatment). At 6-month follow-up: 61.5% showed durable response. No significant psychotic or manic symptoms during infusions; dissociative symptoms were transient.

- 69% treatment responders (≥30% CAPS-5 improvement) at Week 12 -- p. 3
- 54% no longer met PTSD diagnosis at Week 12 -- p. 3

## Duration data
- 6 ketamine infusions over 2 weeks (3x/week)
- 5 WET sessions over ~2 weeks (beginning after 4th infusion)
- Combined treatment duration: approximately 3 weeks
- Primary outcome at Week 12 (3 months post-baseline)
- Follow-up to Week 24 (6 months) for responders

## Trajectory description

The trajectory is rapid and pharmacologically facilitated. Ketamine infusions begin first, with the rationale of "priming the brain" for enhanced neuroplasticity before psychotherapy starts. WET sessions are interleaved with the final ketamine infusions and continue the following week. The most striking finding is the speed of response: 77% were already responders at Week 3, just 1 week after completing the combined treatment. This suggests that the ketamine-enhanced neuroplasticity window allows rapid reprocessing that might take much longer in standard therapy. The 6-month durability data suggest that the combined approach may help maintain gains that ketamine alone typically loses within weeks.

## Relevance to thesis

**Endpoint divergence**: CAPS-5 change and loss of PTSD diagnosis. Also measured depressive symptoms (MADRS), functional impairment (SDS), and clinical global impression (CGI). All symptom-based. The large avoidance reduction (d=2.1) is interesting -- avoidance is often the most treatment-resistant PTSD cluster and a key barrier to deeper processing.

**Population match**: Partial. 62% childhood sexual assault, mean PTSD duration 18.1 years, high comorbidity. This is a chronic, complex population. However, n=13 (all female), no BPD diagnoses reported, dissociative features not systematically assessed. The exclusion of bipolar, psychotic, and active substance use disorders limits generalizability to the most complex cases.

**Strongest version of the counterpoint**: Pharmacological enhancement of neuroplasticity may create a time-limited window in which exposure therapy is dramatically more effective. If ketamine can "unlock" the brain's capacity for change that is normally rate-limited, the constraint is neurobiological, not relational -- and can potentially be addressed in weeks rather than years. The 18-year mean PTSD duration suggests these were chronic cases that had not responded to conventional treatment.

**Where it breaks down**:
1. Open-label, no control group -- cannot rule out placebo effects, expectation effects, or regression to the mean.
2. n=13 -- too small for reliable conclusions.
3. 53.8% were in concomitant psychotherapy -- the ketamine+WET may have been additive to ongoing treatment.
4. All female -- no gender diversity.
5. Ketamine is not FDA-approved for PTSD (esketamine is approved only for depression).
6. No measures of structural personality change, attachment, or identity.
7. 3 patients who responded at Week 12 showed depressive symptom return by Week 24 -- durability is inconsistent.

**What the long-duration position can learn**: The neuroplasticity hypothesis is important: if chronic PTSD involves "synaptic disconnection" that ketamine can reverse, this suggests a biological substrate for treatment resistance that is not primarily relational. Long-term therapy may be necessary in part because the brain's capacity for change is limited without pharmacological enhancement. This reframes the duration question from "how long does the relationship need to be?" to "how can we enhance the brain's capacity for change?"

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Continuous: ~3 weeks of combined treatment. Follow-up monthly for responders.

### One modality or many?
Combined: ketamine (pharmacological) + WET (psychotherapeutic). Many patients also in concomitant psychotherapy (53.8%).

### One therapist or several?
WET delivered by licensed therapists trained by co-developer; ketamine administered by study psychiatrist. Multiple providers involved.

### Session frequency?
Very intensive: 3 ketamine infusions/week for 2 weeks + 5 WET sessions in ~2 weeks. Total 11 clinical contacts in 3 weeks.

### Phase-based or organic?
Sequential protocol: ketamine infusions first (to "prime" neuroplasticity), then WET sessions begin (during final infusions and continuing after).

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Paradigmatic therapy-plus: pharmacological + psychotherapeutic. 46% also on psychotropic medications, 53.8% in concomitant psychotherapy.

## Quality notes
Open-label trial with no control group and n=13 -- pilot-level evidence. The effect size (d=1.9) is very large but must be interpreted cautiously given the absence of blinding and control. The study design is logical (test feasibility before investing in an RCT), and the 6-month follow-up is a strength. The financial disclosures are extensive -- Feder and Charney hold patents related to ketamine for PTSD, and ISMMS has licensing agreements with ketamine manufacturers. This does not invalidate the findings but represents a significant conflict of interest. The small, all-female sample limits generalizability.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Minimal within the trial (13/14 completed), but the sample is tiny and self-selected (recruited through advertising and clinician referrals). Extensive exclusion criteria removed more complex cases.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Extremely compressed: ~3 weeks of active treatment for chronic PTSD averaging 18.1 years duration. The pharmacological component (ketamine) is designed to compress what the brain would normally take much longer to achieve.

### Therapy vs. life
53.8% were in concomitant psychotherapy -- impossible to isolate the effect of ketamine+WET from ongoing therapeutic work. This is a significant confound.

### Access and context
Ketamine infusions require medical supervision, IV access, and monitoring. WET is deliberately brief and scalable. The combined protocol is resource-intensive. Not covered by insurance for PTSD. Mount Sinai research setting -- not generalizable to community practice.

### Definition of endpoint
CAPS-5 change and loss of PTSD diagnosis. MADRS for depression. CGI for global impression. SDS for functional impairment. All symptom and function measures. No structural personality, attachment, or identity measures.

### Dropout data
1/14 withdrawn by study team (behavioral reasons, not related to treatment). 0/13 completers dropped out. Extremely low dropout, though n is very small.
