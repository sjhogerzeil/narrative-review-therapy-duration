## Summary

Case study describing EMDR treatment of "Sabine," a 52-year-old woman who participated in a multicenter RCT investigating EMDR for personality disorders (Hofman et al. 2022). She presented with CPTSD (ICD-11, assessed via ITQ), BPD (DSM-5, assessed via SCID-5-P), recurring suicidal thoughts, flashbacks from physical and sexual violence, binge eating, emotion dysregulation, fear of abandonment, social isolation, and very low self-esteem. Treatment consisted of 10 biweekly EMDR sessions over 5 weeks, without a stabilisation phase. The therapist constructed a list of target memories and used cognitive interweave techniques to overcome "looping" and avoidance. After 10 sessions, Sabine no longer met criteria for CPTSD, no longer suffered intrusive memories, no longer met BPD criteria, showed improved emotion regulation, and engaged in new relationships and activities.

## Key claims
- 10 EMDR sessions over 5 weeks resolved CPTSD and BPD -- results section
- "Case conceptualization and treatment planning for people with CPTSD do not need to differ much from the procedure used to treat 'normal' (severe) PTSD" -- cited in Farina et al. 2025 commentary
- Stabilisation phase is unnecessary; direct trauma-focused treatment is safe for CPTSD -- treatment approach
- Specific EMDR strategies for CPTSD complications: cognitive interweave for "looping," instructions to keep memory in mind without verbalizing (for shame), addressing dissociation as "not dangerous" -- treatment section
- Patient no longer met CPTSD or BPD criteria after treatment -- outcome

## Trajectory description

Rapid, targeted memory reprocessing. The therapist created a list of potentially relevant traumatic memories and worked through them systematically with EMDR, using specific techniques to manage avoidance, shame, dissociation, and processing blocks. The trajectory is linear and goal-directed: identify memories, process them, verify resolution. No stabilisation phase, no extended relationship-building period.

## Relevance to thesis

**Endpoint divergence**: Outcome measured by loss of CPTSD diagnosis (ITQ) and BPD diagnosis (SCID-5-P). This is a categorical, symptom-based endpoint. The claim that BPD was "resolved" in 10 sessions is remarkable and raises the question of what was actually measured. BPD is a pervasive pattern of relating, not just a set of symptoms -- can 10 sessions of memory reprocessing fundamentally change relational patterns, or does it reduce symptom intensity below diagnostic threshold while leaving underlying patterns intact?

**Population match**: This is the closest single-case match to the review's population. A 52-year-old woman with childhood trauma history, CPTSD, BPD, suicidal ideation, dissociative features, and relational difficulties. However, single cases cannot establish generalizability.

**Strongest version of the counterpoint**: If a patient this complex -- CPTSD, BPD, suicidal, dissociative -- can lose both diagnoses in 10 sessions, then the long-duration thesis is wrong in at least some cases. The case demonstrates that direct trauma processing is feasible and effective even in presentations that many clinicians would consider requiring years of stabilisation work.

**Where it breaks down**:
1. N=1 -- no generalizability.
2. No follow-up data reported. Did the gains persist?
3. Farina et al. (2025) respond to this case: "we, as clinicians used to deal with this combination of severe forms of PTSD and personality disorders, [find] that such quick good outcomes cases are rare, and usually therapies are complex, difficult and time-consuming."
4. Farina et al. also raise the possibility that "we are describing similar presentations with a different underlying structure, with more capacities for adaptive information processing and less avoidance" -- i.e., this patient may have had more internal resources than typical CPTSD+BPD patients.
5. The patient participated in an RCT -- she was screened, consented, assessed. Self-selected, motivated participants in research settings differ from typical clinical populations.

**What the long-duration position can learn**: The specific EMDR techniques described for managing CPTSD complications (cognitive interweave for looping, non-verbal processing for shame) are clinically valuable regardless of treatment length. The case also raises the important question of whether some patients who receive long-term treatment might have benefited from earlier, more focused trauma processing.

## Secondary questions

Continuous: 10 biweekly sessions over 5 weeks. No breaks.

### One modality or many?
Single modality: EMDR only. No adjuncts reported.

### One therapist or several?
Single therapist.

### Session frequency?
Biweekly (twice per week) -- intensive format.

### Phase-based or organic?
Explicitly anti-phase-based. Direct trauma processing from session 1.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Therapy alone. No mention of medication or other treatments.

## Quality notes
Single case study -- the lowest level of evidence for treatment efficacy. Published as part of a special issue designed to showcase different CPTSD treatment approaches, which creates a selection bias toward successful cases. No follow-up data. The case was part of a larger RCT (Hofman et al. 2022), which lends some methodological context. De Jongh is a prominent EMDR advocate with a clear theoretical position against phase-based treatment. The Farina et al. (2025) commentary provides important clinical context and appropriate skepticism.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Maximum survivorship bias: this is a single successful case selected for publication. We know nothing about cases where this approach failed.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
10 sessions over 5 weeks = very high dose concentration. Approximately 10 hours of therapy in ~35 calendar days.

### Therapy vs. life
Single case -- impossible to disentangle. The short duration limits the role of life events, but the behavioral changes described (new relationships, recreational activities) could reflect post-treatment life changes as much as therapy effects.

### Access and context
No data on access or cost. The patient was in an RCT, so treatment was free.

### Definition of endpoint
Loss of CPTSD diagnosis (ITQ) and BPD diagnosis (SCID-5-P). Categorical. "No longer met criteria" does not mean fully recovered or structurally changed.

### Dropout data
N/A for single case. Patient completed all 10 sessions.
