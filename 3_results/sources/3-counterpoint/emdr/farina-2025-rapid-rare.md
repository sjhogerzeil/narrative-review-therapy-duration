## Summary

Commentary by Farina, Dimaggio, and Mosquera on five case studies of CPTSD treatment published in a special issue of the Journal of Clinical Psychology: In Session. The commentary reviews commonalities and differences across the treatment approaches (including EMDR, psychodynamic, DBT-informed, and schema therapy). The most important contribution for this review is the commentary on de Jongh & Hafkemeijer's (2023) 10-session EMDR case: the authors acknowledge it as "a brilliantly treated case" but state that "such quick good outcomes cases are rare" based on their extensive clinical experience with severe PTSD and personality disorders. They raise the possibility that the patient may have had "a different underlying structure, with more capacities for adaptive information processing and less avoidance" -- suggesting that similar diagnostic presentations may have different depths of structural disruption, which would explain different treatment trajectories.

## Trajectory description

The commentary implicitly describes two different trajectories for CPTSD: a rapid trajectory (exemplified by de Jongh's case) possible for patients with sufficient "adaptive information" and internal resources, and a longer, more complex trajectory typical for patients with deeper structural disruption. The commentary suggests that the difference is not in diagnostic category but in underlying structure -- a crucial distinction for this review.

## Relevance to thesis

**Endpoint divergence**: The commentary implicitly distinguishes between symptom-level change (which can be rapid) and structural change in the personality organization of CPTSD patients (which is "complex, difficult and time-consuming"). This aligns directly with the endpoint divergence construct identified in this review.

**Population mismatch**: N/A -- this is a commentary, not an empirical study. But the authors' clinical experience is specifically with "severe forms of PTSD and personality disorders" -- directly our population.

**Strongest version of the counterpoint**: The Farina et al. commentary actually SUPPORTS the long-duration thesis from within the counterpoint layer. It represents experienced clinicians acknowledging that rapid outcomes can occur but insisting they are the exception, not the rule. The contribution to the counterpoint is the suggestion that diagnostic categories mask important structural differences -- some CPTSD patients may have more intact internal resources and respond rapidly, while others have deeper disruption requiring longer treatment.

**What the long-duration position can learn**: The concept of "adaptive information" from EMDR theory is relevant: patients who have some intact positive relational experiences to draw on may process trauma more rapidly. The long-duration position should acknowledge that not all CPTSD patients need the same duration -- identifying who can benefit from briefer treatment would be a genuine advance.
