## Summary

State-of-the-science review of EMDR therapy, summarizing 30+ RCTs and clinical evidence. The review covers EMDR's evidence base for standard PTSD and then presents the most directly relevant data for this review: two studies testing EMDR for CPTSD specifically. The Voorendonk et al. (2020, 2023) study offered 8 days of residential intensive treatment (combining exposure, EMDR, psychoeducation, physical activity) to 308 PTSD patients, 66% of whom met ICD-11 CPTSD criteria -- and 88% of those with CPTSD no longer met diagnostic criteria after 8 days. The van Vliet et al. (2021) RCT compared 16 sessions of standalone EMDR vs EMDR preceded by 8 sessions of stabilisation (STAIR) in 121 adults with PTSD from early childhood trauma with CPTSD symptoms. No difference between conditions: stabilisation added nothing. Nearly 70% lost PTSD diagnosis; only 3% of those initially diagnosed with CPTSD still met criteria. These findings directly challenge the long-duration position for this review's defined population.

## Key claims
- 30+ RCTs support EMDR as first-line treatment for PTSD -- abstract
- 8-day intensive residential treatment: 88% of CPTSD patients no longer met diagnostic criteria (Voorendonk et al. 2020) -- CPTSD section
- 16-session outpatient RCT: ~70% no longer met PTSD criteria; only 3% retained CPTSD diagnosis (van Vliet et al. 2021) -- CPTSD section
- No difference between EMDR alone vs EMDR + STAIR stabilisation -- CPTSD section
- "CPTSD can be effectively treated with EMDR without a pretreatment phase in which patients are taught emotion regulation or other coping skills" -- CPTSD section
- Phase-based treatment model (Cloitre et al. 2012; Herman 1992) is challenged by these data -- CPTSD section

## Duration data
- 8-day intensive residential program (Voorendonk et al. 2020) -- dose: multiple sessions daily over 8 days
- 16 sessions outpatient EMDR (van Vliet et al. 2021) -- dose: 16 sessions, frequency not specified
- 24 sessions (16 EMDR + 8 STAIR) in the stabilisation arm -- no added benefit over 16 EMDR alone
- Standard EMDR protocols: typically 8-12 sessions for single-trauma PTSD

## Trajectory description

The trajectory described is rapid, targeted memory reprocessing. EMDR does not aim to build a long-term therapeutic relationship as the mechanism of change; instead, it uses bilateral stimulation during structured exposure to traumatic memories to facilitate reprocessing. The CPTSD data suggest that even patients with multiple childhood traumas and disturbances in self-organization (the hallmark of CPTSD) can benefit from direct trauma-focused work without a stabilisation phase. The 8-day intensive format shows that high-dose, concentrated treatment can produce diagnostic remission in the vast majority of CPTSD cases.

## Relevance to thesis

**Endpoint divergence**: The outcome is loss of PTSD/CPTSD diagnosis based on symptom criteria (CAPS-5, ITQ). This is a symptom-reduction endpoint. CPTSD includes disturbances in self-organization (affect dysregulation, negative self-concept, relationship difficulties), so losing the CPTSD diagnosis does imply some improvement in these domains -- but it is measured categorically (meets/doesn't meet criteria) rather than dimensionally. The question of whether structural personality change, earned secure attachment, or identity integration occurs is not addressed.

**Population match**: This is the CLOSEST match to this review's defined population of any counterpoint source. The van Vliet et al. RCT specifically studied adults with PTSD from early childhood trauma presenting with CPTSD symptoms. However, the degree of dissociative features, the presence of disorganized attachment, and the depth of personality structure disruption are not reported. "CPTSD" as defined by ICD-11 may capture a range of severity -- from patients who narrowly meet DSO criteria to those with severe structural dissociation of the personality.

**Strongest version of the counterpoint**: If 88% of CPTSD patients no longer meet diagnostic criteria after 8 days of intensive treatment, then the assumption that complex trauma requires years of therapy is empirically wrong -- at least for the endpoint of diagnostic remission. The stabilisation finding is equally important: teaching emotion regulation skills before trauma processing adds nothing to outcomes, directly contradicting the phase-based model that has dominated complex trauma treatment for 30 years.

**Where it breaks down for our population**:
1. The 8-day study lacked a control condition and did not examine long-term effects -- we don't know about relapse.
2. CPTSD as defined by ICD-11 may not capture the full severity of the population in this review. Patients with severe structural dissociation (DID spectrum), organized attachment avoidance that prevents engagement with trauma memories, or extremely limited capacity for affect regulation may not be represented in these samples.
3. The intensive format includes multiple therapeutic components (exposure, EMDR, psychoeducation, physical activity) -- it is not pure EMDR.
4. "No longer meeting diagnostic criteria" is not the same as "healed." Patients may have significant residual difficulties in relationships, identity, and functioning that fall below diagnostic threshold but remain clinically significant.
5. Farina et al. (2025) note that "such quick good outcomes cases are rare" in their clinical experience with severe PTSD and personality disorders.

**What the long-duration position can learn**: The stabilisation debate is critical. If extended stabilisation phases do not improve outcomes, then some portion of long-term therapy may be spent on preparations that don't actually help. The long-duration position should be specific about WHICH components of long treatment are doing the work -- if it is the relationship and not the stabilisation exercises, that changes the argument.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
The intensive format (8 days) is continuous by design. The outpatient format (16 sessions) presumably weekly -- no data on breaks or interruptions.

### One modality or many?
The 8-day intensive combined EMDR + exposure + psychoeducation + physical activity. The RCT tested standalone EMDR vs EMDR + STAIR. This is relevant: the intensive format is multimodal.

### One therapist or several?
Not reported for these specific studies.

### Session frequency?
8-day intensive = multiple sessions daily. Outpatient = presumably weekly (16 sessions, timing not specified).

### Phase-based or organic?
Explicitly argues AGAINST phase-based treatment for CPTSD. Central claim is that stabilisation is unnecessary and direct trauma processing is safe and effective.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
The 8-day intensive includes physical activity and psychoeducation alongside EMDR/exposure. Not pure therapy alone.

## Quality notes
This is a narrative review by leading EMDR researchers (de Jongh is a prominent advocate for EMDR in complex trauma). The key studies cited are strong: Voorendonk et al. (2020) is a large uncontrolled study (n=308) and van Vliet et al. (2021) is a properly randomized trial with follow-up. However, the review is not a systematic review -- study selection reflects the authors' perspective. The lack of long-term follow-up in the intensive study and the categorical (diagnosis lost/retained) rather than dimensional outcome measurement are limitations. De Jongh has a clear theoretical position against phase-based treatment, which should be noted as a potential bias.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Dropout rates are mentioned for the van Vliet RCT (no difference between conditions) but not for the intensive study. In the RCT, 70% lost PTSD diagnosis -- what about the 30% who didn't? Their trajectories are not described.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The 8-day intensive is perhaps the most extreme dose-compression in trauma therapy: all treatment concentrated into 8 days of calendar time. The RCT uses ~16 weeks of calendar time for 16 sessions. Both are dramatically shorter than the multi-year trajectories described in other layers of this review.

### Therapy vs. life
RCT design partially addresses this (both arms received therapy). The intensive format's brevity limits the role of life events during treatment -- but post-treatment change could still reflect life factors.

### Access and context
The intensive program is residential -- requiring patients to travel and be away from daily life for 8 days. This is a significant access barrier. The outpatient RCT is more accessible. Neither study discusses financial barriers.

### Definition of endpoint
Loss of PTSD/CPTSD diagnosis based on CAPS-5 and ITQ. This is a categorical, symptom-based endpoint. Does not capture dimensional change in functioning, relational capacity, or identity.

### Dropout data
Dropout rates comparable between conditions in the van Vliet RCT. Not reported for the Voorendonk intensive study.
