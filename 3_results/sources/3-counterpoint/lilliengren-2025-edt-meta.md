## Summary

Meta-analysis updating Lilliengren et al. (2016) with 57 RCTs (n=4330) of experiential dynamic therapies (EDTs) -- transdiagnostic, affect-focused, short-term psychodynamic models emphasizing in-session emotional processing. Includes ISTDP (Davanloo), AEDP (Fosha), Affect Phobia Therapy (McCullough), Emotional Awareness and Expression Therapy (EAET), and Malan's BDT. Studies conducted 1978-2024 across Western (k=38) and non-Western (k=19) countries. EDTs show large effects vs inactive controls that grow at follow-up, and modest but significant advantages over active treatments at follow-up only. This follow-up advantage is the key finding for the duration debate: EDTs may produce "sleeper effects" that continue developing after treatment ends.

## Key claims
- Large effects vs inactive controls at post-treatment: Hedges' g = -0.96 (k=41) -- p. abstract
- Effects grow at follow-up vs inactive: g = -1.11 (k=20) -- p. abstract
- Small, non-significant effects vs active controls at post-treatment: g = -0.17 (k=27) -- p. abstract
- Significant advantage over active controls at follow-up: g = -0.40 (k=19) -- p. abstract
- Substantial heterogeneity (I2 > 75%) limits confidence -- p. abstract
- Moderator analyses reveal "few significant findings" -- relative consistency across diagnostic groups, treatment formats, and active comparators -- p. abstract
- Non-Western and lower-quality studies reported larger effects vs inactive controls -- p. abstract
- EDT definition: any model directly based on or derived from Malan and/or Davanloo -- p. methods

## Duration data
- Treatment duration varies by model: ISTDP typically 5-40 sessions, AEDP variable, APT ~16 sessions
- No aggregate duration data reported; this is a limitation for this review's purposes
- The follow-up advantage (g=-0.40) suggests change continues after treatment ends

## Trajectory description

The key trajectory finding is the "sleeper effect": EDTs perform comparably to other active treatments at post-treatment but significantly outperform them at follow-up. This suggests that the in-session emotional processing activated by EDT models continues to produce change after formal treatment ends. The trajectory is one of acceleration followed by sustained improvement -- not a ceiling during treatment but continued gains after.

## Relevance to thesis

**Endpoint divergence**: This meta-analysis measures symptom reduction on primary outcome measures. It does not assess structural personality change, relational capacity, or identity integration -- the endpoints that matter most for complex developmental trauma. The large effect sizes vs inactive controls are comparable to other therapies; the modest follow-up advantage is the distinctive claim.

**Population mismatch**: The populations studied are mood disorders, anxiety disorders, personality disorders, and somatic conditions. Complex developmental trauma with dissociative features is NOT a defined population in any of the included RCTs. The PD studies (cluster C predominantly) are the closest match but still differ from the review's target population.

**Strongest version of the counterpoint**: EDT models achieve durable symptom change in relatively few sessions (typically under 40), and the gains continue growing after treatment ends. If these models can produce lasting change in personality disorder populations in short-term formats, perhaps the assumption that complex presentations require years of treatment deserves scrutiny.

**Where it breaks down for our population**: The meta-analysis explicitly covers "common mental health conditions." Complex developmental trauma with dissociative features is not a common condition -- it is characterized by precisely the kinds of defensive structures (dissociation, avoidance, attachment disorganization) that may resist the rapid emotional mobilization EDT models rely on. The ISTDP literature itself acknowledges that patients with "fragile character structure, somatization, and severe depression" need the graded format and "typically require more treatment to achieve symptom remission" (Lilliengren et al. 2017, from the Davanloo reference).

**What the long-duration position can learn**: The follow-up advantage is genuinely important. It suggests that effective emotional processing during treatment can set in motion ongoing change that doesn't require continuous therapeutic contact. This is compatible with, not contradictory to, a long-duration model -- perhaps the long duration includes periods of consolidation where earlier processing continues to unfold.

## Quotes

> "While cautious interpretation is warranted due to unexplained heterogeneity, findings support EDTs as efficacious transdiagnostic interventions for emotional disorders, with sustained benefits over time." -- abstract

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No data. The meta-analysis does not report on treatment continuity, breaks, or resumption patterns.

### One modality or many?
The meta-analysis groups multiple EDT modalities together (ISTDP, AEDP, APT, EAET, BDT) based on shared theoretical roots in Malan/Davanloo. No data on modality-switching within individual treatments.

### One therapist or several?
No data.

### Session frequency?
No data reported at the meta-analytic level. Individual EDT models typically use weekly sessions, though ISTDP can use extended initial sessions (2-3 hours for the trial therapy).

### Phase-based or organic?
EDT models are explicitly NOT phase-based. They aim for direct emotional engagement from the first session, bypassing the stabilization phase that other models recommend for complex presentations.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
No data on adjuncts at the meta-analytic level.

## Quality notes
This is a methodologically sound meta-analysis following PRISMA guidelines, with a comprehensive search and appropriate random-effects modeling. The key quality concern is the substantial heterogeneity (I2 > 75%), which the authors acknowledge limits confidence in the pooled estimates. The finding that non-Western and lower-quality studies report larger effects raises questions about publication bias and methodological rigor in parts of the evidence base. For this review's purposes, the main limitation is that no included RCT specifically targets complex developmental trauma with dissociative features.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Not directly applicable to RCTs, but dropout rates are not reported at the meta-analytic level. This is a significant omission -- if EDT models produce high dropout in complex cases, the effect sizes would reflect only those who tolerated the approach.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The meta-analysis does not distinguish between number of sessions and calendar duration. The follow-up advantage finding is notable because it measures elapsed time after treatment ends, not dose during treatment.

### Therapy vs. life
RCT designs with control conditions partially address this concern. The follow-up advantage specifically suggests that EDT produces change beyond what time alone provides (compared to active controls), though the mechanisms of post-treatment change (therapy-initiated processing vs life events) cannot be disentangled.

### Access and context
No data on access barriers, cost, or socioeconomic context.

### Definition of endpoint
Symptom reduction on primary outcome measures (varies by study). This is the key endpoint divergence issue: the meta-analysis measures symptom reduction, not structural change, relational capacity, or identity integration.

### Dropout data
Not reported at the meta-analytic level. A significant gap.
