## Summary

Comprehensive review arguing that psychodynamic psychotherapy has substantial empirical support, with effect sizes at least as large as those reported for other therapies actively promoted as "evidence-based." Synthesizes multiple meta-analyses and identifies seven distinctive features of psychodynamic technique (focus on affect, exploration of avoidance, identification of recurring themes, discussion of past experience, focus on interpersonal relations, emphasis on the therapy relationship, exploration of fantasy life). The most striking finding for our purposes is not about specific duration, but about trajectory: psychodynamic therapy patients continue to improve after treatment ends, with effect sizes increasing at follow-up — a pattern not observed for other therapies. This "sleeper effect" implies that the therapy installs capacities that keep working after formal treatment stops.

## Key claims
- Psychodynamic therapy effect sizes are comparable to ESTs: Table 1 shows ES 0.69–1.46 across meta-analyses vs CBT 0.58–1.0 — p. 99
- Patients who receive psychodynamic therapy "maintain therapeutic gains and appear to continue to improve after treatment ends" — p. 98
- Effect size at treatment completion (Cochrane review, short-term psychodynamic): 0.97; at follow-up (>9 months): 1.51 — p. 101
- For personality disorders: pre-to-post ES 1.46; at long-term follow-up (mean 23 months post-treatment) ES increased to 1.25 (p<.01) — p. 101
- Nonpsychodynamic therapies may be effective partly because skilled practitioners use techniques central to psychodynamic theory — p. 98
- At 5-year follow-up for BPD: 87% of TAU patients still met BPD criteria vs. 13% of psychodynamic therapy patients — p. 102
- SWAP data (Table 2) show personality changes after psychoanalysis: improvements in inner capacities, not just symptom counts — p. 103
- Process research shows psychodynamic techniques predict outcome even in nominally CBT treatments — p. 104

## Duration data
- Short-term psychodynamic therapy (Cochrane review): ES 0.97 at treatment end, 1.51 at >9-month follow-up — p. 101
- Personality disorders: follow-up assessments averaged 23 months post-treatment, showing continued gains — p. 101
- BPD study: 5-year follow-up showing dramatic group separation — p. 102
- Duration data is implicit rather than explicit: the key claim is about continued improvement over time, not about how many sessions are needed
- The growing effect size at follow-up implies that treatment length alone does not capture the full "dose" — elapsed time after treatment is part of the active ingredient

## Trajectory description

Shedler describes an unusual trajectory pattern specific to psychodynamic therapy: effect sizes are moderate to large at treatment end, then *increase* during follow-up. This contrasts with CBT and other ESTs, where effect sizes typically hold steady or decline after treatment ends. The implication is that psychodynamic therapy sets in motion psychological processes (self-understanding, affect regulation, capacity for self-reflection) that continue to develop autonomously after formal sessions stop. The trajectory is not linear dose-response but rather catalytic: therapy initiates change that unfolds over subsequent months and years. For personality disorders specifically, the BPD data show a very long trajectory — 5 years — during which treated patients progressively diverge from untreated patients.

## Relevance to thesis

Directly relevant as a foundational argument for why therapy for complex conditions requires a long time horizon. The continued-improvement finding reframes the "how long does healing take?" question: the answer is not just "how many sessions" but "how many sessions plus how much time after." For complex developmental trauma, this suggests that treatment duration measured only by session count underestimates the therapeutic process. The BPD 5-year follow-up data provide one of the clearest quantitative answers to our primary question for a personality-disorder population. The review also provides ammunition against the cost-effectiveness objection by showing that the full benefit of psychodynamic therapy is not visible at treatment end.

## Secondary questions

### One modality or many?
The review focuses on psychodynamic therapy specifically but notes that psychodynamic techniques appear to be active ingredients even in nominally non-psychodynamic treatments, blurring the boundary between modalities.

No data.

### Session frequency?
No data. The review does not distinguish between session frequencies.

### Phase-based or organic?
Implicitly organic. The seven features of psychodynamic technique describe an ongoing process rather than sequential phases. The continued-improvement finding suggests change is not phase-bound but cumulative and self-reinforcing.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
No data on adjuncts. The review focuses on psychodynamic therapy as a standalone treatment.

## Quality notes
This is a narrative review, not a systematic review or meta-analysis — Shedler is synthesizing existing meta-analyses rather than conducting one. The review has a clear advocacy stance (defending psychodynamic therapy against the claim that it lacks evidence). This should not be disqualifying — the data cited are real and from reputable sources — but it means the selection of evidence may favor the thesis. Shedler is also the co-developer of SWAP, making the SWAP data (Table 2) partially self-referential. The effect-size comparisons are drawn from different meta-analyses with different inclusion criteria, making direct comparison rough rather than precise. Despite these caveats, the review was published in the APA's flagship journal and has been highly influential. The continued-improvement finding, drawn from multiple independent meta-analyses, is the strongest and least debatable claim.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Not addressed. As a review, Shedler aggregates study-level data and does not discuss dropout or attrition. The effect sizes include only completers or intent-to-treat samples from the underlying studies, but this is not examined. The dramatic BPD 5-year finding (87% vs 13%) does not specify how many patients dropped out of the psychodynamic condition.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
This is perhaps the most important contribution of this source to our review. Shedler does not use these terms explicitly, but the continued-improvement finding makes the distinction vividly: therapeutic dose (sessions) and elapsed time (post-treatment follow-up) are both relevant to outcome. The growing effect sizes after treatment ends suggest that elapsed time is doing independent work — the therapy installs something that continues to develop without further sessions. This challenges simple dose-effect models.

### Therapy vs. life
Not explicitly addressed. The continued improvement after treatment ends is attributed to therapy having set processes in motion, but those processes presumably interact with life experiences during the follow-up period. Shedler does not disentangle therapy effects from life effects, and in fact the argument may be that the therapy's contribution is precisely to enable the patient to benefit from life experiences that were previously inaccessible.

### Access and context
Not addressed. The review is entirely about efficacy and does not discuss who can access psychodynamic therapy, cost, or insurance coverage.

### Definition of endpoint
Mixed. The review uses effect sizes from symptom measures (anxiety, depression scales) alongside personality-level measures (SWAP). The SWAP data explicitly capture "inner capacities" — psychological mindedness, affect tolerance, capacity for intimacy — going beyond symptom reduction. The review implicitly argues that symptom measures alone underestimate psychodynamic therapy's effects because the deepest changes are structural/characterological.

### Dropout data
No data. Not addressed at the review level.
