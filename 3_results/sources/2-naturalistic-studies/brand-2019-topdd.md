## Summary

Prospective naturalistic study of the Treatment of Patients with Dissociative Disorders (TOP DD) Network, following 111 patients with DID or other complex dissociative disorders over 1-2 years. The intervention was a web-based psychoeducational program (videos, written materials, behavioral exercises) designed for both therapists and patients, delivered alongside ongoing individual therapy. Effect sizes ranged from d=0.44 to 0.90 for dissociation, PTSD symptoms, emotion regulation, and adaptive capacities. A particularly striking finding was reduced non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI), especially in the most self-injurious patients. Most notable for our review: patients with HIGHER baseline dissociation showed GREATER and FASTER improvement (d=0.54–1.04 vs d=0.24–0.75 for lower dissociation), contradicting the common assumption that more severe dissociative patients are treatment-resistant. This is the closest source in our review to the defined population of complex developmental trauma with dissociative features.

## Trajectory description

The trajectory described is one of progressive improvement over 1-2 years on multiple symptom domains simultaneously. Unlike many studies that show rapid initial improvement followed by a plateau, the TOP DD data suggest continued improvement between year 1 and year 2 for dissociative symptoms, PTSD symptoms, and emotion regulation. The finding that more severely dissociative patients improve faster and more is particularly noteworthy — it suggests that these patients may be more responsive to treatment than commonly believed, not less. The NSSI reduction in the most self-injurious patients suggests that even dangerous symptoms can shift within a 1-2 year treatment window, though this does not mean treatment is complete. The trajectory is one of ongoing, measurable improvement during treatment that has not yet reached a ceiling.

## Relevance to thesis

Highly relevant on population grounds — this is the closest match to our target population (complex developmental trauma with dissociative features). The DID/dissociative disorder population represents the severe end of the complex trauma spectrum, where treatment duration questions are most acute. The finding that higher-severity patients improve more challenges the pessimistic narrative that these patients are untreatable or require decades of therapy before any change is visible. However, 1-2 years of follow-up likely captures only the early portion of recovery for these patients, and the effect sizes, while meaningful, represent improvement rather than recovery. The therapy-plus design (psychoeducation + individual therapy) is directly relevant to the "therapy alone or therapy-plus?" secondary question.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No data. Patients were presumably in ongoing individual therapy throughout the study period, but continuity patterns are not reported.

### One modality or many?
Explicitly multi-modal: web-based psychoeducation (videos, written materials, behavioral exercises) was delivered alongside ongoing individual therapy. The design tests whether adding structured psychoeducation to ongoing therapy improves outcomes, though without a control group the added value of the psychoeducational component cannot be isolated.

### One therapist or several?
Not directly addressed. Patients were in ongoing individual therapy (presumably with one therapist) while receiving the psychoeducational intervention, which was delivered online.

### Session frequency?
No data on individual therapy session frequency. The web-based intervention was accessed at the participants' own pace.

### Phase-based or organic?
The psychoeducational intervention itself is structured (specific modules, exercises), but no data on whether the individual therapy followed a phase-based model. The dissociative disorder treatment literature generally advocates a phase-based approach (stabilization → trauma processing → integration), but this study does not report on treatment phases.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Explicitly therapy-plus. The study tests the addition of a web-based psychoeducational intervention to ongoing individual therapy. Patients were already in therapy before the study began and continued throughout. This is one of the clearest examples in our source set of adjunctive treatment augmenting individual therapy.

## Quality notes
The study has significant methodological limitations that affect its weight. There is no control group — all improvement could reflect natural recovery, regression to the mean, expectancy effects, or the effects of ongoing individual therapy rather than the psychoeducational intervention. The sample is self-selected (participants and their therapists volunteered for the program) and international (introducing uncontrolled variability in treatment contexts). N=111 is modest. However, this is one of very few prospective outcome studies for dissociative disorders specifically — a population that is massively underrepresented in the psychotherapy outcome literature. The naturalistic design captures real-world treatment conditions, and the 2-year follow-up is longer than most studies in this population. The finding that severity predicts improvement, not resistance, is counternarrative and would be unlikely to emerge from selection bias alone. The study should be weighted as suggestive rather than definitive — important for the population it addresses, limited by design.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Present but unquantified. The study follows patients who were already in ongoing therapy with clinicians who volunteered for the psychoeducational program — this is a double selection (patients who found and stayed with therapists skilled enough to volunteer for a dissociative disorders training program). Patients who dropped out of therapy before the study, who couldn't find a qualified therapist, or who didn't have access to the online program are invisible. Dropout during the study period is not reported.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
Not disentangled. The study measures outcomes at 1 and 2 years but does not report total session counts, therapy duration prior to the study, or the "dose" of the psychoeducational intervention (how many modules completed, how much time spent). Change is measured over elapsed time only.

### Therapy vs. life
Not addressed. Patients were in ongoing therapy and received the psychoeducational intervention, but life events, relationship changes, and other non-therapeutic factors over the 1-2 year period are not tracked. For a population that often experiences ongoing crisis and instability, life events could substantially influence outcomes in either direction.

### Access and context
Not directly addressed, but the web-based delivery of the psychoeducational intervention is itself an access innovation — it allows patients in remote areas or with limited specialist access to receive structured psychoeducation. The international sample also implies diverse access contexts, but these are not analyzed. The study does not discuss the cost of ongoing individual therapy for dissociative disorders, which is typically long-term and expensive.

### Definition of endpoint
Symptom-reduction: dissociation (DES), PTSD symptoms, emotion regulation difficulties, adaptive capacities, and NSSI. These are symptom-level and functional measures rather than structural personality measures. Notably absent: measures of integration (a key treatment goal for DID), relational functioning, or quality of life. The effect sizes represent improvement on continuous measures, not recovery or remission thresholds.

### Dropout data
Not reported. The study does not provide attrition data, reasons for non-completion, or differences between completers and non-completers. This is a significant gap for a population known for treatment disruption and crisis-driven dropout.
