## Summary

The Munich Psychotherapy Study (MPS) is a quasi-experimental comparison of three treatment modalities for major depression: psychoanalytic therapy (PA, M=241 sessions, 2–3x/week, on the couch), psychodynamic therapy (PD, M=85 sessions, 1x/week, sitting), and CBT (M=51 sessions, 1x/week). The study asks a deceptively simple question: are the superior long-term outcomes of psychoanalytic therapy simply due to the higher session count (dose), or does the specific technique contribute independently? The answer is: both, but for different outcomes at different timepoints. Post-treatment differences in interpersonal problems and introject affiliation were mediated by higher session count (dose). But follow-up differences (1–3 years post-treatment) in depressive symptoms were mediated by more pronounced application of psychoanalytic techniques — not by dose. This disentanglement of dose and technique is the study's central contribution.

## Trajectory description

The trajectory differs by modality and outcome type. All three modalities produce improvement during treatment. Psychoanalytic therapy produces additional gains in interpersonal functioning and self-concept (introject affiliation) compared to PD and CBT at post-treatment — and these gains are explained by the higher session count. But something different happens during follow-up: PA patients continue to show improvement (or at least maintain gains) in depressive symptoms during the 1–3 year follow-up period, while PD and CBT patients do not show the same sustained trajectory. This follow-up advantage is NOT explained by dose — it is explained by the intensity of psychoanalytic technique application during treatment. This suggests a "sleeper effect" or "delayed consolidation" mechanism: something about psychoanalytic technique sets in motion changes that continue to unfold after treatment ends, independent of how many sessions were delivered.

## Relevance to thesis

This study is critical for the review because it directly addresses the "is it just more sessions?" question. The finding that dose mediates some outcomes but technique mediates others (particularly long-term symptom outcomes) means that the duration question cannot be reduced to a simple dose-effect calculation. More sessions help — but what happens in those sessions matters for sustained change. This complicates a purely quantitative answer to "how long does healing take?" and introduces a qualitative dimension: not just how many sessions, but what kind of therapeutic work is done in them.

The population (chronic major depression, mean illness duration 5–6 years) is more severe than the Howard et al. (1986) sample and closer to our review's target population, though not explicitly complex developmental trauma. The chronicity (61–73 months of illness) suggests that these are patients for whom short-term treatment had not worked.

The study also provides rare data on the dose-elapsed time distinction: PA had far more sessions (241 vs 85 vs 51) but only modestly longer calendar duration (39 vs 33 vs 29 months), because PA used higher session frequency (2–3x/week vs 1x/week). This allows some disentanglement of sessions from calendar time.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No data (full text reviewed). The study does not examine interruptions, breaks, or treatment discontinuity. All three modalities appear to have been delivered continuously until completion or termination.

### One modality or many?
Directly addressed. The study compares three modalities head-to-head, but each patient received only one modality. The finding that different modalities produce different outcome profiles at different timepoints has implications for sequential modality use: perhaps a patient might benefit from CBT for acute symptom reduction followed by psychoanalytic work for sustained structural change — but the study does not test this directly.

### One therapist or several?
21 experienced therapists delivered treatment (mean 15 years experience, mean age 47). Therapist assignment details are not fully reported. The non-manualized, naturalistic design means that therapist individual style likely contributed to outcome variance, but this is not examined.

### Session frequency?
Directly relevant. PA used 2–3 sessions/week; PD and CBT used 1 session/week. The higher frequency in PA means that the 241-session average was delivered in ~39 months rather than the ~4.5 years it would take at weekly frequency. The dose mediation finding means that session frequency contributes to certain outcomes (interpersonal, self-concept) via total session count. Whether the same number of sessions at lower frequency would produce equivalent results is not tested.

### Phase-based or organic?
Not explicitly phase-based. The non-manualized design means treatment unfolded organically based on clinical judgment. The finding that technique (not just dose) matters for sustained change suggests that what happens in the treatment process — which presumably evolves over phases — is important, but the study does not map phases.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
No data (full text reviewed). Concurrent medication or other treatments are not discussed in the provided data.

## Quality notes

The MPS has important strengths: direct comparison of three modalities with the same diagnosis, experienced (non-trainee) therapists, non-manualized treatment reflecting clinical reality, long follow-up (3 years), and sophisticated mediation analysis disentangling dose from technique. The process measure (PQS) adds objectivity to the technique variable.

Key limitations: the study is quasi-experimental (not randomized), which means that treatment selection may have been influenced by patient or therapist characteristics. With only 77 patients (27/26/24 per group), the study is underpowered for the mediation analyses it attempts — the confidence intervals on mediation effects are likely wide. The non-randomized design means that confounds (e.g., patients with more severe interpersonal difficulties being referred to PA) cannot be ruled out. The population is exclusively major depression; generalizability to complex developmental trauma is uncertain. The German healthcare context (insurance covers long-term psychoanalysis) creates an access situation that does not exist in most other countries.

Despite these limitations, the study's conceptual contribution — demonstrating that dose and technique make independent contributions to different outcomes at different timepoints — is valuable and has strong face validity. It should be weighted for its conceptual insight rather than as definitive causal evidence.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
No data (full text reviewed). The study does not report dropout rates or characteristics of patients who left treatment early. Given the demanding nature of 2–3x/week psychoanalysis over 39 months, dropout is likely to have occurred, and completers may systematically differ from non-completers. This is a notable gap.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
This is one of the study's most valuable features. Because PA uses higher frequency (2–3x/week) while PD and CBT use weekly frequency, the study partially disentangles session count from calendar duration. PA: 241 sessions in 39 months. PD: 85 sessions in 33 months. CBT: 51 sessions in 29 months. The calendar duration is much more similar across conditions (29–39 months) than the session counts (51–241). The mediation analysis shows that session COUNT (not calendar time) mediates post-treatment interpersonal outcomes — suggesting that dose in the session-count sense matters, above and beyond mere elapsed time.

### Therapy vs. life
The study attributes outcomes to therapy (dose and technique), not to life events. The 1–3 year follow-up period, during which PA patients continued to show differential improvement, is a period with no therapy — so either the therapy set something in motion that continued to unfold (the technique-mediation interpretation), or life events during follow-up differentially benefited PA patients (not examined). The study does not control for or measure non-therapeutic change factors during the follow-up period.

### Access and context
Not explicitly discussed, but the German healthcare context is crucial. Germany's statutory health insurance system covers long-term psychoanalytic therapy (including 2–3x/week for years) — a level of coverage essentially unique globally. The MPS results are therefore enabled by an access context that does not exist in most healthcare systems. Extrapolating the PA results to settings without this coverage is problematic.

### Definition of endpoint
Multiple endpoints are used: depressive symptoms (BDI), interpersonal problems (IIP), and introject affiliation (SASB). This mixed endpoint approach is a strength — it shows that different outcomes respond to different treatment components. The key finding is that symptom outcomes (depression) are technique-mediated while interpersonal/self-concept outcomes are dose-mediated. "Improvement" is measured as continuous change scores, not as categorical recovery/non-recovery.

### Dropout data
No data (full text reviewed). Dropout rates, reasons for termination, and completer vs. non-completer analyses are not reported in the available data. This is a significant gap for a study of this duration and intensity.
