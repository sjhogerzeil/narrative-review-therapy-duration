## Summary

Updated meta-analysis of long-term psychodynamic psychotherapy (LTPP) for complex mental disorders, including 10 controlled trials with 971 patients. LTPP is defined as ≥1 year or ≥50 sessions. Compared to less intensive forms of therapy, LTPP showed statistically significant between-group effect sizes ranging from 0.44 to 0.68 across outcome domains. The meta-analysis focuses specifically on "complex mental disorders," defined as chronic mental disorders and personality disorders — conditions for which dose-effect data suggest short-term therapy is insufficient. This is the primary meta-analytic evidence base on which much of the LTPP dose-effect argument rests, and it was updated from the authors' earlier (2008) meta-analysis in JAMA.

## Key claims
- LTPP defined as ≥1 year OR ≥50 sessions — p. 15
- 10 controlled trials included, 971 patients total — p. 16
- Between-group effect sizes favoring LTPP over less intensive therapy: 0.44–0.68 — p. 18
- Complex mental disorders = chronic mental disorders + personality disorders — p. 15
- "Dose-effect relationship data suggest that short-term psychotherapy is insufficient for many patients with chronic distress or personality disorders" — p. 20
- LTPP showed superiority across multiple outcome domains including overall effectiveness, target problems, and general psychiatric symptoms — p. 18
- Update of the 2008 JAMA meta-analysis (Leichsenring & Rabung 2008) with additional trials — p. 15

## Duration data
- LTPP definition: ≥1 year OR ≥50 sessions — this is the definitional threshold, not a finding about optimal duration — p. 15
- The comparison condition is "less intensive" therapy, which includes both shorter-term treatments and treatment-as-usual — specific durations of comparators vary across the 10 trials — p. 16
- Dose-effect data cited suggest short-term therapy insufficient for personality disorders and chronic conditions — p. 20
- No specific session count or year estimate for optimal duration is provided — the claim is directional (more is better than less for complex disorders) rather than quantified

## Trajectory description

The meta-analysis does not describe trajectory directly — it compares endpoints between LTPP and less intensive treatments. However, the implicit trajectory argument is that for complex mental disorders, change continues to accumulate over a longer course of therapy. The fact that LTPP outperforms shorter therapy suggests that the change trajectory for these patients has not plateaued by the time shorter therapy ends. The dose-effect argument implies a gradual, ongoing trajectory of improvement that requires extended time to reach clinically meaningful levels — particularly for personality functioning and chronic symptoms.

## Relevance to thesis

This is the primary meta-analytic pillar supporting the argument that complex disorders require long-term therapy. The effect sizes (0.44–0.68) provide quantified evidence that "longer" outperforms "shorter" for this population. However, the meta-analysis does not answer "how long is enough" — it only shows that ≥50 sessions/1 year outperforms less than that. For our review's primary question, this source establishes the direction (longer is better) but not the destination (how long is sufficient). The definition of LTPP (≥50 sessions or ≥1 year) has become the standard operational definition in the field and anchors the duration discussion across multiple subsequent studies.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No data. The meta-analysis does not address continuity of treatment.

### One modality or many?
The meta-analysis focuses on psychodynamic therapy specifically. Comparison conditions include mixed approaches (shorter psychodynamic, CBT, TAU). The question of whether long-term therapy in general (regardless of modality) is superior to short-term therapy is raised but not directly tested in this meta-analysis.

### One therapist or several?
No data.

### Session frequency?
Not directly compared, but the included studies span a range of frequencies (from 1x/week to psychoanalytic frequency). The LTPP definition (≥50 sessions or ≥1 year) conflates frequency and duration — 50 sessions delivered weekly = 1 year; delivered twice weekly = 6 months. This conflation is a limitation for the frequency question.

### Phase-based or organic?
No data. The meta-analysis does not address treatment phases or structure.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
No data on adjuncts at the meta-analytic level.

## Quality notes
This is a well-conducted meta-analysis published in a high-impact journal (BJP), updating an earlier JAMA publication. Random-effects models are used appropriately. However, the meta-analysis has been the subject of debate (see Smit et al. 2012; Leichsenring et al. 2013 response). Key concerns: (a) the number of included trials is small (10), limiting statistical power and generalizability; (b) the comparison condition is heterogeneous ("less intensive therapy" includes both active short-term treatments and TAU); (c) the LTPP definition conflates frequency and duration; (d) publication bias cannot be fully excluded despite funnel plot analysis. The authors are prominent advocates for psychodynamic therapy, and the meta-analysis has been critiqued for potential selection bias in study inclusion. Despite these concerns, the overall finding — that longer psychodynamic therapy outperforms shorter treatment for complex disorders — is broadly supported by the field and consistent with dose-effect theory.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Not addressed at the meta-analytic level. Individual studies presumably report completers or ITT analyses, but dropout patterns across studies are not synthesized. Given that complex mental disorders are associated with higher dropout rates, the meta-analytic effect sizes likely overestimate the benefit for all patients who begin LTPP.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The LTPP definition (≥50 sessions OR ≥1 year) explicitly acknowledges that both dose and duration matter, but the "OR" construction conflates them. A treatment of 50 sessions in 6 months (twice weekly) counts as LTPP, as does a treatment of 30 sessions over 1.5 years (biweekly). The meta-analysis cannot separate the effects of session count from the effects of elapsed time. This is a structural limitation that the MeLAS protocol (Krakau 2023) is designed to address.

### Therapy vs. life
Not addressed. All change is attributed to therapy. The role of concurrent life events, maturation, or other factors during long-term treatment is not discussed.

### Access and context
Not addressed. The cost implications of recommending ≥50 sessions or ≥1 year of therapy are not discussed, despite their centrality to access and implementation.

### Definition of endpoint
The meta-analysis reports effect sizes for "overall effectiveness," "target problems," and "general psychiatric symptoms" — primarily symptom-level outcomes. Personality functioning is mentioned in the 2013 follow-up paper but is less prominent here. The endpoint definition is thus primarily symptom-reduction, though the "complex mental disorders" framing implies that more than symptom reduction is at stake.

### Dropout data
No data. Dropout is not analyzed at the meta-analytic level. This is a significant gap given the population (complex disorders with high expected dropout rates).
