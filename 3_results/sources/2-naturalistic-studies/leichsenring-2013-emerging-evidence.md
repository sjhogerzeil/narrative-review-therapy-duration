## Summary

Review and reanalysis defending the evidence for long-term psychodynamic psychotherapy (LTPP) against the Smit et al. (2012) challenge. LTPP is defined as ≥50 sessions OR ≥1 year. The authors demonstrate that Smit et al.'s critique actually compared LTPP to other forms of long-term therapy (not to shorter therapy), and that LTPP proved as efficacious as other long-term therapies — which itself supports the case for long-term treatment. The paper includes two new meta-analyses (12 RCTs and 10 RCTs) showing significant effect sizes for LTPP across multiple outcome domains. Most critically for our review, the paper synthesizes dose-effect literature providing specific session counts and time estimates for recovery in personality disorders: 50% recovery by 1.3 years (~92 sessions), 75% by 2.2 years (~216 sessions). It also invokes Howard's three-phase model to explain why later phases of change (characterological, interpersonal) require more sessions than initial symptom improvement.

## Key claims
- LTPP is defined as ≥50 sessions OR ≥1 year of psychodynamic treatment — p. 362
- Smit et al. (2012) actually compared LTPP to other long-term therapies, not to shorter therapy — so showing equivalence supports, rather than undermines, the case for long-term treatment — p. 363
- 12-RCT meta-analysis effect sizes: d=0.40 overall, 0.39 target problems, 0.43 psychiatric symptoms, 0.41 personality functioning, 0.60 social functioning — p. 370
- 10-RCT meta-analysis (stricter criteria): d=0.51 overall, 0.48 target, 0.46 symptoms, 0.57 personality, 0.59 social functioning — all significant except personality (p=0.10) — p. 372
- For chronic distress or personality disorders, about 60% and 40% recover after 25 sessions (Kopta et al. 1994) — p. 379
- 50% of personality disorder patients recover by 1.3 years (~92 sessions); 75% by 2.2 years (~216 sessions) (Perry et al. 1999) — p. 379
- Long-term psychotherapy, regardless of orientation, may be the only sufficiently effective treatment for complex mental disorders — p. 379
- Howard's three-phase model: subjective well-being → symptom reduction → characterological/interpersonal functioning — later phases require more sessions — p. 379

## Trajectory description

The paper draws on Howard's three-phase model to describe healing trajectory: (1) subjective well-being improves first and fastest, (2) symptom reduction follows, (3) characterological and interpersonal/social functioning change last and slowest. This implies a nonlinear, decelerating trajectory where early gains are rapid but later, deeper changes require disproportionately more sessions. For personality disorders specifically, the Perry et al. data paint a concrete picture: roughly half of patients with personality disorders remain symptomatic after a year of treatment. Recovery is not a step function but a gradual probabilistic curve, with the most complex patients requiring the longest treatment. The gap between 25-session outcomes for chronic distress (~60% recovery) vs. personality disorders (~40% recovery) quantifies how diagnostic complexity extends the healing timeline.

## Relevance to thesis

This is one of the most directly relevant sources to the primary research question. The Perry et al. dose-effect data (50% at 1.3 years/92 sessions; 75% at 2.2 years/216 sessions) provide the most concrete numerical answer available in the literature to "how long does healing take?" for personality disorders. Howard's phase model explains *why* it takes so long: the kind of change our review is most interested in (structural, characterological, relational) is the last to emerge and requires the most sessions. The paper also provides the definitional anchor for LTPP (≥50 sessions or ≥1 year) used throughout the dose-effect literature.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
No data. The LTPP definition (≥50 sessions OR ≥1 year) does not specify whether treatment must be continuous.

### One modality or many?
The quote about long-term therapy being effective "irrespective of whether it is rooted in CBT, PDT, or another bona fide treatment approach" implies modality is less important than duration for complex disorders. However, the empirical data reviewed are predominantly psychodynamic.

### One therapist or several?
No data.

### Session frequency?
Table 1 lists varying session frequencies across LTPP RCTs, ranging from 1x/week to 4x/week (psychoanalysis). The dose-effect data from Perry et al. express recovery in both years and sessions, which implicitly acknowledges that frequency modulates the relationship between calendar time and dose. However, no direct comparison of frequency effects is offered.

### Phase-based or organic?
Howard's three-phase model (well-being → symptoms → character) is explicitly phase-based. This provides the theoretical frame for why treatment must be long: the phases that matter most for complex disorders (Phase 3) emerge last. However, the phases are described as sequential/overlapping, not as discrete treatment stages with different techniques.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
No data on adjuncts.

## Quality notes
The paper is both a review and a polemic — written in direct response to Smit et al.'s challenge to the LTPP evidence base. This is transparent and the counterarguments are well-structured. The two new meta-analyses are methodologically sound (random-effects models, sensitivity analyses), though the number of included RCTs is small (10–12). The dose-effect data from Perry et al. and Kopta et al. are secondary citations, not original analyses — they are well-established in the literature but derive from older studies with different diagnostic frameworks. The claim that LTPP is the "only sufficiently effective" treatment for complex disorders is strong and goes somewhat beyond what the data directly demonstrate. The paper should be weighted as a strong secondary synthesis rather than as primary evidence.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Not directly addressed. The meta-analytic effect sizes presumably include only patients who completed assessment, but dropout/attrition is not discussed as a source of bias. The dose-effect data (Perry et al.) describe recovery curves for those who remain in treatment — they do not account for patients who drop out before reaching the required dose.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
This is the source's central contribution. The Perry et al. data express recovery in both session counts and calendar years, making the dose-vs-time distinction explicit. The 50% recovery at 1.3 years/92 sessions implies roughly weekly sessions; the 75% at 2.2 years/216 sessions implies roughly twice-weekly sessions (or that the estimate combines patients at different frequencies). The Howard phase model adds a conceptual layer: different phases respond to different amounts of dose, so the dose-response curve is not uniform across outcome types.

### Therapy vs. life
Not addressed. All change is attributed to therapy. The role of life events, maturation, relationships, or other factors in the recovery process during long-term treatment is not discussed.

### Access and context
Not addressed. The cost and accessibility of 92–216 sessions of therapy is not discussed, despite the obvious implications of the dose-effect data.

### Definition of endpoint
Mixed. Effect sizes are reported for target problems, psychiatric symptoms, personality functioning, and social functioning separately. The Perry et al. recovery data use "recovery" without specifying the threshold, though the original source used reliable change criteria. Howard's phase model explicitly distinguishes between well-being, symptom reduction, and characterological change as different endpoints with different dose requirements.

### Dropout data
No data. Dropout is not discussed despite its obvious relevance to the dose-effect question (patients who need the most sessions are presumably most at risk of dropping out before reaching therapeutic dose).
