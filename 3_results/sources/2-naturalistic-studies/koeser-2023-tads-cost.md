## Summary

Cost-effectiveness analysis of the Tavistock Adult Depression Study (TADS), a pragmatic RCT comparing LTPP + TAU vs TAU alone for treatment-resistant depression (N=129). LTPP consisted of 60 weekly sessions over 18 months. The LTPP intervention cost approximately £5,468 and produced 0.16 additional QALYs, yielding an ICER of ~£33,000/QALY — above the NICE cost-effectiveness threshold of £20,000/QALY. There were no compensating savings elsewhere in the healthcare system. This population was exceptionally severe: mean 25 years of depression, mean 3.7-year current episode, 54% unemployed, 81% early-onset dysthymia, mean 4.3 previous failed treatments. The authors explicitly acknowledge that the study's time horizon was likely too short to capture the full benefits — sustained effects over follow-up suggest that benefits continue to accumulate beyond the measurement window. The paper provides the strongest available data on what long-term therapy for complex depression actually costs, and confronts the uncomfortable reality that effective treatment may not meet standard cost-effectiveness thresholds.

## Trajectory description

The trajectory implied by this cost-effectiveness analysis is one of sustained but slow improvement in a profoundly treatment-resistant population. These patients had been depressed for an average of 25 years, with nearly 5 failed treatments behind them. The 48-session LTPP intervention produced detectable QALY gains, but these were modest (0.16 QALYs) and emerged gradually over the follow-up period rather than appearing immediately. The sustained improvement at follow-up — which the authors note may not have been fully captured by the study's time horizon — echoes the "sleeper effect" pattern seen in Shedler (2010) and Fonagy (2015). For a population with quarter-century depression histories, 18 months of therapy may be a necessary beginning rather than a sufficient treatment.

## Relevance to thesis

Relevant on two fronts. First, the cost data provide a concrete answer to "what does this cost?" — approximately £5,500 for a course of LTPP, or about £33,000 per QALY gained. For the blog's audience, this translates the abstract "long-term therapy" into a tangible financial reality. Second, the population description is exceptionally vivid in conveying what "treatment-resistant" means in practice: 25 years of depression, 4.3 failed treatments, majority unemployed, majority with early-onset dysthymia. This is the population for whom the "how long" question is most pressing and for whom short-term therapy has already demonstrably failed. The paper's own conclusion — that the time horizon was too short — implicitly argues that for this population, the true cost-effectiveness calculation requires a much longer view.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Treatment was continuous — 60 weekly sessions over 18 months with no planned breaks. Actual attendance was 48 sessions, suggesting some sessions were missed but treatment was not formally interrupted.

### One modality or many?
LTPP + TAU vs TAU alone. TAU included antidepressants (70-80% on medication throughout), GP visits, and other routine care. This is explicitly a therapy-plus design: LTPP was added on top of existing treatment, not substituted for it.

### One therapist or several?
Not explicitly addressed. Presumably one LTPP therapist per patient, given the psychoanalytic framework.

### Session frequency?
Weekly sessions (1x/week). The comparison to the Helsinki study (2-3x/week for LPP, 4x/week for PA) highlights that TADS used a much lower frequency. The authors note this means TADS delivered a much smaller dose than Helsinki, which may partly explain the more modest effects.

### Phase-based or organic?
Not discussed. The 60-session LTPP protocol is time-limited rather than open-ended, which represents a constraint on organic development.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Explicitly therapy-plus. 70-80% of patients were on antidepressants throughout the study. TAU included GP care, psychiatric medication management, and other routine services. The cost analysis accounts for all healthcare costs, not just therapy costs.

## Quality notes
Methodologically strong: pragmatic RCT with Bayesian cost-effectiveness analysis, sensitivity analyses, and appropriate handling of missing data. The TADS trial itself (Fonagy 2015) is well-designed. The main limitation is that cost-effectiveness analysis inherently privileges short-term measurable outcomes through the QALY framework, which may systematically undervalue interventions with delayed or structural benefits. The authors acknowledge this directly. The comparison to Helsinki is informative but imprecise — different countries, different healthcare systems, different patient populations. The BDI severity comparison (TADS patients scoring 36 vs 18-32 in other LTPP studies) usefully contextualizes the modest effect sizes: this is a harder-to-treat population than most LTPP studies include.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
Not directly addressed. The analysis appears to be intention-to-treat (N=129 randomized), which mitigates survivorship bias at the study level. However, the population itself represents survivors of multiple treatment failures — they persisted through 4.3 failed treatments to still be seeking help at mean age 44.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The study measures a fixed dose (60 planned sessions, 48 attended) over a fixed period (18 months). The comparison to Helsinki (232 sessions over 3 years, or 646 sessions over 5 years) highlights the dose question: is 48 sessions enough for treatment-resistant depression? The authors implicitly suggest not, given their comment about the time horizon being too short. The sustained follow-up improvement suggests that even this modest dose set processes in motion that continued working.

### Therapy vs. life
The TAU control group received ongoing medical and psychiatric care, so the LTPP effect is specifically the added value of therapy beyond what usual care provides. However, 70-80% medication use in both groups means the therapy effect cannot be separated from medication effects. Life events during the follow-up period are not tracked.

### Access and context
The strongest source on access and cost in this batch. Key access data: 54% of patients were unemployed at baseline; treatment cost £5,468 out of pocket (in an NHS context, publicly funded); the ICER exceeds the NICE threshold for public funding. This creates a paradox for our thesis: the patients who most need long-term therapy are least likely to be able to afford it privately, and the treatment is unlikely to be publicly funded at current cost-effectiveness thresholds. The mean 25 years of depression and 4.3 failed treatments represent enormous sunk healthcare costs that are not credited to the LTPP intervention.

### Definition of endpoint
Functional. The primary outcome is QALYs (quality-adjusted life years), which blend health-related quality of life and duration. Employment is a secondary outcome. The QALY framework captures functional improvement in a standardized way but does not distinguish between symptom reduction, structural personality change, or improved relational capacity. For a population with 25-year depression histories, the QALY measure may be insufficiently sensitive to detect the kinds of changes that matter most.

### Dropout data
Not reported separately from the main TADS trial analysis. The difference between 60 planned and 48 attended sessions (80% attendance) suggests some non-attendance but not formal dropout. Dropout data from the parent trial (Fonagy 2015) would be needed for a complete picture.
