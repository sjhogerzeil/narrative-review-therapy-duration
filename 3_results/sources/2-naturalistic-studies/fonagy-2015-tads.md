## Summary

The Tavistock Adult Depression Study (TADS) is a pragmatic RCT comparing 18 months of weekly long-term psychoanalytic psychotherapy (LTPP) plus treatment as usual (TAU) to TAU alone, in 129 patients with treatment-resistant depression (failed 2+ prior treatments, 2+ year illness history, HDRS ≥ 14). The study's central finding is a striking delayed therapeutic effect: at end of treatment (18 months), the difference between LTPP+TAU and TAU was not statistically significant (32.1% vs 23.9% partial remission, p=0.37). But during the follow-up period, the groups diverged dramatically — at 42 months (2 years post-treatment), 30.0% of LTPP patients vs only 4.4% of TAU patients showed partial remission (p=0.001). This pattern of delayed benefit — where the therapeutic effect becomes visible only after treatment ends — is the study's most important contribution to the duration literature.

## Trajectory description

The trajectory in TADS is remarkable for what it reveals about the temporality of therapeutic change. During the 18 months of active treatment, LTPP patients improved but not significantly more than TAU patients. This is already notable — 18 months of weekly psychotherapy did not produce a statistically detectable advantage over "treatment as usual" at the point when therapy stopped. But then something happened: during the follow-up period (months 18–42), the LTPP group continued to improve while the TAU group deteriorated. By 42 months, the gap was enormous (30.0% vs 4.4%). The trajectory is not the typical dose-effect curve of early rapid gains followed by diminishing returns (Howard et al., 1986). Instead, it shows a slow build during treatment followed by continued consolidation after treatment ends — a "sleeper effect." Simultaneously, the TAU group shows progressive deterioration, suggesting that without substantive intervention, treatment-resistant depression follows a worsening natural course.

## Relevance to thesis

This study is among the most directly relevant to our review for several reasons. First, the population (treatment-resistant depression, 2+ year history, 2+ failed treatments) is closer to our complex developmental trauma population than typical depression trials — these are patients for whom short-term treatment has already failed. Second, the delayed therapeutic effect directly challenges the dominant dose-effect model: if this study had been evaluated only at end of treatment, it would have been declared a null result. The therapeutic benefit only became visible 2 years later. This has profound implications for how we answer "how long does healing take?" — the answer may be "longer than the treatment itself." Third, the TAU deterioration trajectory suggests that for truly resistant conditions, the counterfactual is not stability but progressive decline — making the comparison point for "does therapy help?" different from what is typically assumed. Fourth, the complete remission rates are sobering: even at 42 months, only 14.9% of LTPP patients achieved complete remission. Healing from chronic conditions is partial, slow, and ongoing.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
LTPP was delivered as continuous weekly treatment for 18 months. The study does not examine interruptions within treatment. The follow-up period (months 18–42) is a naturally occurring interruption — treatment stops and change continues. This is arguably the most important finding about continuity in the entire study: the therapeutic process does not stop when sessions stop.

### One modality or many?
The TAU condition included whatever treatment patients and their GPs chose — potentially including medication, counseling, or other interventions. LTPP was delivered as a single modality (psychoanalytic psychotherapy) alongside whatever TAU involved. The study does not detail the TAU content, making it difficult to know whether the comparison group received structured therapy or only medication/GP support.

### One therapist or several?
LTPP was delivered by individual therapists — presumably one therapist per patient for the 18-month course. The study does not examine therapist effects explicitly.

### Session frequency?
Weekly sessions for 18 months. This is notably lower frequency than the psychoanalytic therapy in the Munich study (2–3x/week). At weekly frequency, the total dose is approximately 72 sessions over 18 months — which places it in the range that Howard et al. (1986) would predict 80–85% improvement for average patients, but this was NOT an average population. The delayed effect may partly reflect the lower frequency: at 1x/week, the therapeutic process may need more calendar time to consolidate than at higher frequency.

### Phase-based or organic?
Not explicitly phase-based in the study design. However, the trajectory data implicitly suggests phases: a working phase during treatment (months 0–18) where change is gradual and statistically indistinguishable from natural fluctuation, followed by a consolidation/integration phase after treatment (months 18–42) where the therapeutic work bears fruit. This two-phase pattern (treatment + post-treatment consolidation) is an organic finding, not a designed feature.

### Therapy alone or therapy-plus?
Both groups received TAU (treatment as usual), which likely included antidepressant medication for most patients given the treatment-resistant profile. LTPP was explicitly an adjunctive intervention — added to whatever patients were already receiving. This makes it a "therapy-plus" design, though the study does not analyze the interaction between LTPP and concurrent treatments.

## Quality notes

TADS is a well-designed pragmatic RCT published in World Psychiatry (high-impact journal), with several notable strengths. The pragmatic design reflects clinical reality better than efficacy trials. The extended follow-up (42 months) is rare and captures the delayed effect that shorter studies would miss entirely. The treatment-resistant population is clinically meaningful and addresses a genuine gap — these are patients for whom standard treatments have already failed.

Limitations: the sample size (N=129) is modest, and the study was not powered for the follow-up comparisons that turned out to be its most important finding — the significant follow-up results may be partially a function of TAU deterioration rather than LTPP improvement. The TAU condition is not well-specified, making it unclear what the control group actually received. Assessor blinding at follow-up may have been compromised. Complete remission rates are low in both groups, raising the question of whether "partial remission" is a clinically meaningful endpoint for this population. The study was conducted at the Tavistock Clinic, a center of psychoanalytic expertise, which may limit generalizability to less specialized settings.

Despite these limitations, the delayed effect finding is striking and consistent with the Zimmermann et al. (2014) technique-mediation finding. The study should be weighted as strong evidence for the proposition that some therapeutic changes require time beyond the treatment period itself to manifest.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
The study uses intention-to-treat analysis, which mitigates survivorship bias at the analytic level — all randomized patients are included in the analysis regardless of treatment completion. However, the study does report some dropout: not all LTPP patients completed the full 18 months of treatment. The specific completion rates and dropout characteristics would need to be examined in the full text. The ITT design is a strength compared to per-protocol analyses that would exclude dropouts.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
This study powerfully illustrates the dose-vs-time distinction. The treatment dose was fixed at 18 months of weekly therapy (~72 sessions). But the therapeutic effect emerged during the follow-up period (months 18–42), when no additional sessions were delivered. This means the elapsed time for the effect to manifest (42 months) far exceeded the treatment dose period (18 months). Dose alone cannot explain the outcome — elapsed time, and whatever happens during that time (integration, consolidation, continued psychological development), is essential. This is the strongest evidence in our Layer 2 sources that duration is not reducible to session count.

### Therapy vs. life
The follow-up period (months 18–42) is a period of life without therapy. The continued improvement of LTPP patients during this period could be attributed to (a) delayed therapy effects now manifesting, (b) therapy having equipped patients with capacities that allowed them to benefit more from life experiences, or (c) some combination. The study cannot distinguish these mechanisms. The TAU group's deterioration during the same period suggests that without the therapeutic foundation, life alone does not produce improvement — and may produce worsening — for this treatment-resistant population.

### Access and context
The study was conducted within the UK National Health Service, at the Tavistock Clinic in London. This is a publicly funded setting, removing direct financial barriers for participants. However, the Tavistock is a specialized psychoanalytic institution — the availability of 18 months of weekly psychoanalytic psychotherapy within the NHS is not typical and may not be replicable in routine NHS services. The study does not discuss access barriers, waiting lists, or the broader availability of this treatment.

### Definition of endpoint
Two levels of outcome are defined: partial remission (HDRS < 14, representing clinically significant but incomplete improvement) and complete remission (HDRS ≤ 7, representing full symptom resolution). The study also measures self-reported depression (BDI) and social adjustment. The distinction between partial and complete remission is important: partial remission rates were the headline finding, but complete remission was rare (14.9% LTPP vs 4.4% TAU at 42 months). For our review, this raises the question of what "healing" means in treatment-resistant populations — is partial remission sufficient, or is it a way-station?

### Dropout data
The YAML indicates `has_dropout_data: yes`. The study uses ITT analysis, meaning all 129 randomized patients are included. The specific dropout/completion rates for the LTPP arm are not detailed in the provided data summary, but the ITT design ensures that dropout does not inflate outcome estimates. Dropout rates and reasons would need to be extracted from the full text for a complete picture.
