## Summary

Analysis of treatment non-attendance in the Helsinki Psychotherapy Study (HPS), a randomized trial comparing three psychotherapy modalities for outpatients (N=326) with depressive or anxiety disorders. The study examines predictors of treatment refusal and premature termination across short-term psychodynamic psychotherapy (SPP, 20 sessions), solution-focused therapy (SFT, up to 12 sessions), and long-term psychodynamic psychotherapy (LPP, 2–3x/week for ~3 years). Overall non-attendance was 23% (10.1% refused, 12.9% premature termination). LPP had dramatically higher refusal rates. Poor suitability for psychotherapy (measured by the Suitability for Psychotherapy Scale) predicted non-attendance in LPP, driven by poor flexibility of interaction, poor self-concept, and poor reflective ability. An intriguing inverse pattern emerged in SPP: patients with good suitability were more prone to premature termination.

## Trajectory description

This study does not describe the therapeutic trajectory per se, but describes the trajectory of treatment engagement. The critical finding is that the most intensive/longest treatment (LPP) faces the highest barriers to engagement — both at the point of entry (refusal) and during treatment (premature termination). Patients who are least suitable for psychotherapy (poor reflective ability, poor self-concept, rigid interaction style) are the ones most likely to refuse or drop out of long-term treatment — creating a selection effect where the patients who may need the most intensive treatment are the least likely to receive it. Conversely, the finding that well-suited patients drop out of short-term treatment more often suggests that some patients may recognize when a treatment format is insufficient for their needs.

## Relevance to thesis

Directly relevant as a survivorship bias counterweight. Any study reporting outcomes of long-term psychotherapy must contend with the fact that the people who complete long-term therapy are a self-selected (and therapist-selected) group. This study quantifies the selection: LPP lost nearly a quarter of randomized patients before completion, and the losses were systematically related to psychological characteristics (poor mentalization, rigid interaction) that overlap substantially with the complex developmental trauma population our review focuses on. This means that outcome data from long-term therapy studies may systematically overestimate effectiveness for the most impaired patients — precisely the patients for whom long treatment is most often recommended.

The inverse finding in SPP (good-suitability patients leaving short-term therapy) is also relevant: it suggests that well-suited patients may recognize the insufficiency of brief treatment and seek more, while poorly suited patients may be unable to tolerate the demands of intensive treatment.

## Secondary questions

### Continuous or interrupted?
Implicit data. The study distinguishes between refusal (never starting) and premature termination (starting then stopping), but does not examine patterns of interruption and resumption within treatment. "Life situation" as a reason for premature termination (affecting patients across all modalities) implies that real-world interruptions play a role, but this is not explored.

### One modality or many?
The study compares three distinct modalities (SPP, SFT, LPP) but does not examine whether patients who dropped out of one modality sought another. The randomized design means patients were assigned to a modality they did not choose, which may inflate refusal/dropout rates compared to naturalistic treatment-seeking. The inverse dropout patterns across modalities (poor suitability predicts dropout from LPP; good suitability predicts dropout from SPP) suggest that modality-matching matters.

### One therapist or several?
No data (full text reviewed). Not addressed — the study does not examine therapist effects or therapeutic alliance as predictors of dropout.

### Session frequency?
Directly relevant. The three modalities differ markedly in session frequency: SPP 1x/week, SFT flexible, LPP 2–3x/week. The dramatically higher non-attendance in LPP (the highest-frequency condition) suggests that session frequency is itself a barrier — the demand of attending 2–3 sessions per week for years is substantial and may exceed the capacity of patients with limited psychological flexibility.

## Quality notes

This study has strong methodological features: it draws on a well-known randomized trial (the Helsinki Psychotherapy Study), reports on all randomized patients (not just completers), and examines a neglected but critical question — who drops out and why. The Suitability for Psychotherapy Scale provides a structured, validated measure of the patient characteristics that predict non-attendance.

Limitations for our review: the population (depressive/anxiety disorders, ages 20–46, 76% women, severe personality disorder excluded) is moderately severe but not the complex developmental trauma population we focus on. The exclusion of severe personality disorder is particularly relevant — the patients most analogous to our review population were screened out. The 18.1% with personality disorder who were included likely represent milder presentations. The randomized design, while a strength for internal validity, creates an artificial situation where patients are assigned to treatment formats they did not choose — LPP refusal rates may be inflated by this.

The study should be weighted carefully: its core finding (that patient characteristics systematically predict who can and cannot engage with intensive treatment) is important and likely generalizable, but the specific rates may not transfer to naturalistic settings where patients self-select their treatment format.

## Cross-cutting observations

### Survivorship bias
This study IS about survivorship bias — it is one of the few studies that directly quantifies the selection effects in psychotherapy research. The 23% non-attendance rate means that roughly one in four randomized patients did not receive the treatment they were assigned to. In LPP specifically, the non-attendance was driven by patient characteristics (poor reflective ability, poor self-concept, rigid interaction) that overlap with the complex trauma population. This means that LPP outcome data from this and similar studies reflects a selected sample — the patients who could tolerate and engage with intensive treatment. The study does not report outcomes for non-attenders, so we do not know whether they improved through other means.

### Dose vs. elapsed time
The study distinguishes between session frequency and total duration: SPP is high-frequency-for-short-duration (20 sessions/5–6 months), LPP is high-frequency-for-long-duration (2–3x/week for ~3 years), and SFT is low-dose-flexible-duration (up to 12 sessions/8 months). The differential dropout rates across these conditions suggest that it is the combination of high frequency AND long duration that creates the engagement challenge — not either dimension alone.

### Therapy vs. life
"Life situation" was a reported reason for premature termination across all three modalities (LPP 8, SFT 4, SPP 4), suggesting that non-therapeutic life events actively compete with treatment engagement. The study does not explore this further, but the finding implies that the "dose" a patient receives is not solely determined by the treatment protocol — life intrudes and interrupts.

### Access and context
Not directly addressed, but the study has implicit access implications. The Helsinki Psychotherapy Study was publicly funded, removing the financial barrier. Yet even without financial constraints, LPP had the highest refusal rate — suggesting that access barriers to long-term therapy are not purely financial but include psychological readiness, practical logistics (attending 2–3x/week), and the existential commitment required. The population was Finnish, ages 20–46, which limits generalizability to other healthcare systems.

### Definition of endpoint
The study's primary outcome is non-attendance, not therapeutic improvement. It does not define or measure "healed," "recovered," or "improved." The endpoint is engagement/completion rather than clinical outcome.

### Dropout data
This is the study's primary contribution. Detailed dropout data: 10.1% refused treatment entirely, 12.9% terminated prematurely. LPP refusal was 11.6 times higher than SPP. Reasons for premature termination are reported by modality: disappointment with treatment/therapeutic relationship was the most common reason in LPP (13 patients) and SFT (7 patients), while life situation changes were distributed more evenly. The study does not report outcomes for people who dropped out.
