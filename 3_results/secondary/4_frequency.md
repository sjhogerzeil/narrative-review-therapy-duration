## Key findings

1. **Higher frequency does not clearly shorten calendar duration.** The Helsinki data (Lindfors 2019) is the strongest evidence: PA at 4x/week (646 sessions over 56 months) and LPP at 2-3x/week (232 sessions over 31 months) converged at 10-year follow-up. Tripling the session count did not produce proportionally better long-term outcomes.

2. **Higher frequency may deepen rather than shorten.** Zimmermann 2014 found that post-treatment interpersonal gains were mediated by dose (more sessions = more interpersonal change), but follow-up depression reduction was mediated by psychoanalytic technique, not dose. This suggests frequency delivers different benefits than elapsed time.

3. **Intensive formats compress calendar time but may serve a different function.** Sciarrino 2020 shows intensive formats (multiple daily sessions over 1-3 weeks) produce large effects AND reduce dropout. But these studies use symptom-reduction endpoints; whether intensive formats produce structural change is unknown.

4. **Client experience reveals that frequency is entangled with attachment.** The myptsd-2020-session-frequency thread shows that frequency decisions are shaped by attachment dynamics (reducing frequency triggers attachment anxiety) and access constraints (pro bono = monthly = "pure hell"), not by clinical protocol.

5. **Elapsed time appears to contribute independently of dose.** The sleeper effect (Fonagy 2015, Shedler 2010, Zimmermann 2014, Bateman 2008) shows improvement AFTER treatment ends — suggesting that what happens between and after sessions matters as much as what happens during them.

Note: The silence on frequency across these 11 sources is itself a finding. Most practitioner, memoir, and counterpoint sources treat session frequency as unimportant or assumed (weekly as default), while the most granular frequency data continues to come from naturalistic studies (Layer 2) and Reddit threads (Layer 5).
