# Developmental mechanism convergence (emergent construct)

*Identified during Layer 1 annotation. Five independent traditions describe the same underlying process.*

## The convergence

| Tradition | Author | Mechanism | Unit of change |
|-----------|--------|-----------|---------------|
| Self psychology | Kohut (1984) | Transmuting internalization | Optimal frustration → micro-internalization cycles |
| Object relations | Winnicott (1965) | Regression to dependence → resumed maturation | Holding environment sustained over developmental time |
| Relational psychoanalysis | Bromberg (1998) | Self-state expansion | Capacity to "stand in the spaces" between states |
| Intersubjectivity | Benjamin (1988) | Mutual recognition | Repeated destruction-survival cycles |
| Mentalization | Fonagy et al. (2002) | Marked mirroring → mentalizing capacity | Attachment-based experiential learning |
| Affect regulation neuroscience | Schore (2000, 2021) | Right-brain regulatory structure | Experience-dependent neuroplasticity |
| Polyvagal theory | Porges (2022) | Autonomic reorganization | Repeated co-regulation → neuroception shift |
| Neurosequential model | Perry (2019) | Bottom-up developmental layers | Patterned, repetitive, sequential experience |
| Implicit process | BCPSG (2010) | Implicit relational knowing | "Accumulation of many small shifts" |

## Why this matters for duration

All nine descriptions share three features:
1. **Repeated relational cycles** — change happens through many iterations, not insight
2. **Gradual structural change** — what changes is internal structure (not symptoms)
3. **Inherently temporal** — the process cannot be compressed by technique or frequency

This means duration is not a preference or a resource question — it is a structural requirement of the change process itself. The biology sets the pace.

## Key distinction from dose-effect

Howard's dose-effect model (Layer 2) measures sessions. These developmental mechanisms suggest the relevant "dose" is not sessions but **completed relational cycles** — attunement-disruption-repair sequences that may or may not occur in any given session. This reframes the dose question fundamentally.

## Emerging pattern: the mechanism is layered and each layer has its own timeline

The sources added in this batch reinforce the core convergence but add an important structural observation: the developmental mechanism is not a single process but a stack of layered processes, each with its own timeline and each requiring the previous layer as scaffolding.

**Ogden** articulates this most precisely through the hierarchical processing model: sensorimotor processing (lowest, most "fixed") → emotional processing → cognitive processing. Lower levels are characterised by "greater fixity and concreteness" — "read-only memory" versus cognitive "random-access memory." This explains *why* the developmental mechanism is slow: the injury is encoded at the level most resistant to change, and "emotional responses to very strong stimuli, such as trauma, do not appear to extinguish" (Frijda, 1986, via Ogden). New responses must be built *alongside* old ones rather than replacing them — inherently slower than cognitive restructuring.

**Fisher (workbook)** crystallises the clinical implication as "slower is faster": early abreactive approaches failed because they "exceeded most individuals' capacity to feel." The nervous system must integrate each piece before the next can be introduced — "bite-sizing" is the mechanism's own requirement, not a therapeutic preference. The workbook is positioned as *preparation* for EMDR or Sensorimotor Psychotherapy, meaning the psychoeducation/stabilisation phase (itself months to years) precedes the processing phase entirely.

**Fisher (podcast)** adds the structural dissociation model: children in traumatic environments split into a "keeping on" part and survival-reactive parts. Treatment must work with the body and with parts rather than with the story, because traumatic activation shuts down the prefrontal cortex. This is not event-processing but personality reorganisation — inherently developmental.

**Heacock/Razvi** provides the clearest empirical illustration of the layered timeline. Razvi's personal healing trajectory proceeded through distinct modality-layers: talk therapy (cognitive understanding only, "didn't fundamentally change anything in terms of the patterns"), 2.5 years of somatic/nervous system work (releasing traumatic charge), 1.5–2 years of attachment/self-parenting work (building new relational patterns), then MDMA-assisted psychotherapy (accessing dissociative core). Each layer addressed a different level of the hierarchy; each required the prior layer as "scaffolding." The three-tier autonomic framework (stress → panic → dissociation) maps the layers onto the nervous system: dissociative shutdown is the deepest and most treatment-resistant layer, inaccessible to modalities that rely on self-report. Even with MDMA compression of the final layer, the total pre-psychedelic conventional therapy trajectory was 4–5+ years.

**Spring** provides the lived-experience confirmation. Her developmental-rate metaphor describes survivors rebuilding missed developmental stages (affect regulation, distress tolerance, emotional labelling) from scratch — each stage requiring the prior stage, the rate individual and non-benchmarkable. Her 9.5-year timeline maps precisely onto the logic of sequential stage-building. "You can't do the next stage until you've conquered this stage."

**Bisson/Davitt** confirms the developmental deficit from a clinical-diagnostic perspective. Davitt's description of being "an eight-year-old boy, even though I was a 50-year-old man" names arrested emotional development as the mechanism. The core wound is *absence* of developmental input (love, attention, attunement), not the presence of traumatic events. Skills that should have been learned in childhood must be rebuilt in therapy — a process that is developmental by definition.

**Heacock/Jacklin** provides the relational-trust dimension of the mechanism. Trust-building with a severely attachment-disrupted client (Sherlock) required approximately 3 years before he "stopped being so guarded." Erin's approach was "slow and deliberate... like you would with a wounded animal." The therapeutic relationship *is* the developmental environment — and trust-building at this pace cannot be compressed by technique.

### Counterpoint complications

**Fosha (AEDP)** challenges the developmental mechanism's requirement for extended incubation. Her concept of "transformance" — the innate psychic force towards growth — positions transformation as immediately accessible rather than gradually constructed. Meta-processing consolidates change within the session rather than across extended developmental time. A 6-session complete treatment is documented on DVD. However, beginning transformation is not the same as completing structural repair, and AEDP does not claim that complex developmental trauma resolves in 6 sessions. The complication is about *onset* (transformation can begin immediately) rather than *completion* (full developmental repair still takes years).

**Tilson** mounts the strongest challenge to the neurobiological rationale. She argues that the triune brain model (used by Ogden as a hierarchical processing metaphor) was "disproved in the 1970s," and that polyvagal theory (Porges, used by several sources to explain autonomic reorganisation) has been shown "untenable or highly implausible." If these frameworks are scientifically unfounded, the specific neurobiological explanation for *why* the developmental mechanism is slow weakens. However, the convergence holds at the clinical and experiential level: Spring, Heacock, Fisher, Bisson, and Razvi all observe slow developmental change independently of the specific neuroscience invoked. The mechanism's existence is more robustly supported than its neurobiological explanation. Tilson also endorses evidence-based symptom-focused treatments (TF-CBT, Prolonged Exposure) without engaging the distinction between symptom reduction and structural change — the very distinction that defines the developmental mechanism construct.
