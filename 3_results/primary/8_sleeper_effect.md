# Sleeper effect (emergent construct)

*Identified during Layer 2 annotation. Therapeutic change that continues, or first becomes visible, after treatment has ended.*

This pattern emerged independently across multiple sources and study designs. It challenges the standard practice of evaluating therapy at end-of-treatment and has direct implications for the duration question: if effects emerge after treatment, then "how long does therapy take?" is the wrong question — the real question is "how long does healing take, including the period after therapy ends?"

## Evidence by source

| Source ID | Finding | Timeframe |
|-----------|---------|-----------|
| fonagy-2015-tads | LTPP non-significant at 18-month end-of-treatment; p=0.001 at 42-month follow-up | Effects emerge 2 years post-treatment |
| zimmermann-2014-munich-dose | Post-treatment differences mediated by dose; follow-up differences mediated by psychoanalytic technique | Technique effects visible only at 1-3 year follow-up |
| shedler-2010-efficacy | Effect sizes increase from 0.97 (end of treatment) to 1.51 (>9 months follow-up) across multiple meta-analyses | Consistent pattern across studies |
| leichsenring-2013-emerging-evidence | "benefits of psychodynamic psychotherapy increase in long-term follow-up suggesting the treatment brings structural changes enabling delayed and continued gains" | Referenced across multiple meta-analyses |
| lindfors-2019-helsinki-10yr | PA showed less benefit during first years but more benefit after 5th year (post-treatment) | PA benefits emerge years 5-7, then converge at year 10 |

## Implications for the primary question

If the sleeper effect is real, then:
1. **Duration estimates based on end-of-treatment are underestimates** — the full arc of healing extends beyond the therapy itself
2. **The dose vs. elapsed time question is sharpened** — elapsed time after treatment may be as important as elapsed time during treatment
3. **Cost-effectiveness analyses with short time horizons systematically undervalue long-term therapy** (Koeser 2023 explicitly notes this)
4. **The argument for long-term therapy is strengthened but complicated** — the therapy may need to be long enough to set structural change in motion, but the change itself continues autonomously

## Questions for synthesis

- Is the sleeper effect specific to psychodynamic/psychoanalytic therapy, or does it occur with other modalities?
- Does the sleeper effect require a minimum dose to activate? (Howard's dose-effect model suggests a threshold)
- How does this interact with the therapy-vs-life confound? Post-treatment improvement could be therapy's delayed effect OR life experience enabled by therapeutic gains
- Should the synthesis reframe the thesis as: "therapy takes X years, but healing takes X+Y years"?
