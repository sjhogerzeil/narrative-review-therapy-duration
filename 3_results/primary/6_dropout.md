## Pattern of silence

The pattern of reporting is itself a finding:

- **Layer 1 (academic):** 2 of 8 annotated sources report dropout data. Theoretical texts (Kohut, Winnicott, Bromberg, Benjamin, Fonagy 2002, Schore, Porges, etc.) do not track participants because they are not studies. The clinical-empirical sources that could report dropout (Griffiths 2025, Freixo Ferreira 2023) do — but even in Griffiths' review, only 4 of 19 included studies reported dropout rates.

- **Layer 2 (naturalistic):** 3 of 10 sources address dropout meaningfully. Knekt 2021 is the only source designed specifically to study dropout predictors. The Helsinki study and TADS provide some attrition data. The remaining Layer 2 sources are silent on dropout.

- **Layer 3 (counterpoint):** 5 of 10 sources address dropout. The intensive format finding (Sciarrino 2020: 5.51% vs 18-39% standard) is the most striking — it suggests that the delivery format itself is a major dropout driver. The ISTDP finding (35.8% attended only one session) raises questions about whether this represents rapid improvement or failure to engage.

- **Layers 4-6 (practitioner, client, memoir):** Zero dropout data. These layers are silent by design — practitioner essays describe treated patients, forum threads capture people still engaged, and memoirs are written by survivors.

## Key findings for the survivorship bias discussion

1. **The treatment-engagement paradox** (Freixo Ferreira 2023): The interpersonal difficulties that define BPD (hostility, alliance problems) are the same factors that predict dropout. The patients who most need long-term treatment are the least likely to stay.

2. **Suitability predicts non-attendance in long-term therapy** (Knekt 2021): Poor reflective ability, poor self-concept, and rigid interaction predict dropout from LPP (RR=2.76). These characteristics overlap substantially with the complex developmental trauma population.

3. **Higher dissociation predicts dropout** (Griffiths 2025, citing Baekkelund 2022): The most severely dissociative patients — those for whom 3-5 year treatment is recommended — are the most likely to leave.

4. **Intensive formats dramatically reduce dropout** (Sciarrino 2020): 5.51% vs 18-39%. This suggests that standard weekly delivery may itself be a barrier, and that the "dose problem" includes not just how many sessions but how they are distributed.

5. **Exposure without relational foundation increases dropout** (Murphy 2019, citing Cloitre 2010): Supports the phase model argument that stabilisation must precede processing.

6. **The overall field does not track who leaves.** Most treatment outcome studies in the complex trauma literature do not report dropout rates, making it impossible to know how representative their results are.
