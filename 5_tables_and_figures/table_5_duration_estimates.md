# Table 5: Duration estimates by source and endpoint level

**Selection criteria:** Only sources reporting explicit quantitative duration data (calendar time, session count, or both) are included. Sources providing only theoretical arguments about duration without numbers (e.g., Kohut, Winnicott, Bromberg, Porges, Schore, BCPSG) are excluded. Where a source reports data at multiple endpoint levels, it is placed at the level corresponding to its primary outcome measure. "Estimated" session counts are marked with ~.

**Limitations:** (1) Calendar time and session counts are rarely reported together, making dose-vs-time comparisons difficult. (2) Populations vary widely across sources — the table organises by endpoint, not by diagnosis. (3) Memoir and forum durations reflect total healing arcs, not bounded treatment episodes. (4) Some endpoint classifications are judgment calls; the "Mixed/unclear" category captures genuine ambiguity.

## Reading guide

This table demonstrates the **endpoint-duration nexus** — the central finding of the review. Duration estimates do not randomly scatter; they cluster by endpoint level:

- **Level 1 (symptom reduction):** weeks to months, dominated by Layer 3 counterpoint sources using diagnostic measures (CAPS-5, ITQ, PCL)
- **Level 2 (functional improvement):** months to 1-2 years, with Links (2015) providing the clearest stepwise milestones
- **Level 3 (structural change):** years, with the Helsinki 10-year study (Lindfors 2019) and MBT 8-year follow-up (Bateman & Fonagy 2008) providing the strongest longitudinal evidence
- **Level 4 (growth/reconnection):** years to decades, dominated by memoirs and practitioner case reports
- **Level 5 (recovery as direction):** unbounded, dominated by client narratives that reject fixed endpoints

**The dose-vs-time confound:** Most sources report either calendar time OR session count, rarely both. Where both are available (Lindfors, Zimmermann, Murphy), they reveal that tripling the session count does not proportionally shorten calendar duration — elapsed life-time appears to contribute independently of session dose. The sleeper effect (Fonagy 2015, Shedler 2010, Bateman & Fonagy 2008) further complicates duration reporting: treatment effects that emerge years after therapy ends are invisible to endpoint-at-termination measures.

**What the table does NOT show:** Sources providing only mechanistic arguments for why healing is slow (e.g., Schore, Porges, Perry, Ogden, BCPSG) are excluded because they report no quantitative duration data. Their theoretical contributions are addressed in the discussion of neurobiological time constraints.
