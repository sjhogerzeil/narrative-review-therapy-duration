# Table 4: Developmental mechanism convergence

The review's strongest theoretical argument for why structural change takes years is the convergence across independent traditions. These traditions were developed by different researchers, use different concepts and methods, and emerge from different clinical and research contexts. Yet they converge on three features: (1) change happens through many repeated relational cycles, (2) what changes is internal structure, not symptoms, and (3) the process is inherently temporal. The probability that this convergence is coincidental is low. The table also notes which frameworks survive Tilson's (2025) evidence critique of polyvagal theory and the triune brain model.

| Tradition | Key theorist(s) | Mechanism described | Temporal implication | Survives evidence critique? |
|-----------|----------------|---------------------|---------------------|-----------------------------|
| Self psychology | Kohut (1984) | **Transmuting internalisation** — thousands of cycles of empathic attunement, optimal frustration, and repair gradually build owned psychic structure | Thousands of cycles; cannot be compressed | Yes — no neuroscientific claim; purely clinical observation |
| Object relations | Winnicott (1965) | **Regression to dependence and resumed maturation** — the patient regresses to the developmental level where things went wrong, then resumes maturational processes within the holding environment | Developmental time; by definition not compressible | Yes — developmental psychology, not neuroscience |
| Relational psychoanalysis | Bromberg (1998) | **Expanding capacity to "stand in the spaces"** — gradual development of the ability to hold multiple self-states in awareness simultaneously without dissociation | Gradual expansion; incremental | Yes — clinical phenomenology |
| Intersubjectivity theory | Benjamin (1988) | **Repeated recognition-destruction-survival cycles** — mutual recognition is repeatedly lost and re-found, each cycle building the capacity for intersubjectivity | Incremental accumulation | Yes — relational process theory |
| Mentalization theory | Fonagy (2002) | **Experiential learning of mentalizing capacity** — the ability to understand self and other in terms of mental states develops through attachment-based experiential learning | Attachment-paced; develops through relationship | Yes — empirically grounded (MBT outcome data) |
| Phase-oriented treatment (Janet tradition) | Steele (2001); Van der Hart (2006) | **Development of secure dependency** — the capacity to use the therapist as a secure base develops on a relational timeline; each phase requires the prior phase | Relational timeline; sequential and non-compressible | Yes — clinical observation + Janet (1897/98) |
| Affect regulation neuroscience | Schore (2000, 2021) | **Experience-dependent right-hemisphere maturation** — right-brain regulatory structures mature through repeated dyadic affect regulation experiences | Neural maturation pace; experience-dependent | Partially — empirical observations robust; specific right-brain lateralisation claims contested |
| Polyvagal theory | Porges (2022) | **Autonomic reorganisation** — the nervous system shifts from chronic defensive states (sympathetic/dorsal vagal) toward ventral vagal flexibility through repeated neuroception of safety | Slow, sequential reorganisation | **No** — polyvagal theory described as "untenable or highly implausible" (Grossman critique, via Tilson 2025). Clinical observation of slow autonomic change may hold; the specific vagal mechanism is contested |
| Neurosequential model | Perry & Hambrick (2019) | **Bottom-up sequential brain reorganisation** — lower brain regions must be regulated before higher regions can develop; each level requires "patterned, repetitive" experiences | Sequential; each level requires its own duration | Partially — triune brain model debunked; Perry acknowledges this and retains hierarchical processing as clinical framework, not anatomical claim |
| Implicit process research | BCPSG (2010) | **Accumulation of "moments of meeting"** — implicit relational knowing shifts through many small, non-interpretive relational events that cannot be planned or rushed | "Many small shifts"; inherently cumulative | Yes — empirical (microprocess research) |
| Sensorimotor processing | Ogden (2006) | **Hierarchical processing with "bite-sizing"** — sensorimotor memories are closer to "read-only memory" than cognitive "random-access memory"; new responses must be built alongside old ones rather than replacing them | Slower than cognitive change; nervous system integrates only small pieces at a time | Partially — hierarchy used as clinical metaphor, not anatomical claim; Fisher's "bite-sizing" is clinical observation |

## Reading guide

Read the "Survives evidence critique?" column for intellectual honesty: the clinical convergence (left columns) is robust across all 11 traditions, but the neuroscientific explanation (Porges, Perry) is contested. The review's claim that "structural change takes years" rests on the clinical convergence and outcome data, not on polyvagal theory or the triune brain model. The three traditions most vulnerable to the evidence critique (rows 8–9) can be removed entirely without weakening the convergence: eight remaining traditions still independently predict that structural change requires repeated relational cycles over developmental time.
