# The definition of healing

The question "how long does healing take?" assumes that "healing" is a settled concept with a shared meaning across the field. It is not. Different clinical traditions, different research methodologies, and different lived experiences define the endpoint of healing differently — and these different definitions produce duration estimates that differ by orders of magnitude. This may not be a side issue. It may be the central issue.

## Working definition

*The capacity to live with agency, relational openness, and tolerable selfhood, rather than organised around survival.*

This definition is offered as a starting point, not a conclusion. The review treats "what counts as healed?" as a finding to be derived from the evidence, not as a premise to be stipulated in advance.

## Five levels of change

The literature, practitioner accounts, client narratives, and memoirs do not describe a single endpoint called "healing." They describe at least five distinguishable levels of change, each with a different relationship to duration:

- **Symptom reduction** — fewer flashbacks, lower scores on standardised instruments, loss of diagnosis. The primary outcome in most RCTs. Measurable in weeks to months.
- **Functional improvement** — holding employment, maintaining relationships, reduced crisis episodes. The typical endpoint for insurance and service delivery systems. Measurable in months to years.
- **Structural change** — personality reorganisation, mentalizing capacity, integration of dissociated parts, earned attachment security. The person is not merely managing symptoms but has become someone who processes experience differently. Measurable in years — if measurable at all.
- **Growth and reconnection** — post-traumatic growth, identity transformation, the capacity for intimacy and agency. Described in memoirs and long-term client accounts as emerging after years or decades.
- **Recovery as direction** — not a destination to arrive at but an orientation to maintain. Some accounts describe recovery as a way of living rather than a state to achieve, which makes the question "how long does it take?" structurally unanswerable in its usual form.

These levels may be nested rather than parallel — each subsuming the previous ones. A patient who has achieved structural change will necessarily also show symptom reduction, but the reverse does not hold. If this nesting is real, then duration estimates at different levels are not competing answers to the same question but correct answers to different questions.

## Why this may matter more than it appears

If the field's disagreement about duration is actually a disagreement about the definition of the endpoint, then the confusion is not empirical but conceptual. The data are not contradictory — they are incommensurable, because they measure different things. A review that merely asks "how long does it take?" without specifying the endpoint level would reproduce the confusion rather than resolving it. This review takes the definition of healing not as a preliminary to be settled before the analysis begins, but as a dimension of the analysis itself.
