# Clinical population

This review concerns people whose trauma is not a single event but a developmental environment — chronic, relational, often beginning before language, and personality-organising. The injury is not a symptom overlay on an otherwise intact personality. It is a structural adaptation: the personality itself, its relational patterns, defensive organisation, and sense of self, has been shaped by the need to survive conditions that were not survivable in any ordinary sense. The result is not a disorder added to a person but a person organised around survival.

The clinical presentations that fall within this population include ICD-11 complex PTSD (which adds disturbances in self-organisation — emotional dysregulation, negative self-concept, and disturbed relationships — to the core PTSD symptoms), DSM personality disorders (understood here as traumatic adaptations rather than primary pathology), and dissociative disorders across the structural dissociation spectrum. The proposed but unadopted diagnosis of developmental trauma disorder captures the population most precisely, but it does not appear in current diagnostic systems.

## The structural depth continuum

A critical feature of this population — and one that bears directly on the duration question — is that the same diagnosis can mask fundamentally different degrees of structural disruption. The structural dissociation model provides the most precise vocabulary for this continuum:

- **Primary structural dissociation** — one emotional part alongside a functioning apparently normal part. The person may present with clear PTSD symptoms but retain substantial adaptive capacity, relational skills, and self-coherence.
- **Secondary structural dissociation** — multiple emotional parts, each carrying different traumatic material. The personality is more fragmented; functional impairment is broader; the capacity for self-regulation is more compromised.
- **Tertiary structural dissociation** — multiple apparently normal parts as well as multiple emotional parts (dissociative identity disorder). The personality system is fundamentally divided; the person may function well in some contexts while being entirely unavailable in others.

A patient with primary structural dissociation and preserved adaptive capacity is a different clinical entity from a patient with tertiary structural dissociation and pervasive developmental deficits — even when both carry a CPTSD diagnosis. This heterogeneity within the diagnosis has direct consequences for how long treatment takes, and for whether brief treatment claims and long-treatment claims are actually describing the same population.
