# Background

An eight-day residential programme produces CPTSD remission in 88% of participants. A dose-response analysis shows personality disorder recovery requiring 216 sessions over 2.2 years. A Holocaust survivor describes healing still in progress at ninety. A Reddit poster reports 10 years and 900 appointments. A Cochrane review concludes that short-term psychodynamic therapy is effective in under 40 sessions. A phase-oriented treatment model projects 3 to 5 years for dissociative disorders, with most participants still symptomatic at study endpoints.

These numbers span a range of roughly 1,000 to 1. They are not from fringe sources — they come from peer-reviewed trials, meta-analyses, and clinician-researchers with decades of experience. The field appears to disagree fundamentally about whether healing from complex developmental trauma is an acute intervention or a life's work.

This disagreement has consequences. Insurance systems design session limits around the briefer estimates. Training programmes teach manualised protocols calibrated to the shorter timelines. Patients who do not recover within the expected window are told — implicitly or explicitly — that they have failed the treatment, rather than that the treatment has been insufficiently specified for their injury. Clinicians who work with this population over years know from experience that deep change takes time, but the formal evidence base is dominated by studies that measure outcomes at 12 weeks.

The gap between what clinicians observe and what the literature formally supports is not a minor inconvenience. It shapes which treatments are funded, which patients receive care, and how long that care is allowed to continue. In the Netherlands, where the author practises, specialised mental health care has been progressively reorganised around manualised, time-limited protocols. The kind of sustained relational therapy that this review examines is increasingly available only in private practice — which means it is available only to those who can pay.

The question "how long does therapy take?" sounds straightforward. But the literature's inability to converge on an answer suggests that something more fundamental is at stake. The disagreement may not be about the data. It may be about what the question means — and specifically, about what counts as "healed." If different parts of the field are measuring different things and calling them all "recovery," then the contradiction in the numbers is not a scientific failure but a definitional one.

This review asks whether the field's apparent disagreement about therapy duration can be resolved — not by choosing a side, but by examining whether both sides are answering the same question.
